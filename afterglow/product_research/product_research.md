Turns your trip's photos and notes into a beautiful, automatically organized travel journal you'll actually finish.

People who take many trip photos but never organize them into anything meaningful afterward.

On-device clustering of Photos-framework metadata into a day-by-day trip story the user captions, producing a polished exportable journal entirely offline, with no cloud photo processing.

## Why Users Would Pay

Premium layout themes, unlimited archived trips, printable photo-book export, richer on-device narrative generation from captions/voice notes.

## Subscription Model

Free: 1 trip journal, basic themes. Paid: unlimited trips, premium themes, print export, narrative generation.

## Local-First MVP Scope

Entirely on-device — reads Photos metadata with permission, clusters by day/location, exports as PDF/shareable images.

## Future Backend Roadmap

Optional cloud backup of finished journals; potential shared collaborative journal for co-travelers who also have the app.

## AR / Local ML / Local LLM Fit

On-device Vision for clustering and highlight-photo selection; optional on-device LLM to draft short narrative captions strictly from the user's own notes.

## Design Direction

Premium photo-book aesthetic — full-bleed imagery, elegant serif captions, a designed keepsake rather than a generic gallery.

## App Store Positioning

The travel journal that makes itself — from your own photos, entirely on your phone.

## Invalidation Criteria

If most users abandon the caption step after auto-clustering, ship a faster no-caption default output instead of assuming the concept fails.

## v1 Build Scope

Photo selection, auto day-clustering, captions/voice notes, one layout theme, export.

- Select a trip's date range/photos
- Review auto-generated day clusters
- Add captions or voice notes
- Choose a layout theme
- Export/share the finished journal

- Photos framework + on-device Vision for clustering

Before anyone gets excited about clustering algorithms and Vision pipelines, I want to name the actual competitor here, because it changes what "MVP" needs to prove: Apple's own Photos app already does day/location clustering and auto-generates Memories slideshows for free, with zero setup, already installed on every target user's phone. Everything in the first build should be judged against "does this make the exported PDF better than an Apple Memories screen-recording," because that's the honest comparison a skeptical user will make in about four seconds.

On audience and use context: this is not a daily-use app. Someone downloads it after they get home from a trip, probably within a week while the memory's still fresh, and does the whole flow in one or two sitting sessions — likely on a couch, likely with imperfect attention (kids, TV on, half paying attention while scrolling their camera roll). That has real design consequences: the flow needs to survive interruption (app backgrounded mid-caption, phone call during voice note recording, force-quit before export) with state actually persisted, not lost. It also means the widget matters more than it looks like on paper — it's not a nice-to-have decoration, it's the only mechanism pulling a user back into an app they have no daily reason to open, so if it's dropped or half-built the retention story collapses for anyone not actively mid-trip.

Comparable patterns worth learning from, with assumptions flagged since I haven't run these apps myself in this session: Apple Photos Memories and Google Photos highlight reels (the free, ambient, no-effort competitor); Chatbooks and Mixbook/Artifact Uprising (paid physical photo-book pipelines — validate people already pay real money for "my photos, professionally assembled," which supports the monetization thesis, but also sets a print-quality expectation Afterglow's v1 explicitly isn't attempting); Day One (journaling app with a captioning/voice-memo pattern worth mimicking for the caption editor's tone — personal, low-friction, not form-like); 1 Second Everyday (proof that "small daily/trip capture assembled into one artifact" has a real paying audience). The pattern across all of them: the paid tier is never "more AI," it's "more polish and physical output" — which should make everyone nervous about over-investing engineering time in the on-device LLM narrative feature relative to just making the export look genuinely beautiful.

Platform opportunities, concretely: PHPickerViewController and PHAsset creationDate/location for clustering, PHCachingImageManager with downsampled targetSize thumbnails (non-negotiable for the thousand-photo case — full-res decode on a grid view is how this app becomes a case study in the OOM pitfall doc), Vision for saliency/highlight-photo selection but scoped tightly since this is the single biggest place scope can quietly balloon, AVAudioRecorder + on-device Speech framework for voice notes (transcribing audio to text is fine and doesn't violate the "never invent details" rule since it's literal transcription, not generation — that's a distinction worth locking in now), PDFKit for the actual export artifact, WidgetKit for the cover-photo widget, and ShareLink/UIActivityViewController for the share sheet.

My biggest risk flag, as the person whose job is to find where this quietly fails: Vision-based highlight/best-photo selection is exactly the kind of feature that eats three weeks for a result users can't tell apart from "pick the sharpest, best-exposed photo of the day" using CIImage-level heuristics. I'd bias the first build hard toward the cheap heuristic and treat true Vision saliency scoring as a post-MVP polish item, not a v1 blocker — burning the build budget there is the most likely way this ships with a half-built export screen instead. Second risk: the free tier is "1 trip journal" — that's an extremely thin free experience for evaluating whether people will even do the caption step (which the spec's own invalidation criteria already worries about), so the first build needs actual instrumentation-free but state-visible tracking of whether users bail at the caption screen, even if it's just a locally logged event count, so a later phase can act on the invalidation criteria instead of guessing. Third risk: multi-day trips with genuinely no location data (flights, indoor museums, screenshots mixed into the camera roll) need to produce a journal that still looks intentional day-by-day, not degrade to an undifferentiated photo dump — that's a testable state, not a vague concern, and the first build should include a synthetic no-GPS trip as a design/test case from day one rather than discovering it late.

Bottom line for what the first build should actually contain: prioritize timestamp-based day clustering (cheap, robust, works everywhere) with location as pure enrichment; a fast, low-friction caption/voice-note editor; one genuinely well-designed export theme rather than a rough multi-theme picker; the cover-photo widget, because it's the only re-engagement lever this app has; and explicit, deliberate restraint on Vision/LLM sophistication until the core "select photos → get a keepsake" loop is proven to not embarrass itself next to a free Apple Memories slideshow.

**Implications for the first build**
