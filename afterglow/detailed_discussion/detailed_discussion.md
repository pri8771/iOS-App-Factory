**Claude (QA & Risk · Skeptic) — Round 1**

Let me stress-test this before we lock anything in — there are real gaps prior phases have glossed over, and a few claims that don't actually survive contact with real camera rolls.

Start with the thing nobody's said out loud yet: "zero network calls in the core loop" is not actually true as stated, and I want that named precisely rather than left to quietly break during build. Most iOS photo libraries are iCloud-backed with "Optimize Storage" on, meaning full-resolution originals for a chunk of a user's trip photos aren't on the device — PHImageManager has to set `isNetworkAccessAllowed` and pull them down from iCloud on demand, or the export renders soft, low-res proxies without warning. That's not our cloud, it's Apple's own sync, but it is a network call, and if we don't decide now how to handle it, someone in the build phase will either silently allow network access (breaking the "airplane mode" claim we already made) or silently skip it (producing blurry export photos with no explanation). My assumption, stated plainly so it doesn't get relitigated: the app is local-first meaning *no calls to any Afterglow-controlled or third-party backend*, but it is allowed to request iCloud-original downloads through PHImageManager, with a visible "downloading originals" progress state and a offline-friendly fallback to the best-available local resolution if the network truly isn't there. That needs a real UI state, not a spinner that hangs forever with no explanation on a plane.

Second, the trip auto-detection heuristic ("gap in photos" implies a trip) is underspecified and I don't trust it to work for the actual target user. Someone who takes photos constantly in daily life has no clean "gap," and someone who barely uses their camera day-to-day might have a gap that has nothing to do with a trip (just a boring week). I'd rather we commit now to date-range selection being manual-first with auto-suggestion as an assist, not a required detection step — if auto-detection is wrong and there's no fast manual override sitting right next to it, the very first screen creates a bad impression before the user has done anything.

Edge cases that need explicit answers, not vague deferrals:

- A trip photo library often has screenshots, memes forwarded in a group chat, and photos of a whiteboard mixed into the date range. Does the day cluster include everything with a timestamp in range, or is there a lightweight filter (e.g., exclude screenshots via `PHAsset.mediaSubtypes` or asset source type)? If we include everything, day 3 of someone's Portugal trip includes a screenshot of a boarding pass and a meme — that's a real "does this feel premium or does this feel like a dump" moment, and I think the answer has to be: screenshots and non-camera-source assets are excluded from clusters by default, with an "add more photos to this day" affordance for the user who actually wants that meme in there. Silent inclusion undermines the entire "designed keepsake" positioning this app is betting on.
- Burst-shot duplicates: ten near-identical photos of the same sunset will make a day cluster's strip look repetitive and will make cover-photo heuristics unstable (which of ten near-identical frames wins?). Needs at least a naive perceptual-similarity or burst-ID collapse so the strip doesn't read as "same photo ten times," or the day-cluster experience feels broken on exactly the trips with the best photos.
- Videos and Live Photos in the library: does the PDF export handle these at all, or do they get silently dropped, or does it crash trying to treat a video asset like a still? This needs an explicit decision — I'd say v1 exports a still frame/poster image for videos and Live Photos into the layout, named as a limitation, rather than silently excluding a chunk of what might be someone's best trip content.
- Trip spanning a DST change or a date line crossing (the classic "flew from Tokyo to LA and arrived before you left" scenario) — this isn't a hypothetical, it's exactly the multi-timezone case the whole reassign-cluster feature exists to catch, so it needs a specific test fixture, not just "we'll rely on the manual reassign button to bail us out."
- Permission revoked mid-session: user backgrounds the app mid-review, goes to Settings, flips Photos access off, comes back. The app needs to detect that transition (via `PHPhotoLibraryChangeObserver` or re-checking authorization status on foreground) and degrade gracefully — showing what was already cached/persisted rather than crashing on a now-invalid PHAsset fetch mid-render.
- Voice note recording interrupted by an actual phone call or Siri — `AVAudioSession` interruption handling needs a real answer: does the partial recording save, discard, or corrupt? This is squarely inside the "interruption resilience" principle this whole project has been holding itself to, so I don't think it's optional polish.
- Zero-photo library or a date range with literally one photo: does the single-cluster, single-photo case render as a legitimate (if minimal) journal page, or does the layout break because it was designed assuming 5-15 photos per day? This needs to be an explicit fixture, not discovered when someone tests with a weekend day-trip.
- Export file size: full-bleed, near-original-resolution images across a 10-day, 400-photo journal could produce a PDF in the hundreds of MB, which chokes Mail attachment limits and makes AirDrop slow. There needs to be a defined export resolution ceiling per image (this is also a memory-pressure issue during PDF generation, not just a file-size issue) — downsampling for export, not just for thumbnails, needs to be stated as a requirement here, not assumed to fall out of "downsampled thumbnails" which prior phases only discussed for the review-grid case.
- Uninstall/reinstall: the local SQLite/SwiftData store has no backup path in v1 (no iCloud, no export/import of the app's own state, only export of the finished PDF). If the user deletes the app, every draft-in-progress and every past journal's editable state is gone forever, even though the exported PDFs (if saved elsewhere) survive. That's a real, sharp limitation worth stating explicitly now rather than let someone discover it the hard way — I'd flag it as a named v1 limitation, not a bug, but it needs a line in the app somewhere (or at minimum in the review) rather than being invisible.

On privacy specifically, one thing I want checked rather than assumed: when photos with embedded GPS EXIF get rendered into the exported PDF, is that GPS data still recoverable from the exported file, or does rasterizing into a PDF page strip it (it generally does, since PDFKit draws bitmaps into page content rather than embedding original image files with metadata intact) — but "generally does" isn't good enough for a privacy claim we might make in App Store copy. This should be verified against the actual rendering pipeline during build, not assumed, especially because the whole growth mechanism for this app is people forwarding this PDF to family — a leaked home GPS location in a journal about someone's own house would be a real problem, and I'd rather build have to explicitly confirm "no embedded location metadata in the export file" is true and testable, than have it be an unstated assumption.

Risk register, concretely, beyond what's already logged: (1) iCloud-network-dependent originals contradicts an unqualified "fully offline" claim — mitigate by scoping the claim precisely as above and building a visible download-state; (2) screenshot/non-photo content polluting clusters undermines the premium promise — mitigate with a default filter plus manual override; (3) burst duplicates make cover-photo selection and cluster strips look broken — mitigate with basic similarity collapse even before Vision saliency exists; (4) uninstall data loss is real and currently silent — named limitation, not fixed for v1, but must be named; (5) export file size/memory pressure on large trips is a crash risk, not just a UX nicety — needs an explicit downsampling ceiling for export-resolution images, separate from the review-thumbnail downsampling already discussed.

Final assumptions I'd lock for this phase so design/architecture doesn't have to re-derive them: date-range selection is manual-first with auto-suggestion as an assist, not a blocking auto-detect step; screenshots and non-camera-roll-source assets are excluded from clusters by default with a manual add-back option; videos/Live Photos get a poster-frame treatment in export, named as a limitation on motion content; export images are downsampled to a defined resolution ceiling independent from review thumbnails; iCloud-original network fetches are allowed and visibly stated as such, distinguishing "no third-party network calls" from "fully airplane-mode capable in all cases"; and app uninstall/reinstall data loss for in-progress and past journals is an accepted, explicitly named v1 limitation rather than a silent gap.
