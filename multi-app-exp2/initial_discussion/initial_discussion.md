If either of those two moments has friction, the product fails regardless of how good the dashboard looks the rest of the week.

Core loop: capture a person and a note quickly → the app anchors that person's "clock" at the moment of creation or last interaction (never at zero interactions, more on why below) → each person has a per-person expected contact cadence, not a single global threshold, and a deterministic score expresses "time elapsed since last touch" relative to "expected cadence for this relationship" → the weekly ritual surfaces a capped, ranked queue of people crossing that threshold, with three exits per person: log a real outreach, snooze to a specific future date, or archive (stop tracking) → completing the ritual is a real, designed end-state, not just an empty list. That closes the loop and re-primes the next week.

One ambiguity I want resolved right now rather than left for the build phase to improvise: "going cold" cannot be one clock for everyone. A close collaborator you talk to weekly and a former mentor you catch up with once a year both look "healthy" on wildly different cadences. So person creation should ask for a lightweight importance tier — something like Close / Regular / Occasional — which sets an expected cadence (e.g. ~2 weeks / ~6 weeks / ~6 months), and the score is elapsed-time divided by expected-cadence. That's still fully deterministic and testable (fix a person, fix a clock, assert the band), but it avoids the single dumbest failure mode of this category: flagging someone as neglected on a schedule they never agreed to.

A second ambiguity worth nailing down: what anchors day zero for a brand-new person with no logged interactions yet? The detail the final phase output has to preserve is exactly this: cadence is per-person and tiered, day-zero is anchored at creation not last-interaction, the ritual queue is capped with an honest overflow message, and the widget/extension architecture requires an App Group from the start — these are concrete enough to check against later, unlike "feels premium," which isn't a criterion anyone can verify.
