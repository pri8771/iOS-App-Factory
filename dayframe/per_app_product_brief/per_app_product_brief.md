That regret is the whole subscription pitch: "keep every month, not just this one." That's a value proposition that survives someone asking "wait, what am I actually paying for" — the answer isn't cosmetic, it's "your good months don't get taken away and you get to fix the AI when it picks the trash can instead of your kid's face."

Core loop as I'd build it: grant Photos permission (limited-library mode has to work, not just full access — a lot of privacy-conscious parents will pick "select photos" and if that silently produces an empty app, we've lost them at step one) → background scan tags qualifying days → she opens the app to a calendar/timeline view where days with a pick show a thumbnail and empty days are just blank, not apologized for → she can tap any day to watch that day's pick standalone, immediately, without waiting for a month to complete → at the point a month has enough picks, a "your July reel is ready" moment surfaces, she watches it end to end in one sitting, and shares or exports. The daily-pick-is-watchable-immediately part isn't in the earlier phase notes explicitly but I think it needs to be load-bearing: waiting 30 days for the first payoff is a terrible activation curve. Give her value on day one, not day thirty.

Competitive wedge: the obvious competitors are Apple's own Memories/Photos "For You" reels, Google Photos highlight videos, and a swarm of "AI video editor" apps on the App Store that require you to select clips yourself. Apple and Google's auto-reels are cross-media (mix photos and video, mix months, algorithmically opaque, and change without your input) and are bundled utilities, not a product you'd pay for — they're proof-of-concept for the category, not a wedge to beat head-on with more features. Our wedge is narrowness and legibility: Dayframe only does one thing — daily video, one pick a day, one file a month — and because it's narrow, the user can actually form a mental model of what it does and correct it when it's wrong (the swap feature). Apple's Memories is a black box you can't argue with. That's a real, defensible difference, not a marketing angle, because it directly serves the "never feels broken" requirement from the last phase — a system you can correct feels intentional even when it errs; a system you can't correct just feels wrong.

Growth angle: this is niche, not viral in the broad-sharing sense, and I want to resist inflating that. The natural sharing moment is the monthly reel itself — a 60-90 second video of "this is our July" is genuinely the kind of thing that gets sent to a partner, a grandparent, a group chat, and each one of those is a soft ad for the app because it's watchable and clearly not a generic template. I'd deliberately keep a small, tasteful export watermark or end-card ("made with Dayframe") on the free tier's shared reels specifically because those get forwarded outside the App Store funnel — that's the free tier's actual job, distribution, not conversion. Paid removes it. This is a slower, word-of-mouth-through-family-group-chats mechanism, not a broad viral loop, and I think that's honest and fine for this category — parents don't go viral, but they do forward things to six people who then wonder how it was made.

Local-first: everything through scoring, picking, and rendering the reel happens on-device with Vision and AVFoundation, no account required to use the app at all in v1. The data model needs a stable identifier per day-pick record (day, chosen asset reference, score, swap history) that's keyed by something more durable than a local file path — PHAsset local identifiers are already semi-stable, but the record should carry a content hash or asset identifier plus enough metadata to re-resolve or flag "this pick's source video was deleted" gracefully, because users delete raw footage after a reel is made and that shouldn't corrupt history. Cloud-ready means: when we do add sync, the same record gets a remote URL field and a sync-state enum that's unused today — I don't want to build any sync logic now, just make sure nobody hardcodes "this record only ever has a local file URL" anywhere in the picking or rendering code path.

Retention driver — the thing that makes her open this app in month four, not just month one — is the reel history becoming a real archive she doesn't have anywhere else in this watchable, curated form. Photos has the raw footage; Dayframe has the edited memory. The risk to name honestly: if the auto-pick quality is mediocre for two months running (bad Vision scoring on dim indoor lighting, which is extremely common for home video), she churns before the archive value ever kicks in, so the swap-override and the "watch today's pick immediately" loop aren't nice-to-haves, they're what keeps her using it long enough for the archive to become sticky. If I had to name the single biggest risk to this whole brief, it's that Vision's frame-level quality signals (sharpness, face detection, exposure) are a weak proxy for "moment worth remembering" — a blurry clip of a first step matters more than a sharp clip of nothing happening — and no amount of design polish fixes a model that consistently picks the wrong thing. That's a build-phase risk, not a product-brief risk, but the brief should say clearly: the manual swap isn't a paywall gimmick bolted onto a good model, it's compensating for a model that will get it wrong often, and design has to make swapping feel like normal light editing, not like admitting the AI failed.
