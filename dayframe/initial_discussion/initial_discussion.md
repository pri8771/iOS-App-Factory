Here's my independent read on this, coming at it from the "what actually breaks and what's the smallest thing that ships" angle.

The product promise I'd commit to: Dayframe watches the video clips you already shoot day to day, uses on-device Vision to quietly pick the single best moment from each day, and once a month hands you a finished reel you never had to open an editor for. The emphasis has to be on "never had to edit" — that's the whole promise. If the MVP asks the user to trim, reorder, or pick music before it'll render anything, we've built an editor with extra steps, not Dayframe.

Primary user: someone who shoots a lot of casual video — kids, pets, a dog on a walk, a trip — and has hundreds of unwatched clips sitting in Photos that they will never manually review. Not a content creator, not someone optimizing for social virality on each clip. The job-to-be-done is closer to "give me a reason to look back at this month" than "help me make a good video." That framing matters because it caps how much manual control we owe the user in v1 — control undermines the promise as much as it serves it.

Core loop, stated plainly: app gets Photos permission → on each day that has qualifying video, Vision scores candidate clips (sharpness, face presence, motion stability, some saliency signal) and picks a best moment → at month's end (or on demand) those daily picks get stitched into one reel with a consistent, simple transition style → user watches it, and can export/share. That's the whole loop. Anything beyond that — manual re-ordering, music library picking, filters — is v2 territory dressed up as v1 scope creep.

The single biggest open decision I'd force now rather than let architecture quietly answer it later: does "best moment" mean picking the best whole clip among that day's videos, or trimming down to a highlighted sub-segment inside a longer clip? Those are very different engineering problems — one is clip-level comparison, the other is sliding-window temporal scoring inside a single asset. My pragmatist answer: do clip-level selection first (score sampled frames per candidate clip, pick the best clip), then apply a hard duration cap by taking the highest-scoring contiguous window within that winning clip if it runs long. That gets us an actual "moment" feel in the reel without needing a full temporal-saliency model across every clip a user shot that day. If we don't decide this now, whoever builds it will guess, and the guess will either blow the timeline or under-deliver on the promise.

Hard scope boundaries I'd draw and defend: video only, no substituting photos on a day with zero video — a day with no video is just absent from the reel, shown honestly as "no moment this day" rather than papered over. No cloud upload or sync in this build, even though the data model should leave a slot for a remote asset reference later. No manual timeline editing, no custom music import from Apple Music/user library — if we want music, it's a small bundled royalty-free set the user picks from, or no music at all with just clip audio, and I'd rather ship silent/clip-audio-only first than deal with sync-license complexity before there's a validated user. No cross-device anything. No multi-person/shared reels. And a processing bound: we cap how much footage per day actually gets analyzed (e.g., skip or sample-only past some number of candidate clips or total duration) so a power user with 40 clips in one day doesn't turn "open the app" into a thermal-throttling multi-minute stall.

The failure mode I'm most worried about, and the one I want named in the record rather than discovered during build: Vision-based "quality" scoring on real amateur footage is going to produce a lot of near-ties and outright wrong picks — dark rooms, handheld shake, a great moment that's slightly blurry versus a boring moment that's technically sharp. If the MVP has zero user recourse when the auto-pick is bad, the app will feel broken even when it's working exactly as designed. I'd rather bake a minimal override — swap today's pick for the next-best candidate — into the definition of "done" for this MVP, not as a nice-to-have, because otherwise our own production-readiness bar (named in the contract) about ambiguous scoring has no user-facing answer. That override, gated behind the paywall as "unlimited re-picks" or similar, also happens to be a real monetization lever instead of a cosmetic one — free tier gets the auto-pick with maybe one swap per reel, paid tier gets unlimited swaps plus full history and un-watermarked export.

Measurable success criteria I'd hold this to: given a test library spanning a full month with a realistic mix (some days with 1 clip, some with several, some with none, at least one corrupted or unsupported file), the app produces a playable, exportable reel without crashing or hanging; days with no qualifying video are visibly and correctly represented as absent rather than silently dropped in a way that looks like a bug; the whole per-day analysis pipeline degrades a single bad clip (unsupported codec, HDR oddity) by excluding it, not by failing the whole day or month; and the paywall gate is testable in both directions — free tier is usably limited but not broken, subscription unlocks something a QA pass can actually verify changed.

The thing I don't want lost by the time this reaches build: "best moment" is not one operation, it's two — pick the clip, then bound its duration — and both steps need explicit failure handling, because Vision returning nothing useful is not an edge case here, it's a Tuesday.
