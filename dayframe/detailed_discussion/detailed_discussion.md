**Claude (QA & Risk · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

Picking up where the last two phases left off, there's a cluster of decisions that got skipped because they only surface once you imagine the pipeline actually running against a real, messy Photos library for a real month, and I think several of them are load-bearing enough that design and architecture shouldn't be left to invent them independently.

The one I'd force first is day-boundary definition. "Each day gets a pick" sounds trivial until you ask: day in which timezone — the device's timezone at capture time, or at analysis time, or UTC? A parent flying cross-country mid-month, or just living somewhere that observes DST, will have videos whose local capture day depends on which clock you trust. My answer: use the asset's creationDate combined with the timezone metadata AVFoundation/PHAsset exposes for that specific asset (not the device's current timezone), so a video shot at 11:40pm local time on the 3rd stays on the 3rd even if the phone's clock has since moved. If we get this wrong, whole days will silently shift or double up during any month with travel, which is exactly the kind of bug that looks fine in the demo and wrong in real use.

Second, and this one actually contradicts an implicit assumption sitting under the whole "best moment" scoring approach: the target persona is explicitly a parent shooting kids *and pets*, but Vision's face-presence signal will systematically undervalue any day whose only good footage is a dog running around with no human face in frame. If face-presence is weighted the way generic "quality" scoring usually is, pet-only or landscape-only days will get shafted for no product reason — the day isn't lower quality, the scoring proxy is just blind to what makes it good. I want this named explicitly as a resolved requirement: scoring must not require face presence to produce a high score, only reward it when present, with saliency/motion/subject-in-frame signals carrying real weight on their own. Otherwise we'll ship an app that quietly does worse on exactly the households (pet owners, one parent solo with the kid off-camera) that are supposedly in scope.

Third — and I think this is the biggest silent risk in the whole pipeline — is the iCloud-optimized-storage case. Most iPhones with any meaningful video history don't have full-resolution originals on disk; they're thumbnail-only locally with the real asset in iCloud. Vision needs actual pixel data, which means every "analyze this day's clips" pass may need to trigger a network fetch per asset, which can be slow, can fail, can get interrupted by the user going offline, and can silently balloon local storage if we cache full 4K originals for scoring and don't clean them up. This isn't an edge case, it's close to the default case for anyone with a phone that isn't nearly full. I'd resolve it explicitly: analysis requests iCloud download with a visible "waiting for iCloud" state per day (not a silent hang), a timeout after which that day is marked as deferred rather than failed, a retry when the network's available again, and any temporary full-res download used purely for scoring gets deleted immediately after — we should never accumulate a shadow copy of the user's whole video library on-disk just because we scored it once.

Fourth, reprocessing and mutability. The earlier phases decided a day's pick is watchable immediately and a month's reel renders once enough days have picks — but nobody's said what happens when the underlying data changes after that. Concretely: user deletes the source video for a day after that day's pick was already shown or already baked into a rendered monthly reel. I'd resolve this as: the rendered monthly reel file is a self-contained export (baked frames, not a live reference), so it survives source deletion untouched — that's actually a feature, it's the archive. But the *day view* and the *swap* affordance need graceful handling when the PHAsset resolves to nothing: show the day as "no longer available" rather than crashing or showing a blank player, and disable swap on days where the candidate pool has shrunk to nothing. Separately: does editing a video in Photos after the day was scored trigger a rescore? I'd explicitly exclude this from MVP — treat the asset as scored-once, no listening for downstream edits — and say so as a named limitation rather than silently getting it wrong.

Fifth, render caching and idempotency. If the monthly reel is regenerated every time the user opens the month view, that's an expensive AVFoundation export running repeatedly for no reason, and it also means two consecutive views of "the same" reel could produce different files if anything about scoring is non-deterministic (frame sampling with any randomness, floating point score ties resolved differently across runs). I'd resolve this as: render once, cache the output file keyed to a hash of that month's ordered list of (day, chosen asset identifier, trim window), and only re-render when that key changes — i.e., after an explicit swap. This also gives us a concrete answer for what "swap" actually invalidates: just that day's slot, not a full rescan.

Sixth, the two things every asset-processing pipeline eventually gets bitten by: partial/interrupted state and orphaned temp files. Backgrounding mid-scan or mid-export needs a checkpoint model where per-day analysis and the final export are individually resumable/idempotent, not restarted from zero — otherwise a user who backgrounds the app on a slow phone will watch it seemingly work forever, redoing the same days each time it's foregrounded. And any temp files from an interrupted AVAssetExportSession (monthly reel export, or the iCloud-download-for-scoring temp copies above) need cleanup on next launch, or storage quietly fills up over months of use — a real complaint vector for an app whose whole premise is "hands-off."

Seventh, on the paywall/data-retention interaction that the product brief specified: free tier loses access to older months. I want the exact mechanic pinned down because there are two very different implementations hiding behind "loses access" — (a) we actually delete the older rendered reel files, which is real, unrecoverable data loss the moment someone's subscription lapses, or (b) we keep the files on disk but gate the UI so a re-subscribe instantly restores everything. I'd resolve hard in favor of (b): never delete a user's already-rendered reel for a monetization reason. Silently destroying someone's kid's monthly video because a card expired is the kind of thing that generates one-star reviews and possibly support/legal blowback; gating access while preserving the underlying file is both kinder and cheaper to implement (no re-render on resubscribe needed).

On privacy specifically: since the whole pitch to a parent is "your kid's videos never leave your phone," this needs to be true in a way that's checkable — no analytics event should carry frame data, thumbnails, or even derived scores tied to identifiable content; crash reports need to be scrubbed of anything image-derived. This is worth stating as a resolved requirement now because it constrains any future logging/telemetry decisions in the build phase, not just a nice-to-have.

Final assumptions I'd lock in explicitly rather than let get decided implicitly during build: single local Photos library, no Family Sharing/shared-library merge handling in v1; a minimum clip duration floor (something like 1–2 seconds) below which a video doesn't qualify as a candidate, to filter out accidental pocket-recordings; screen recordings and saved/downloaded videos (non-camera-captured assets, detectable via PHAsset source type) are excluded from candidacy even though they're technically "video" in the library, since scoring them as if they were personal footage would produce nonsense picks; and a fresh install with a large historical library does a chunked, resumable backfill with visible progress rather than a blocking one-shot scan, with no guarantee every past month gets a reel on day one — only that the current and upcoming months are prioritized so activation isn't blocked on the backfill finishing.
