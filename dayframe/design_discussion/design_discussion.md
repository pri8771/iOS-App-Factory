**Claude (Design Lead · Visionary) — Round 1**

Coming at this from the screens outward, because that's where this app either feels like a keepsake or feels like a gallery app with an AI gimmick, and every earlier phase locked in the *what* without ever picking the *feel*.

The boldest version of this, and the one I want to argue for hard: Dayframe should not look or feel like a utility. Everything in the prior phases — the name, the "one frame a day," the swap-as-correction, the archive-you'd-lose-if-you-churn — points toward a physical-media metaphor, not a photo-management metaphor. I'd commit visually to something closer to a darkroom/film-negative aesthetic: near-black or deep charcoal stage (the video is the only bright thing on screen, always), a single warm accent color (amber, or something closer to tungsten-bulb warmth than iOS system blue), quiet serif or humanist display type for dates and month names so it reads like a keepsake object rather than a dashboard, and thumbnails treated literally as frames — a thin frame border, maybe the faintest suggestion of a sprocket edge — never as square Photos-app grid tiles. If this ships looking like a calendar app with a video player bolted on, we've built the *feature* without building the *thing*. The two moments that have to earn this treatment, though, are day playback and the month reel reveal — I'll come back to why I'm not spending this budget everywhere.

Screen inventory, as I'd build it: a permission/access primer (the pre-ask explainer plus the three post-decision states — full, limited, denied — need to look like genuinely different screens, not the same shell with different copy, because limited-library access has to visibly communicate "I can only see some of your days" or a parent will think the app is broken); the Home calendar, which is the actual hub of the app and the one screen I want to deliberately keep *boring* and legible — a grid, month by month, swipeable, each cell either a real frame thumbnail, or truly blank, or a small distinct glyph for "waiting for iCloud" / "deferred" — because scannability here matters more than delight, and I don't want cinematic flourish fighting a parent's need to just find July 14th; Day Detail, full-bleed, minimal chrome, the pick playing automatically on entry with a swap control that's a real persistent affordance (not hidden in a menu) and disables cleanly rather than erroring when there's no next candidate; the Month Reel screen, which is the one place I want unapologetic ceremony — first time a month's reel is ready, I want the frames to visibly assemble (each day's thumbnail sliding into a filmstrip that becomes the reel's cover) rather than just presenting a video player, because that's the emotional payoff moment the whole month of quiet background work was for, and if that moment is just "here's an mp4," we've wasted the best beat we have; a Reel History/Archive screen, a shelf of month-covers going back in time, where free-tier months past the current one are visibly frosted/desaturated rather than just missing — I want the loss to be seen, not just described in a paywall sheet, because a grayed-out cover of "your April" is a much sharper regret trigger than a locked-icon placeholder; Export/Share, which needs its own preview state so the user can see the watermark-vs-clean difference before committing, not discover it after; the Paywall itself, contextual and triggered at the moment of friction (swap limit hit, locked archive month tapped, clean-export attempted) rather than a single generic upsell screen shown once at onboarding; and Settings, unglamorous by design — subscription management, the processing-cap/skip-reason disclosure, notification toggle, privacy statement — because this is the one screen where "boring and clear" is exactly correct, a parent auditing what the app can see should get bureaucratic plainness, not more ceremony.

On the primary flow: permission primer → grant → land immediately on the current month's calendar, which may be mostly empty at first launch while backfill works quietly in the background (a thin, dismissible progress indicator, never a blocking spinner over the whole screen — the user should be able to tap into whatever's already scored while older months keep filling in) → tap any day with a frame → it plays immediately, no waiting on month completion → swap if it's wrong, which snaps to the next candidate in place, same screen, no navigation away → back out to the calendar, watch it fill in over the month → a reel-ready state surfaces on the calendar itself (not just a notification — the current month's cell in a "months" strip visibly changes to show its cover is ready) → open the reel, watch the assembly ceremony once, then straight playback → share or export, previewing the watermark difference → at some point hit a wall (swap limit, an older locked month, clean export) → contextual paywall, tied to the thing they were just trying to do, not abstract.

State model, screen by screen, because this is where a build actually lives or dies: the calendar cell needs at minimum five distinct visual states — has-a-pick, honestly-empty, scanning-in-progress-for-this-day, waiting-for-iCloud, and deferred/low-signal — and if any two of those collapse into looking the same, we've reintroduced the exact "limited access looks like no video" bug the earlier phases flagged and explicitly forbade. Day Detail needs playing, no-next-candidate (swap disabled, not erroring), source-deleted ("no longer available," gracefully, no dead player), and iCloud-waiting-with-retry. The Month Reel screen needs not-ready-yet (with a legible sense of progress — "18 of ~22 days" rather than a bare percentage that means nothing to a non-technical user), first-reveal (the ceremony, shown exactly once per month), cached-return-view (instant, no re-render, no ceremony replay — replaying the assembly animation every time you reopen a reel you've already watched would curdle fast), re-rendering-after-swap (brief, clearly distinct from first-reveal so it doesn't feel like the app forgot the reel existed), and render-failed-with-retry-while-old-reel-stays-playable. The Archive needs unlocked, locked-frosted, and current-in-progress. The paywall needs to render differently depending on what triggered it, because "unlock this specific April" reads completely differently from "unlimited swaps" and conflating them into one generic sheet wastes the specific regret we just spent design effort manufacturing.

The single biggest objection to my own direction, and I want to name it rather than bury it: there's real tension between "make this feel like a premium keepsake object" and "the calendar has to be scannable in under a second because that's the actual daily-use screen." My answer is to spend the cinematic budget in exactly two places — the reel reveal ceremony and the day-detail entry — and keep the calendar grid deliberately restrained, because if I let the film-frame metaphor bleed into the grid itself (ornate borders, animated cells, texture everywhere), I'll have traded the thing that makes this usable daily for the thing that makes it demo well once. The other real risk: all this motion and ceremony has to have a Reduce Motion fallback that isn't just "well, disable it" — a user with vestibular sensitivity still deserves the reel-ready moment to feel a little special, so the fallback should be a simple cross-fade into the cover art rather than the full filmstrip assembly, not a bare cut to a video player.

On accessibility specifically, the thing I don't want lost: the calendar's state differences are currently described as visual (frosted, blank, glyph) — every one of those needs a VoiceOver label that speaks the state explicitly ("July 3rd, moment available," "July 14th, no video," "July 20th, waiting for iCloud," "April, locked — subscribe to unlock"), because a blind or low-vision parent is exactly as much the target user as anyone else and the whole "honest empty state" design idea is worthless if it's only encoded visually. The swap control needs a real accessible button in addition to whatever gesture we give it — swipe-only interactions that only work for sighted users are a known failure mode and would directly undermine the "correctability" wedge this whole product is built on. Dynamic Type needs to hold up in the reel-ready progress copy and the paywall specifically, since those are the two screens most likely to get cluttered under larger text sizes. And the warm-amber-on-charcoal palette needs an actual contrast check before anyone falls in love with it — moody and premium is worthless if the swap button reads as decoration instead of a control.

Two small gaps worth a one-line pickup in the architecture/build phase rather than grounds to fail this one: (1) the navigation container structure isn't stated explicitly — e.g., whether Calendar/Archive/Settings live under a TabView versus Archive and Settings being reached via toolbar buttons from a single NavigationStack root; a SwiftUI developer can infer something workable from the flow, but an explicit choice would remove ambiguity. Neither gap contradicts a locked decision or leaves a required workflow without any screen/state path, so this doesn't block consensus — flag both explicitly as open items for the architecture phase to close.
