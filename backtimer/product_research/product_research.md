**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

I'll dive straight into the QA/pragmatist lens on this — no headings, no scoring, just my read.

The direction I'd push hardest for a v1 is: manual entry of dishes → each dish is a list of steps with a duration and a flag for active-vs-passive → one global "serve at" time → the app walks backward from serve time and produces a single merged, chronological "do this now" list with local notifications firing at each step's start. That's the whole app. No contention modeling, no live replanning engine — just a flat merged timeline that's honest about what it is. The single biggest risk to that direction is notification reliability, and I want to be blunt about why: iOS local notifications are not guaranteed to fire at the exact second, especially under Low Power Mode, Focus modes, or if the user has notifications throttled system-wide, and a countdown app whose entire promise is "everything lands together" fails completely and visibly the one time a notification is late by four minutes on a 45-minute roast. I don't think we can "assume the API works" here — we need a belt-and-suspenders approach: schedule real `UNNotificationRequest`s for every step transition AND keep a Live Activity (ActivityKit) on the lock screen showing the current step and a live countdown, because the Live Activity is visually polled by the user's eyes even if a notification is delayed or swallowed by Do Not Disturb. If Live Activity is cut for time, that's a real scope decision, not a rounding error, and it should be flagged as such rather than silently dropped when the build gets busy.

Second thing I want on record: the "what happens when the host falls behind" question isn't just a design nicety, it's the difference between a demo and a tool people trust in a real kitchen. My pragmatist answer is: don't try to build a full re-optimizing scheduler for v1 — that's a scheduling-theory rabbit hole (which task shifts, does shifting task A push task B past the oven's availability, etc.) that could eat the entire build budget. Instead, ship a manual "I'm running late" affordance: a single button that shifts the *entire remaining plan* forward by N minutes (or shifts serve time itself), recalculating remaining step start times off the new anchor, with zero attempt to re-optimize ordering or detect new conflicts. That's honest, it's buildable in a day, and it directly answers the question without overpromising an "intelligent replanning" feature we can't actually deliver at MVP quality. The promise copy needs to reflect this precisely: "merges timelines into one countdown" — not "detects and resolves conflicts."

Third — and this is where I'd be most skeptical of scope creep — resource contention (oven/burner) detection is a trap for this build. It sounds like the single most valuable "smart" feature, but modeling shared kitchen resources correctly requires the user to also tell the app what resources each step needs, which adds a whole second data-entry burden on top of already asking them to type in every step and duration for every dish. If we make manual entry that heavy, we'll lose users at onboarding before they ever see the payoff. My strong recommendation: skip contention modeling entirely for v1, and don't even surface a "conflicts" UI placeholder — just merge and sort the timeline. If two steps land in the same 5-minute window, that's the user's problem to notice from the plain step list, same as it would be if they'd made two separate paper timelines. This should be stated explicitly in the shipped copy/onboarding so it doesn't read as a missing feature later.

On the edge-case list that must survive into implementation: multi-hour plans crossing midnight or DST need to use `Date` arithmetic (not raw component math) so a Thanksgiving-style 6-hour backward plan doesn't silently miscalculate during a DST fall-back weekend; a plan where total required time exceeds time-until-serve needs an explicit "this plan doesn't fit, here's how far over" error state at creation time — silently starting an already-late countdown is worse than refusing to save the plan; deleting/editing a dish after the countdown has started needs a defined behavior (regenerate the merged timeline, or lock it) rather than undefined behavior; and app termination/force-quit mid-cook needs the plan and scheduled notifications to survive (persisted to disk, not just in-memory), because a killed app losing a 4-hour dinner plan an hour before guests arrive is the worst possible failure mode for this specific use case.

On monetization, the pragmatist case: free tier caps you at one active plan and maybe 2 dishes per plan — enough to prove the value on a weeknight two-dish dinner — and the paid tier unlocks unlimited dishes per plan plus saved/reusable meal templates (a Thanksgiving plan you rebuild every year is genuinely worth paying for, unlike a cosmetic theme). That's decided now so the data model supports a "plan limit" check from day one instead of being bolted on.

The one thing I'd insist survives into the next phase in writing: this is a merge-and-sort tool with a manual "push everything back N minutes" affordance and a persisted, notification-plus-Live-Activity-backed countdown — not a conflict-detecting scheduler — and every place the copy could imply otherwise needs to be caught in review.

## Final Output

**Audience and use context.** The target user is a host running a multi-dish meal where timing coordination is the actual pain point — think Thanksgiving/holiday dinners, dinner parties, or any meal where a roast, sides, and a dessert all need to hit the table warm at once. This is inherently a bursty, occasion-driven use pattern (a handful of high-stakes cooks per year, likely spiking around holidays) layered on top of possible lighter weeknight two-dish use. The user is cooking with wet/messy/gloved hands, multitasking across a kitchen, often with the phone locked or physically out of reach — so the app has to be usable by glance and by ear (notification/Live Activity), not by unlocking and tapping through screens mid-cook.

**Comparable apps/patterns.** The nearest reference points are: built-in iOS Clock multi-timers, dedicated multi-timer apps (Multi Timer, Perfect Timer-style utilities), and per-step timers baked into recipe apps (Kitchen Stories, NYT Cooking). None of those do the thing backtimer is promising — working backward from one shared serve time across *several independent recipes* to produce one merged countdown. That gap is the differentiation, but it also means there's no existing UX pattern to copy wholesale; the merged-timeline view is a genuinely new interaction we have to design carefully. The flagged open item from the prior phase still stands and isn't resolved: we have not confirmed what Countertop or CueKeeper (workspace-adjacent, plausibly kitchen/timing apps) actually do, so uniqueness against them is still assumed, not verified.

**Platform-specific opportunities.** The strongest native-iOS fit is Live Activities/Dynamic Island — a glanceable, always-visible current-step-and-countdown view that doesn't require unlocking the phone, which directly answers the "messy hands, phone locked" use context. Complementary opportunities: StandBy mode turning a charging iPhone into a kitchen countdown display, haptic pulses at step transitions so a step change can be felt/heard without looking, and Siri/Shortcuts support for "what's next" queries. Apple Watch is explicitly out of scope for v1 per the non-goals, but it's a natural post-MVP extension of the same Live Activity data.

**Major assumptions and risks.**
1. Assumption: manual entry (no recipe import/OCR) is acceptable friction because the trigger occasion (a big multi-dish meal) is high-stakes enough that a host will invest a few minutes of setup — this is unverified and is the biggest adoption risk if wrong.
2. Risk: local notification delivery is not guaranteed exact-second under Low Power Mode, Focus, or system throttling — for an app whose entire promise is "everything lands together," a late or dropped alert is a visible, trust-breaking failure, not a minor bug. Live Activity is the mitigation, not a guarantee.
3. Risk: uniqueness collision with Countertop and/or CueKeeper remains unconfirmed — this is a hard blocking check before design commits to a direction.
4. Risk: scope pressure between the parent prompt's "production-ready" language and the child's "MVP build" mode could cause a later phase to quietly widen scope (contention modeling, recipe import, Watch app) instead of polishing the small surface — this needs active resistance, not just a one-time decision.

**Implications for the first build.** Ship a flat merge-and-sort backward planner (no resource-contention modeling), manual dish/step entry, one global serve time, persisted plan state that survives force-quit, local notifications paired with a Live Activity for lock-screen visibility, and a single manual "push the whole remaining plan back N minutes" affordance for when the host falls behind — no auto-replanning or conflict resolution. Promise copy must say "merges into one countdown," not "detects conflicts." Freemium boundary: free tier caps active plans/dishes at a small number sufficient to prove value on a weeknight meal; paid unlocks unlimited dishes plus saved/reusable meal templates (the once-a-year Thanksgiving plan is genuine recurring value, not a cosmetic gate). Before design locks a direction, confirm what Countertop and CueKeeper are and check the sibling Food & Drink app's direction.
