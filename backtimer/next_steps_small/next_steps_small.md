Here's the slice I'd build first: set a serve time, add exactly two dishes with two steps each, watch the merged list interleave live as the second dish's steps land inside the first dish's timeline, hit Start behind a hard confirmation, then live-execute with the current step dominant, a single fixed "+10 min, I'm running late" button, a lock-screen Live Activity showing the current step, and a real completion screen when the last step clears. That's it. One dish would build nothing worth showing — there's no merge to see, and the entire promise is the merge. Two dishes with a handful of steps is the smallest input that actually produces the "oh, it just figured out my order for me" reaction. If that reaction doesn't land with two dishes and eight total steps, it won't land with five dishes either, so this is the cheapest possible test of the actual hypothesis, not a scaled-down demo of unrelated features.

Must-haves inside that slice, non-negotiable even at this size: the live re-sort as steps are added (this has to happen before Start, not just after — it's the whole "feel the value early" bet from initial_discussion); the hard Start confirmation sheet showing the full merged order one last time; the Live Activity on lock screen, because a countdown app that only works with the phone unlocked in your hand hasn't actually tested the real use context of messy hands and a locked phone mid-cook; and a genuine completion screen, because if the payoff moment is a flat "0:00" the whole validation is worthless — you're testing whether people *feel* something, and you can't feel a zero.

What I'd cut, deliberately, even below the already-decided MVP: no saved dish templates or plan history (irrelevant to validating the merge, and it's paid-tier work that has zero bearing on whether the concept lands); no arbitrary duration picker on the late button — one fixed increment is enough to prove the mechanic exists, and a picker is UI work that teaches us nothing new; no mark-step-done-early affordance in this first slice — it's a real feature for later but it's orthogonal to "does the merge feel good," and skipping it doesn't compromise the emotional test; no multiple concurrent plans, no free/paid gating logic wired to a real IAP flow (stub the tier check, don't build StoreKit yet); no settings screen at all. The one thing I will not cut, even though it's the most expensive item on this list: the Live Activity. Every other cut is "doesn't test the hypothesis." Cutting Live Activity would mean testing a different, easier hypothesis (does a countdown list feel good on an unlocked screen) instead of the one that actually matters (does this survive real kitchen conditions) — and if we ship the small slice, get excited about the reaction, and then discover in a later phase that Live Activity is hard and the whole trust model depended on it, that's the expensive mistake this phase exists to prevent.

Biggest risk to my own direction: two dishes / eight steps might be too tidy to surface the ugly edge cases that will actually break trust in the field — a step landing in the past, a plan that doesn't fit before serve time, what "running late" does to a step that's already mid-flight. If the validation slice is too clean, we'll convince ourselves the concept works and then get blindsided by exactly the edge cases prior phases already flagged (the past-due step at creation, DST). So I'd insist the two test dishes aren't both simple — one of them should include a passive step long enough that its required start time is already in the past when the plan is created at "now," specifically to force that already-decided-but-never-built loud-flag behavior to actually get exercised, not just designed on paper.

What must survive into the build phase unchanged: this slice is not a prototype-and-throw-away — it's the first vertical build of the real MVP, using the real data model (Dish/Plan as separate entities, per initial_discussion), real SwiftData persistence, and real wall-clock Date-based scheduling, not a mocked timer. The only things deferred are templates, multi-plan, IAP, and mark-done-early — everything else in this slice is production code, not a spike. Validation criteria I'd hold this to: a person with no prior explanation can go from empty state to seeing the merged list re-sort in under two minutes; force-quitting the app mid-countdown and relaunching shows the correct current step with zero drift; locking the phone for the full duration of a step still delivers the Live Activity update and notification without the user touching the app; and the past-due step at creation actually renders its "already late" flag rather than silently accepting an impossible plan. If those four things are true, we've validated the concept for real money and effort; if any of them is false, we've learned something no amount of additional dish-count or feature breadth would have told us.
