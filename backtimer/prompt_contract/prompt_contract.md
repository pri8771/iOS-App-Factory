The parent portfolio prompt says "generate at least 12 candidate ideas, discuss thoroughly, choose the best 6, build all 6." But the child project header already says `Selected App Slug: backtimer` and hands us a fully-formed spec. Second contradiction worth naming out loud: the parent prompt demands "production-ready, not just a few features... 0 to production-ready," but the child spec says "Build mode: MVP build" and "working local-first SwiftUI MVP." Those aren't the same bar. I don't think we should silently pick one. My assumption, stated so later phases can hold me to it: we build a small feature set (MVP scope — don't try to cram in recipe import, Watch app, widgets, sharing) but whatever we do build must be finished to production craft — no placeholder screens, every screen has empty/loading/success/error states, real persistence, real accessibility, real notification handling. Small and finished, not broad and half-built. If a later phase tries to expand scope to "hit production-ready" by adding features, that's scope creep dressed up as compliance — the fix for the MVP/production-ready tension is depth of polish, not breadth of features.

Third, and this is the one I'd actually worry breaks the app in practice: the core promise is a backward-planned countdown merging multiple recipes' active and passive time to one serve time. That's a scheduling problem, not a timer problem, and scheduling problems have edge cases that timers don't. What happens when two dishes both need the oven at different temperatures at the same wall-clock moment — does backtimer even model resource contention (oven, stovetop burners, mixer), or does it just naively sum/overlap timelines and let the user discover the conflict at 6pm when the oven fight happens for real? If it doesn't model contention, the "merges several recipes into one plan" promise is weaker than it sounds and needs to be worded honestly. What happens when the user falls behind — does the whole plan recompute shifted from now, or does it keep counting toward the original serve time regardless of reality? A backward planner that doesn't handle "I'm running 12 minutes late" in real time is a spreadsheet, not an assistant, and hosts under active cooking stress are exactly the users who will punish that gap. I also want it on record that we need to decide, explicitly, whether recipe steps are manually entered by the user (durations typed in) or parsed from pasted text — if it's manual entry only for MVP, that should be stated as a non-goal (no recipe import/OCR/parsing) rather than discovered as a missing feature later.

Fourth: notifications. If the countdown is the entire value proposition and the user backgrounds the app or the phone locks (which it will, because they're cooking with wet/messy hands), do time-critical alerts still fire reliably, do they fire early enough to act on a "flip the chicken now" instruction, and is there a Live Activity / lock-screen view so the host doesn't have to unlock the phone mid-cook? This needs to be a functional requirement, not an afterthought, or the app fails at the exact moment it's supposed to be useful. I'd also want DST transitions and midnight rollover explicitly tested — multi-hour multi-dish plans (a holiday roast is a 4+ hour backward plan) can cross a DST boundary or midnight, and naive Date math breaks silently there.

Fifth, monetization: the parent prompt bans cosmetics-only monetization and demands a subscription or paid tier with genuine value. For a countdown-timer-style app that's a real design constraint, not a formality — free tiers of "just a timer" apps get cloned in a weekend, so whatever's paywalled (unlimited concurrent dishes tracked past some small free count, saved meal templates, Apple Watch companion, multiple simultaneous plans) needs to be decided now, not discovered during a monetization phase after the architecture is already locked in a way that makes gating awkward.

Sixth, and I'd flag this as the sharpest risk in the whole contract: the uniqueness rule explicitly excludes "Countertop" and "CueKeeper" as prior apps in this workspace, and both of those names sound plausibly kitchen/timing-adjacent. Nobody in this phase has actually confirmed what those two apps do. If Countertop turns out to be a kitchen-timer or multi-dish-coordination app, backtimer as specced is at serious risk of violating the hard non-negotiable uniqueness rule, and that's not something a later design phase should discover after wireframes exist — it needs to be checked and settled before we invest in a direction. Same goes for the sibling Food & Drink app in this same batch — two F&D apps are required and they can't converge, so whichever one gets designed second needs visibility into what the first one became.

What the final contract for this phase needs to preserve, concretely: that backtimer's scope is locked (this is not an ideation phase), that MVP-scope-but-production-craft is the resolution to the MVP/production-ready tension and it's a named assumption, that resource-contention handling and real-time replanning-when-late are open design questions the design phase must answer explicitly rather than default away, that manual time entry vs. recipe parsing must be decided and stated as a non-goal if it's manual-only, that background/lock-screen notification reliability is a hard functional requirement with a way to verify it (not just "trust APNs/local notifications work"), that the monetization split must be decided before architecture locks features behind boundaries that are hard to retrofit, and that uniqueness against Countertop/CueKeeper and the sibling F&D app must be actively verified, not assumed clean because nobody flagged it.
