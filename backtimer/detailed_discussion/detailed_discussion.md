Now the technical risk I think everyone's been dancing around without naming: the whole design assumes "current step" is singular — one dominant thing on screen, next step secondary. But this app's entire reason to exist is that a host has multiple dishes cooking *simultaneously* — a turkey resting while potatoes are mashing while gravy simmers. In real execution, there will routinely be several steps "in progress" at once, not a clean serial queue. If the merged timeline is really a queue of start-events ("what do I start next"), then once you start something, it doesn't disappear from the world just because the next start-event fires — a passive 45-minute roast step is still "in progress" while three other things start and finish around it. If the Live Countdown screen only shows one dominant "current step," where do the other in-flight passive steps go? Do they just vanish from view until their own end-time makes them relevant again? I think this needs to be resolved now: the screen needs a small "in progress" list (even if visually secondary to the one dominant next-action) alongside the next thing to start, or hosts will lose track of the roast that's still cooking in the background. This is a real gap in the design as currently described, not a minor detail.

Related edge case that falls out of that: two steps landing at the exact same start time from different dishes. The merge-and-sort needs a defined, deterministic tie-break (I'd suggest dish entry order as the secondary sort key) or the "current step" concept breaks entirely the moment two things are due at once — which, for a multi-dish plan, is not a rare corner case, it's Tuesday.

Two platform ceilings I want on record because they directly attack the app's core promise rather than being generic iOS trivia: first, ActivityKit Live Activities are automatically ended by the system after roughly 8 hours, and go stale well before that. A real Thanksgiving plan — prep starting mid-morning for a 6pm serve — can plausibly run 8-10 hours, which is exactly the scenario this app is built for and exactly the scenario where the lock-screen visibility guarantee could silently disappear partway through the cook. That has to be an explicit, named risk with a mitigation decided now: either the app re-requests/refreshes the Live Activity before the cap (which still leaves a real gap for plans over 8 hours), or we accept and clearly communicate that very long plans lose Live Activity visibility late in the cook while local notifications keep firing regardless. Silently hoping this doesn't come up is not acceptable given who the target user is. Second: iOS caps an app at 64 pending local notifications. Free tier's 3-dish cap is safe, but "unlimited dishes" on paid is exactly the tier most likely to be used for the big holiday spread with 6+ dishes and 6-8 steps each, which can blow past 64 scheduled notifications easily. The fix I'd bake in now: don't schedule every notification for the whole plan at once — schedule only the next N (say, 16-20) at start/foreground/each-completion, and roll forward as steps complete. That's an architectural decision, not a detail to discover during implementation.

A few more things I don't think can be left implicit. What happens if the host realizes mid-cook they forgot an entire dish, after Start has locked editing? Every prior phase has said editing is locked post-start, but nobody's said what the escape hatch is. I don't think "no escape hatch at all" survives contact with real use — I'd resolve it now as: post-start, you cannot edit existing steps/timing (that's the hard lock, for the notification-integrity reason already established), but you can add a brand-new dish, which gets merged into the *remaining* timeline only, past steps and serve time untouched. If that's rejected as too complex for MVP, then the alternative has to be an explicit, honest limitation stated at the Start confirmation screen itself ("once started, you can't add more dishes — cancel and rebuild if you're missing something"), not silence.

The "running late" push-back needs precision too: it has to shift every not-yet-started step's target time by N minutes, the serve time itself by N minutes, and — this is the part I think gets missed — the end/notification time of any already-in-progress passive step (a roast whose "it's done" alert would otherwise fire on the old schedule while everything else has moved). If push-back only touches future starts and ignores in-flight passive steps, the “done” alert for the turkey fires at the wrong time relative to the rest of the now-shifted plan, which is precisely the kind of quiet inconsistency that breaks trust in an app whose whole promise is that things land together.

On data and privacy: everything is local, single-device, no accounts, no analytics SDK, nothing to disclose beyond "your dish and step names, kept on your device" — that's the whole privacy story and it should stay that simple for v1. Notification permission and Live Activity permission are two separate iOS toggles a user can independently deny; the app needs a real degraded-mode state for "notifications denied" (visible banner, offered at the Start-confirmation screen, not app launch, with a path to Settings) since without it, the countdown still runs visually in-foreground but the core "phone locked, messy hands" promise is silently broken and the user won't know why until they miss a step.

Final assumptions I'd lock coming out of this phase: single timezone only (no travel/timezone-change handling); step start times are fixed absolute Dates computed once at plan creation, independent of each other, so marking a step done early never cascades into recomputing other steps' times; tie-broken deterministic sort order for simultaneous steps; notification scheduling is rolling/windowed, not all-at-once, to respect the 64-notification ceiling; Live Activity's 8-hour system limit is a named, accepted limitation for very long plans, communicated rather than hidden; and post-start editing is locked except for the explicitly resolved add-a-forgotten-dish path above.
