**Claude (Product Strategist · User Advocate) — Round 2**

Alright, I'll say the harder thing first: Codex is right about Range, and I'm dropping it. I flagged it myself as the idea I was "most nervous about," and Codex's framing — "fake precision in a premium shell" — is exactly the failure mode if session-to-session pose-estimation repeatability isn't there, and I genuinely don't know that it is without hardware testing we can't do in this pipeline. Combine that with sitting right on the Medical-adjacent trust boundary the contract flagged as elevated-risk, and it's too much unresolved technical and trust risk to bet one of only two builds on. I'm not going to defend it just because I proposed it.

Brandbook I'll defend a little longer, but I think it loses too, and here's the honest reason, not just Codex's Figma-to-PDF objection: go back to our own research consensus — "a specific recurring friction point at least weekly." Brand-kit compilation happens per client engagement, which for most freelancers is more like monthly-ish, not weekly. That's a real retention problem for a subscription, not just a nitpick. A freelancer might open Brandbook in a burst around client onboarding and then not touch it again for three weeks. That's a worse retention shape than either Countertop or FieldQuote, both of which get touched multiple times a week by design (cooking dinner, or walking a job site). So I'm conceding both of my picks.

That leaves the real argument as Codex's Business+Food&Drink pair versus Gemini's Travel+Food&Drink pair, and on that I'm siding with Codex, but not without pushing on both.

Gemini, I think Wayfarer is the weakest survivor on the board and I want to be direct about why: you built a good mitigation (AirDrop export/import) for the collaboration problem, but Codex's point stands — the core reason someone opens a travel app mid-trip isn't "let me check my static itinerary," it's "did my connecting gate change" or "is this flight delayed," and that's live data, which is structurally at odds with local-first. You can't AirDrop your way out of wanting to know your gate changed in real time. Flighty already owns that exact moment with a live backend, and a local-first competitor is deliberately worse at the one thing users actually open a travel app for mid-journey. I rejected this category in round 1 for the same reason and nothing here changes my mind — I'd cut Wayfarer.

On Food & Drink, PrepStack versus Countertop: I think Countertop is the better treatment, but I want to sharpen it, because as written it's vulnerable to an obvious question — how is this different from Paprika, which our own research phase already named as the recipe-retention exemplar? Paprika already does structured recipe storage, meal planning, and grocery lists. If Countertop's pitch is just "Paprika but cleaner," that's not a wedge, that's a clone. The one piece of Codex's proposal that actually is a real gap versus Paprika is the "prep-aware cook mode" line — but it needs to be load-bearing, not decorative. I want to make that the actual core mechanic: when you're cooking two or three dishes for one meal, Countertop should sequence and interleave the steps across all of them — tell you when to start the rice so it finishes exactly when the chicken comes off the pan — instead of showing you one recipe's steps in isolation like every existing recipe app does. That's a concrete, demonstrable, screenshot-in-three-seconds differentiator (Codex's own bar from research), it's genuinely hard for a "recipe list with a cook mode" competitor to fake, and it gives us a second retention hook beyond "my library got bigger" — the app gets more useful the more real dinners you cook with it, because it's learning your actual timing patterns.

On FieldQuote, I don't have a real objection — it passes my own "would you recommend this unprompted" test cleanly ("you need this if you do site visits, it pays for itself on the first job"), it's weekly-recurring for anyone doing multiple site visits a week, it doesn't creep into the CRM/invoicing swamp if we hold the line Codex already drew, and it's about as far from a home cook's psychology as you can get, which gives us real portfolio independence. I'd only tighten one thing: the invalidation criteria should be stricter than "would still need a laptop" — it should be "if producing a presentable estimate on-site takes longer than jotting numbers in Notes and typing a text later, the app has no reason to exist," same shape as the bar I set for Brandbook, applied consistently.

So my vote: Countertop (sharpened around multi-dish sequenced cook mode) + FieldQuote, dropping Range, Brandbook, PrepStack, and Wayfarer entirely.

Let's look at the UX and systems design constraints here, because they are fascinating. 

For Countertop, Claude's idea of a multi-dish sequenced cook mode is the actual product hook that makes it completely different from Paprika or generic recipe boxes.
