Tap once when something catches your eye out in the world, and Waylay quietly reminds you the next time you're actually standing near it.

## Target User

Everyday iPhone users who constantly get real-world recommendations (from friends, storefronts, social media, conversations) and lose track of them because saving something somewhere doesn't equal remembering it at the moment it matters.

You walk right past a restaurant three friends recommended, but you don't remember until you're home; or you save it to Notes or Maps and that list never gets opened again.

Apple Reminders technically supports location-triggered reminders but the capture flow is buried and slow to use in the moment; Google/Apple Maps saved lists never proactively resurface anything. ## Why Users Would Pay

Free tier caps the number of actively monitored places (bounded further by iOS's real 20-region-per-app monitoring limit); paid tier unlocks the full saved-places library with an intelligent rotation engine, richer capture (photo/voice notes), and widgets.

## Subscription Model

$3.99/mo or $24.99/yr Pro: unlimited saved places with smart region rotation, photo/voice notes, home screen widgets. Free tier: ~8 actively monitored places.

## Local-First MVP Scope

Sub-3-second capture (share sheet, camera, manual pin), CoreLocation region monitoring with priority-based rotation across the 20-region system cap, local persistent store, place library browser, proactive resurfacing notifications, snooze/dismiss/mark-visited.

## Future Backend Roadmap

CloudKit-based multi-device sync; optional shared/collaborative lists later, added carefully so it doesn't become a network-effect dependency for the core single-player loop.

Not required for the core loop. Optional on-device Vision OCR to pull a name off a photographed sign is a nice-to-have, cut without hesitation if it adds friction.

Warm, tactile, 'little discoveries' feel — resurfacing a memory, not managing a system.

### Round 1


**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

Waylay looks like it lives in the gap between “I should remember that place” and “I never open the list where I saved it.” The likely first audience is not power users of productivity apps. It is fairly ordinary iPhone users in dense, repeat-travel routines: people who walk the same neighborhoods, commute through the same corridors, revisit commercial strips, and get lightweight recommendations socially. The core use context is fast, distracted, one-handed capture while standing on a sidewalk, in a car at a light, leaving a cafe, or seeing something in passing on Instagram and wanting it tied to a real place. That matters because it pushes the app away from library-first behavior and toward ambient behavior. If the user has to “manage” places, the concept collapses into a worse Notes app.

The obvious comparison set is Apple Reminders with location triggers, Apple Maps saved places, Google Maps lists, Notes, and “save it for later” behavior in general. The lesson from those patterns is not just what features they have, but why they fail this use case. Reminders is too buried and task-coded. Maps is good at storage, weak at resurfacing. Notes is fast enough for raw capture, but detached from physical context. So the product cannot feel like “a list with a map tab.” It has to feel like a tiny capture tool with a quiet memory engine underneath. The emotional model is closer to camera favorites or voice memos than to project management. The best pattern to borrow from native iPhone apps is aggressive defaulting: save first, enrich later.

The strongest native iPhone opportunity is that the app can be useful with very little manual structure if it leans into system affordances properly. One-tap capture from a widget, share sheet, camera shortcut, or current location is more important than any browse screen. Notifications need to be treated like a product surface, not plumbing; the notification copy, timing, and actions are the feature. App Intents and widgets make the app feel native only if they collapse time-to-save. If they mostly expose the library, they are wasted. The other big iPhone-native opportunity is designing around background behavior honestly: a visible “actively watching” set, clear permission education, and a human explanation for why one place is monitored and another is waiting. That transparency is part of UX, not just debug tooling.

My biggest concern is that the concept sounds simpler than it is. There are at least three ways it can quietly break. First, region rotation is not just architecture, it is user trust. If the app saves 40 places but is only monitoring 8 or 20 of them, the user needs a comprehensible mental model or they will assume reminders are random. Second, “nearby” is emotionally sensitive. A reminder that fires across the street may feel magical; one that fires while driving past at 35 mph, or after the user already passed the place, feels dumb. Third, capture speed can easily be sabotaged by seemingly reasonable design decisions: naming a place before save, waiting for reverse geocoding, asking which list it belongs to, forcing a photo crop, or making the user confirm too much. Any of those turns the app into ceremony.

There are also a few assumptions that should be labeled clearly because they drive the first build. I am assuming the early market is urban and suburban repeat-movement users, not destination travelers; that makes recurring proximity more common and the app more valuable. I am assuming users will tolerate Always or While Using plus notification permissions only if the app demonstrates value immediately and explains itself plainly. I am also assuming the paid story is not “more storage” but “more reliable resurfacing across a larger saved library,” which means free users still need a complete experience with a smaller active set. If that assumption is wrong, the subscription model will feel arbitrary.

For the first build, the implication is that the main screen should probably not be a traditional list home. It should foreground capture and status: save something now, see what is actively being watched, understand recent resurfacing, and get back out. The library matters, but as a secondary management surface. Every saved place should have a very compact state model the UI exposes clearly: active, queued, snoozed, visited, dismissed recently. The app also needs a plain-English reason string per place like “Watching because you pass here often” or “Waiting because your active limit is full.” That detail feels small, but it is the difference between a product that seems intentional and one that seems flaky.

If I were forcing one product decision into the next phase, it would be this: optimize for save-first, explain-later. On capture, default the name, default the location, persist instantly, and let refinement happen afterward. On resurfacing, explain why it fired and provide immediate actions that teach the system what to do next. On the library, make monitoring status legible so the 20-region cap never feels like hidden failure. If the next phase loses that, Waylay becomes a pleasant concept wrapped around unreliable behavior.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Let me think through this from the failure-mode side before anyone else frames it optimistically.

On audience: the realistic user is not a power user who wants a system, it's someone mid-conversation or mid-walk who hears "you have to try this place" and has maybe four seconds of attention before the moment passes. That means the target user profile is closer to "someone who currently does nothing" than "someone who currently uses Apple Reminders location triggers or Google Maps saved lists" — those existing tools already technically solve this and are barely used for it, which tells us the failure isn't feature-availability, it's friction and trust. I'd assume this skews toward people who travel, explore new neighborhoods, or get a lot of word-of-mouth recs (foodies, new-city transplants, people whose social circle talks about places a lot) rather than a general mass-market user — worth stating as an assumption since we have no actual user research, just the spec's own framing.

On comparable apps and patterns, the honest list of "prior art" is instructive precisely because they all half-solve this and get abandoned: Apple Reminders' "remind me at a location" (buried inside a reminder-creation flow that assumes you already opened Reminders and are thinking in task terms), Google Maps "Want to go" / saved lists (passive, never resurfaces, you have to remember the list exists), Google Keep location reminders (same fate), and geofencing-based apps like Life360-adjacent or retail beacon apps (different intent — monitoring people/promos, not personal memory). The pattern across all of them: capture is not the bottleneck, decay of intent to check the list is the bottleneck. Nobody has nailed proactive, trustworthy resurfacing as a first-class citizen. That's the actual competitive opening, and it means the build should spend its scarce engineering effort on resurfacing correctness and capture latency, not on library/browsing polish — a nice place library that never proactively resurfaces is just a worse version of Apple Maps saved lists, and we'd have built the thing that already fails.

Platform-specific opportunities worth calling out concretely: the share sheet extension is probably the single highest-leverage native surface here, since a lot of real "someone recommended a place" moments happen via a text message, an Instagram post, or a Maps link someone shares — capturing directly from the share sheet without opening the app at all is the difference between "sub-3-second" and "sub-3-second in theory." Home screen widgets matter less for capture and more for ambient trust — a widget that shows "3 places nearby" turns the app into a passive-glance surface instead of something you have to remember exists, which directly fights the "list app nobody opens" failure mode. App Intents/Siri should be treated as genuinely optional here, not a checkbox: if it takes real engineering time to make "Hey Siri, remember this place" work reliably with location context, and it's not clearly faster than the share sheet or a Live Activity-style capture, I'd want it de-prioritized rather than let it eat time that should go to rotation logic and notification reliability. That's a bet, and I'll name it as one.

Now the risks, stated as things I'd actually want a QA pass to check rather than assume away. First, and this compounds the already-known 20-region cap risk: CoreLocation region monitoring on a real device is materially less reliable than anyone builds for on day one — entry/exit events can lag by minutes, get suppressed under low-power mode or with motion/significant-location-change throttling, and monitoring silently stops if the user force-quits the app (a fact Apple documents but that's easy to forget until you're debugging "why didn't it fire" in the field). If the first build doesn't have a way to distinguish "rotation correctly deprioritized this place" from "iOS just didn't deliver the event" from "user force-quit and nobody told them monitoring paused," every bug report will look identical and undebuggable. That argues for building the decision log and some minimal on-device diagnostics (even a hidden debug view) as part of v1, not as a later nice-to-have, because there's no way to validate the core promise of the app without it.

Second risk: the free tier cap (~8 places) interacting with the 20-region system cap creates a confusing three-way relationship that needs to be nailed down before any UI gets built — is the free cap "8 places saved total" or "8 places actively monitored, with older/less-relevant ones falling out of rotation automatically"? The spec implies the latter (rotation engine), which means even free users experience "rotation," just over a smaller pool, and paid users experience it over up to 20 real regions with intelligent picking from a larger saved library. If that distinction isn't crisp, either engineering will build the wrong thing or users will be baffled about why a saved place isn't notifying them. I'd flag this as needing an explicit, simple mental model a user could state back to us in one sentence — something like "we actively watch your closest/most relevant places right now, and swap in others as you move around" — and if we can't state it that simply, it's too complicated to ship.

Third risk, more pragmatic: testing this at all is hard. You can't meaningfully validate region monitoring, notification timing, or rotation behavior in the Simulator — this needs real-device walking tests or deliberate location simulation (Xcode's location simulation / GPX route playback) as part of the build-verification step, not just unit tests on the rotation algorithm in isolation. I'd want the first build to explicitly budget for "did we actually walk near a monitored region and get notified" as a manual test criterion, because unit-testing the priority-sort logic proves the algorithm is internally consistent, not that CoreLocation actually cooperates.

For implications on the first build: I'd keep the v1 slice ruthlessly small on breadth and deep on the one loop — manual pin + share sheet capture (camera/photo capture can be the thing that slips first if time is short, since it adds a permission and a UI step that a manual pin doesn't need), a rotation engine with a debuggable "why is this monitored" log, real notification delivery with explicit suppression rules (no re-notify within some cooldown after dismiss/snooze, a daily cap), library browsing with the four UI states, and a real but simple free/paid gate. Widgets and App Intents are the pieces I'd treat as negotiable scope if time runs short, since neither one is the core loop — they're amplifiers of a working loop, and an amplifier on a broken loop is wasted effort. If forced to cut something to protect ship quality, cut those two before touching capture speed or notification reliability.

Notifications need explicit suppression rules (no re-notify within a cooldown after dismiss/snooze, some daily cap) decided now so design and QA have something concrete to build and test against.
