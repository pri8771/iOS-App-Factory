**Codex (Product Strategist · User Advocate) — Round 1**

Waylay’s product promise should get even sharper than the spec: save a real-world place in under three seconds, then get one useful reminder only when being physically near it is likely to matter. That is the whole product. Not “save places,” not “build a personal map,” not “organize recommendations.” If the app cannot be explained that narrowly, it will sprawl into a list manager and lose the reason it exists.

The primary user I would keep in the room is a normal iPhone user who is out moving through life, not sitting down to manage a system. They hear “that bakery is great,” pass a shop with a long line, or get sent a Maps link in a text, and they want to catch it before the moment disappears. The important scenario is not planning a weekend itinerary at home. It is a distracted capture moment on the sidewalk, in transit, in conversation, or mid-scroll. That matters because it means the app has to win against doing nothing, not against power-user tools.

The core loop should be treated as one motion with four steps: notice something, save it instantly, let the app decide whether it is worth actively watching, then surface it once when proximity and timing are good enough to feel helpful. After that, the user either snoozes it, dismisses it, or marks it visited, and the system adapts. If any part of the app does not strengthen that loop, it is secondary at best.

The hard scope boundary I would enforce is this: v1 is a place-memory engine, not a place-discovery product and not a general reminder system. No reviews, no social features, no collaborative lists, no trip planning, no folders/tags users must maintain, no cloud dependency, no “smart” enrichment that slows capture, no feature that asks the user to do more work than “save this now.” Even inside named v1 scope, I would keep the rule that share sheet, manual pin, notification actions, monitored-state visibility, and the free-vs-paid boundary matter more than visual flourish or extra metadata.

The biggest ambiguity that needs to be resolved decisively is what the free tier actually means in plain user language. It cannot feel like “some of your places work and some mysteriously don’t.” The model should be: you can save as many places as the tier allows, but only a limited active set is being watched right now, and the app tells you why each place is active, queued, snoozed, visited, or paused. If that state model is not legible, the app will feel random even when the logic is correct.

The measurable success criteria should be concrete enough that engineering and product can both fail them honestly. Capture success means a new place can be saved from the app or share flow with no required typing and no required post-save setup in under three seconds on a real device. Resurfacing success means when entering a monitored region, the app sends at most one timely notification per place within the defined cooldown rules, and the user always has immediate actions to snooze, dismiss, or mark visited. Scope success means a first-time user can explain in one sentence what the app does, can tell which places are currently being watched, and never has to wonder whether a saved place is broken or simply not active right now. Business success for MVP is simpler: free users get enough value to trust the loop, and Pro clearly buys expanded monitoring/value rather than arbitrary storage.

The biggest risk in my own framing is that I am pushing hard toward simplicity, and that can become an excuse to under-deliver on required v1 surfaces like widgets, restore flow, or subscription enforcement. I do not want “focused MVP” to become a polite way of skipping the boring parts. The right interpretation is narrower product scope, not lower product completeness.

The one detail the final output must preserve is this: the user is not adopting Waylay to maintain a collection. They are outsourcing a moment of future recall. Every product decision should be judged by whether it makes that future “oh right, this place” moment faster to capture, more trustworthy to receive, and less annoying to live with.

**Claude (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

Coming at this fresh with a design-and-systems eye rather than re-litigating what's already locked: the product promise I'd commit to is "capture a place in one tap from wherever you are, and Waylay tells you when you're standing near it again — nothing more." That sentence has to survive contact with every screen we design. If a screen doesn't serve either the capture half or the resurfacing half, it doesn't belong in v1, full stop.

The primary user and scenario I'm designing for is a single, very specific moment: someone is mid-conversation, mid-walk, or mid-scroll, their phone is in their pocket or one thumb away, and someone just said "you have to check this place out" or they scrolled past a location tag. They have maybe three seconds of intent before it evaporates into "I'll remember" (they won't). The scenario is NOT "I am organizing my saved places on a Sunday afternoon." That second scenario exists (it's the library browser) but it is secondary, low-stakes, and should be designed to be boring and clear rather than rich — every ounce of design energy goes to the capture moment and the resurfacing moment, because those are the only two moments where the product either earns trust or loses it.

Core loop, stated as an information-architecture problem rather than a feature list: capture writes a place into one of a small number of lifecycle states (new → active-monitored → resurfaced → snoozed/dismissed/visited), and the ONLY job of everything else in the app is to move a place through that state machine correctly and transparently. The interesting design problem isn't the capture screen or the notification banner in isolation — it's that a place's state can change for reasons entirely outside the user's control (rotation kicked it out because you moved, or because you're at your 20-region ceiling), and if the UI doesn't make that legible, the whole system reads as random and untrustworthy the first time a user notices a place they cared about never got mentioned. So I'd treat "why is this place in the state it's in, right now, in one glance" as a first-class design requirement of the library screen, not an afterthought — a badge or short phrase per place, not a debug log the user has to dig for.

The biggest systemic risk I see, and the one I'd flag against my own framing: a state machine with five-plus states (new, active, resurfaced, snoozed, dismissed, visited) sounds clean on a whiteboard but creates real edge-case density — what happens to a snoozed place when it re-enters the monitoring set, does a dismissed place ever re-enter rotation at all or is dismissal terminal, what does "visited" mean if the user marks it visited manually before ever getting a proximity notification (they walked in without our help — do we still count that as a win state or silently drop it)? These aren't hypothetical corner cases, they're the majority of real usage once free-tier users have more saved places than monitored slots. I want it decided now, not discovered in the design phase under time pressure: dismissed is terminal (never re-enters rotation, place is effectively archived unless the user explicitly reactivates it from the library), snoozed is temporary and time-boxed (re-enters rotation eligibility after the snooze window, not immediately), and manually-marked-visited counts as a completed loop regardless of whether a notification ever fired — because punishing the user for beating the app to the punch would be perverse.

Hard scope boundary I want stated plainly in this phase's output, not left implicit: the library browser is a management surface, not a discovery surface. No search-driven recommendations, no "places near you that you haven't saved," no social layer, no maps-replacement functionality. If a future design phase starts sketching a map view with pins and filters and sort options, that's scope drift away from "ambient memory" and toward "worse version of Maps" — the systems-thinking argument against it is that every extra affordance in the library is a place a rotation bug or a permission edge case can hide, and we have limited design/engineering budget to spend making sure the two moments that matter (capture, resurface) are bulletproof.

Measurable success criteria I'd hold this phase to, stated so a developer could check them without ambiguity: (1) time from "user has the intent to save a place" to "place is durably persisted" is under 3 seconds and requires at most one confirmation tap — no required text entry, no required category, no wait on network/geocoding before the save completes locally; (2) for any saved place at any time, the app can state in one short phrase why it is or isn't currently being monitored (this is a UI requirement, not just a debug requirement); (3) a user who dismisses or snoozes a place never gets re-notified about that specific place within its cooldown window — zero tolerance, this is a trust mechanic not a nice-to-have; (4) the free-tier cap and the rotation-into-Pro upgrade path can be explained back by a user in one sentence after seeing the UI once, without reading help text.

The one thing I don't want lost from this phase: the temptation will be to describe this as a "places app with reminders," and if that framing wins, we'll unconsciously start designing toward a list-manager information architecture (tabs, sort, filter, bulk actions) that fights the actual promise. The IA should be built around exactly two front-and-center surfaces — capture (fast, gets out of the way) and "what's happening right now" (what's being watched, what just resurfaced) — with the full library one level deeper, reachable but not default-home.
