That’s not just an architecture cleanliness point, it’s a product integrity point. The minute we let the app start “estimating” quarterly taxes or variable renewals, we’ve quietly turned one conservative engine into two weaker ones, and the second one is exactly the kind of fake-smart feature that makes finance apps feel magical right up until they burn someone. Dana can tell us her next insurance payment is probably $1,200 or her quarterly tax set-aside bill is likely $2,800. Fine. She enters that. But the app does not infer it, smooth it, or get clever. One engine means one engine.

So my vote is to close this phase with the following assumptions treated as resolved, not tentative: monthly user-facing cycle over a dated-event engine; income entries on real dates; obligations with cadence and due dates, but always manual amounts; reserve balance user-entered only and optional, with its own empty state; safe-to-spend allowed to go negative and shown honestly; confidence shown as insufficient, low-confidence computed, or fully computed; no silent dedupe, no silent normalization, negative adjustments allowed, export/import included, notifications out, and biometrics nice-to-have but below truthfulness features if scope gets tight. That’s enough specificity that design and architecture can move without inventing product policy on the fly, which is the real job of this phase.
