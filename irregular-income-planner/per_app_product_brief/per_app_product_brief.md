**Codex (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Irregular Income Planner should be built for self-employed and variable-pay workers who are not trying to “budget better” in the abstract; they are trying to avoid making one bad decision after a good month. The user is a freelancer, contractor, commission earner, tipped worker, or seasonal operator with predictable obligations and unpredictable inflows. The use case is narrow on purpose: before they move money, spend freely, or decide what they can safely treat as personal pay this cycle, they open the app to get one conservative answer they trust.

The product they should pay for is not tracking, charts, or generic finance hygiene. It is decision safety under volatility. The free experience can cover the core local planner: manual income history, recurring obligations, reserve target, manual low-month fallback, and one safe-to-spend recommendation with plain-language risk context. The subscription has to unlock real utility that changes behavior: multiple planning scenarios, longer historical windows, alternate baseline strategies, “what if I lose two clients” stress tests, obligation timing simulations, and premium forward views like risk-window calendars and reserve recovery plans. That is functional value. If premium is just extra themes or prettier charts, the app has no business charging.

The core loop should stay brutally small. The user logs income as it arrives, keeps recurring obligations current, and sees the safe-to-spend number update from the same conservative engine every time. If there is enough history, the number is computed from a deliberately cautious baseline. If there is not enough history, the app says so clearly and uses the user’s manual low-month estimate instead. The user then either pays themselves, holds back, or adjusts obligations/reserve assumptions, and comes back next cycle. The app earns retention only if that loop is faster than opening a spreadsheet and more trustworthy than eyeballing a bank balance.

The wedge is that this is not a budget tracker and not a forecasting toy. Most adjacent products either assume stable income, bury irregular earners inside broad budgeting software, or overfit to averages that become dangerous after a windfall month. This app’s defensible position is that it is conservative by construction, explicit about uncertainty, and centered on one high-stakes recurring decision. It should make the user feel that the app is protecting them from overconfidence, not encouraging financial optimism. That is a stronger angle than “AI finance assistant,” and more credible for a local-first MVP.

The growth angle is niche but real: freelancers and commission earners already swap survival tactics in communities, group chats, creator circles, contractor forums, and among peers with the same feast-or-famine pattern. The app can grow through shareable, privacy-safe outputs like a redacted “safe this month / holdback / runway” card or a scenario snapshot that teaches the method without exposing raw transactions. The organic pitch is simple enough to repeat: “this is the thing I check before I pay myself.” That is better than chasing broad virality with a generic consumer finance message.

Local-first needs to be a product benefit, not just an architecture note. All income history, obligations, reserve targets, scenario state, and outputs should live on-device with deterministic persistence and full functionality offline. No account should be required to get value. That matters here because people are entering sensitive earnings data and may not trust a new finance app with cloud sync on day one. The cloud-ready path is straightforward if the boundaries stay clean: keep the planning engine pure and deterministic, isolate storage behind a repository, and treat sync later as replication of canonical local records rather than a rewrite of business logic. Future cloud can add encrypted backup, device sync, shared accountant export, or optional advisory features, but v1 must not depend on any of it.

The real reason this app deserves to exist is that irregular earners do not mainly suffer from lack of data; they suffer from unstable decision rules. A good month lies to them, a lean month scares them, and generic budgeting tools are built around steady paychecks. This app can win by replacing mood-based money decisions with one conservative operating number. If it does that reliably, users keep it because it becomes part of the moment that matters: “can I afford to move this money yet?”

The biggest risk in this direction is false authority. If the math is opaque, too optimistic, or too eager to produce an answer with weak data, the app becomes actively harmful. The brief has to preserve one non-negotiable detail into design and build: the app must prefer an honest “not enough data yet” state over a confident lie, and every premium feature must deepen decision quality around uneven income rather than widen scope into general personal finance.

The competitive wedge against the budgeting apps and even against the few "freelancer finance" apps already out there is the conservative-baseline decision the earlier phases locked: nobody else, as far as this brief needs to assume, is willing to intentionally understate what you can spend to protect you from your own best month. Most competitors either average blindly (dangerous for lumpy income, as already established) or require bank-linked transaction data to feel "smart," which means they're doing expense inference, not obligation planning, and they take weeks to feel accurate. This app is accurate on day one of real use because it doesn't need to learn your spending habits — it only needs your income history and your known fixed costs, both of which the user already knows cold. That's a faster and more honest path to a trustworthy number than anything that waits on bank sync or transaction categorization to "learn" the user.

Growth angle, and I want to be honest that this is a niche-virality play, not a broad one, because pretending otherwise would be dishonest to the brief: irregular earners talk to each other inside tight communities — photographer Discords, rideshare driver forums, actor/gig-worker subreddits, freelance Slack groups — and the thing that spreads in those communities isn't a referral code, it's someone posting a screenshot of the safe-to-spend screen saying "this told me not to buy the thing and it was right." That only works if the screen itself is screenshot-worthy: clean, single-number, not cluttered with chart junk, something you'd actually show a friend without it looking like a spreadsheet. I'd treat "would Dana screenshot this to a friend unprompted" as an actual design bar, not a marketing afterthought bolted on later.

Local-first isn't a technical footnote here, it's part of the trust pitch: this is financial data about a person's real, uneven income, and "your numbers never leave your phone unless you choose otherwise" is a legitimate feature to say out loud in onboarding, not just an architecture decision nobody mentions. The cloud-ready path — a sync layer added later without restructuring the local store — should exist so that a future "restore on new phone" or optional backup doesn't require re-architecting the persistence model, but I want to be firm that v1 must be fully functional with zero network dependency, because a finance tool that goes dark without wifi has already broken the promise.

The biggest risk to my own direction: I'm assuming Dana will tolerate manually entering income and obligations rather than wanting bank sync, and if that assumption is wrong, the whole "fast, honest, no-waiting-to-learn-you" pitch collapses into "yet another app asking me to do data entry." The one detail I most want preserved past this phase: the paid tier is more depth on the same trustworthy engine — longer history, scenario stress-testing, a longer risk-window horizon — never a second calculator, never cosmetic, and the free tier has to be good enough on its own that someone would recommend it to a friend before ever hitting a paywall.

## Final Output

**Target user and use case:** Freelancers, contractors, commission-based salespeople, tipped and seasonal workers — people with steady bills and unsteady pay. The moment that matters isn't onboarding, it's the recurring "can I afford this right now" check three months into real use, before they move money, spend, or pay themselves.

**Paid value and subscription value:** Free tier gives the whole core engine for real — safe-to-spend number from a fixed trailing lookback, plus one risk-window flag — genuinely useful on its own, never crippled to force an upgrade. Premium unlocks more depth on that *same* engine, never a second calculator: longer historical windows (12–24 months) for seasonal businesses, named what-if obligation scenarios ("what if I lose this client," "what if rent goes up $200"), alternate baseline strategies, a rolling year/risk-window horizon instead of just the current cycle, and reserve-recovery guidance. This is functional value tied to depth and foresight, not cosmetics.

**Core loop:** Log an income entry or a cycle's income and keep obligations/reserve current → the same conservative engine recomputes the safe-to-spend number → user sees that number, a plain-language runway read, and any risk-window flag, with a visible signal of whether it's computed-from-history or manual-fallback → user acts (pay themselves, hold back, adjust obligations) → repeat next cycle. Checking should take under ~20 seconds; logging a cycle under ~90. Anything that slows that down (mandatory categorization, review rituals) is scope creep.

**Competitive wedge:** Budgeting apps assume steady income and average blindly; "smart" finance apps wait on bank-linked transaction data to infer spending habits, which takes weeks to feel accurate. This app needs only income history and known fixed obligations — data the user already has memorized — and is deliberately conservative by construction, so it's trustworthy from the first real week of use, not after a learning period.

**Growth angle (niche, not broad):** Irregular earners already trade survival tactics in tight communities — photographer Discords, gig-worker forums, freelancer Slacks. The spreadable artifact is a clean, screenshot-worthy single-number screen (or a redacted "safe this month / holdback / runway" card) that someone shares with "this told me not to buy the thing and it was right." Design the primary screen to be shareable on purpose, not as an afterthought.

**Local-first and cloud-ready plan:** All income history, obligations, reserve targets, scenarios, and outputs live on-device with deterministic persistence; the app is fully functional offline with no account required — and that's a stated trust pitch in onboarding ("your numbers never leave your phone unless you choose otherwise"), not just a technical detail, since this is sensitive earnings data. Cloud-readiness comes from keeping the planning engine pure/deterministic and storage behind a repository boundary, so a future sync layer is replication of canonical local records — encrypted backup, new-device restore, optional export — rather than a rewrite of business logic.

**Why this app deserves to exist:** Irregular earners aren't short on data about their own finances — they're short on a stable decision rule. A good month makes them overconfident, a lean month makes them panic, and mainstream budgeting tools are all built for steady paychecks. This app replaces mood-based money decisions with one conservative, honest number, and its non-negotiable line into design and build is: prefer an honest "not enough data yet" state over a confident lie, and every premium feature must deepen decision quality about uneven income — never widen scope into general personal finance.
