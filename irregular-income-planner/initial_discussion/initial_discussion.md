### Round 1


**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

The one-sentence product promise should be this: Irregular Income Planner is a local-first iPhone app that helps people with uneven income calculate a conservative, trustworthy amount they can safely pay themselves this cycle without endangering upcoming obligations.

The primary user is someone whose income arrives unpredictably but whose bills do not: freelancers, contractors, commission workers, creatives, seasonal workers, tipped workers, and solo operators who need a steady decision rule, not a budgeting hobby app. The key scenario is not “track every purchase.” It is “I got paid, I have bills coming, my last few months were uneven, and I need to know what I can safely treat as available right now.” If the app drifts toward category management, spending charts, or generic wellness language, it has already lost the plot.

The core loop needs to stay brutally short. The user records income entries, records fixed obligations and reserve targets, and immediately gets one primary output: a safe-to-spend number for the current cycle. From that same engine, the app can show two supporting interpretations that matter: how long the current buffer lasts if income stays conservative, and where the next risk window appears when obligations overtake the safe baseline. That is enough. I would actively resist adding goals, debt plans, bank syncing, invoice generation, tax estimators, or collaborative household features, because each of those expands the information architecture and weakens the app’s clarity.

The biggest UX risk is false confidence. Finance apps usually fail less by being ugly than by looking reassuring while hiding shaky assumptions. If this app shows a precise dollar number too early, or without clearly signaling that it is based on sparse data or a manual fallback, it will feel polished and still be wrong. That is worse than an obvious empty state. So the insufficient-data experience has to be a first-class flow, not an error afterthought: with zero or one income entries, the app should explicitly say it cannot calculate a dependable baseline yet, explain why, and let the user enter a cautious manual low-month estimate until real history is available. The app should make it visually clear whether today’s answer is computed from history or guided by fallback input.

Hard scope boundaries should be explicit enough that nobody can smuggle in a second app. This build is only about conservative spend planning under income volatility. It is not a budgeting system, not a net-worth tracker, not bookkeeping, not tax prep, not investment advice, not a subscription manager, not bank-linked automation, and not multi-user finance coordination. StoreKit is also out of scope. A premium tier can exist conceptually, but this build should spend its complexity budget on a trustworthy planning engine and clear states, not purchase plumbing.

For measurable success criteria, I’d lock five things. A new user can understand the app’s promise within one session. A user with sparse data gets an honest fallback flow instead of a fabricated result. A user with several months of uneven income, including a windfall month, gets a safe-to-spend number that stays conservative rather than spiking upward. A returning user can relaunch and find all entries and outputs intact. And a developer or tester can explain the app in one sentence without listing a bundle of loosely related finance features.

The detail that must survive into later phases is this: the app is selling confidence, not calculation volume. One engine, one primary answer, and visible honesty about uncertainty. If that gets watered down, the product turns into a dressed-up spreadsheet and loses its reason to exist.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Here's my take, coming at this from the standpoint of someone who has to figure out how this thing breaks before we write a line of Swift.

The product promise I'd lock in: this is the app that tells a freelancer or gig worker, in one number, what's actually safe to pay themselves this cycle without going broke two lean months from now — everything else in the app is in service of that one number being trustworthy. If we can't say that sentence, we haven't earned the right to start building. I'd resist any temptation to soften it into "a financial planning tool for irregular earners" — that's a category description, not a promise, and categories don't tell you what to cut when you're behind schedule.

Primary user and scenario, concretely: a freelance designer, rideshare driver, or commission-based salesperson who gets paid unevenly — some months $6k, some months $1.5k — and currently either eyeballs their bank balance and hopes, or over-corrects into anxious hoarding. The scenario that matters most isn't "onboarding day," it's the boring Tuesday three months in where they open the app before deciding whether they can afford something, see a number, and act on it. If the app is only good on day one and gets stale or ignored by month two, we've built a demo, not a tool. That recurring-use moment is the one I'd design and test against, not the flashy first-run screen.

Core loop, stated as plainly as I can: log an income entry (or a cycle's worth) → the app recomputes the conservative baseline and current safe-to-spend number using trailing history minus known obligations → user sees that number plus a runway/cushion read and any risk-window flag → user makes a real decision (spend, save, brace) and optionally logs an obligation or reserve change → next cycle, repeat. Everything else — settings, history charts, premium scenario stuff — hangs off that loop; nothing should be able to exist as an island screen unconnected to it.

Hard scope boundaries, and I want to be blunt about the ones I think will get quietly violated under time pressure: no bank linking, no multiple income "categories" or tagging taxonomy (that's budgeting-app creep), no debt payoff or goal-tracking module, no tax-withholding calculator even though it's tempting and adjacent, no actual purchase/StoreKit code, no multi-currency support beyond basic locale formatting of a single currency. The single riskiest scope-creep vector I see is "let's also add expense tracking so the safe-to-spend number is more accurate" — that's a second product bolted onto this one, and it should be resisted; obligations should be entered as known recurring fixed costs, not itemized transaction tracking.

Measurable success criteria, the way I'd actually test them rather than just assert them: (1) with a synthetic dataset containing one windfall month and one lean month, the computed safe-to-spend number must not move meaningfully off the windfall — I'd want an actual numeric tolerance defined once we pick the percentile method; (2) zero-entry and one-entry states must render an honest "not enough data" screen with a manual fallback, never a $0 or a fabricated figure — this is the one I'd write an automated test for first, because it's the easiest thing to accidentally regress; (3) force-quit and relaunch after entering data must reproduce the exact same numbers, not recalculated-differently or lost; (4) every number on screen must be traceable by hand from the input data — if a QA person can't reconstruct the safe-to-spend figure with a calculator from the raw entries, the math is too opaque to be a finance product people trust with real decisions.

The thing I most want the final phase output to preserve, above all the rest: this is a one-number app with supporting context, not a dashboard. If the one-sentence promise ever needs a second sentence to explain what the app actually does day-to-day, we've lost the plot.
