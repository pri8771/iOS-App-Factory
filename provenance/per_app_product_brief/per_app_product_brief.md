**Codex (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

Provenance should be built for the collector who has already crossed the line from hobby to responsibility. This is the person with real money tied up in objects, a spreadsheet that is always half out of date, and a recurring low-grade anxiety that if something were stolen, damaged, or needed to be documented tomorrow, they would be reconstructing facts from memory and camera roll scraps. The product is not for browsing collectibles, trading, or fandom. It is for fast capture at the moment of acquisition and credible review later. The key use case is simple and demanding: photograph the item, save it before the details disappear, and trust that the record can deepen over time without getting messy.

The paid value has to be operational, not decorative. Free needs to prove the basic ledger: up to 25 manually created items, structured condition and provenance fields, and a clean local catalog that already feels better than Notes. Paid should unlock the behaviors that make the app become a living record instead of a static list: unlimited items, on-device classification assist during capture, manual value history with charts, insurance-ready export, and the value widget. That is a defensible subscription because it maps to ongoing utility: larger collections, continued documentation, periodic valuation updates, and repeated export needs. If paid is just “nicer screens” or cosmetic organization, it will not hold. The reason to keep paying is that the app becomes the authoritative ledger for the collection.

The core loop should be designed around relief, not completeness. A user opens capture, takes or selects a photo, enters a title, confirms or overrides a category suggestion, and saves. At that point the item exists and shows up in the catalog. Everything else is enrichment after the fact: purchase facts, provenance notes, dated condition updates with photos, and dated value entries. That separation matters because it keeps the intake flow fast enough to beat a spreadsheet while still allowing the record to become serious over time. On the detail screen, the information architecture should make the record feel archival: a stable cover image and canonical facts at the top, then clearly separate modules for condition/provenance history and value history. If those histories collapse into one generic feed, the record stops feeling trustworthy.

The competitive wedge is not better identification than category-specific apps. That would be a false fight and the app would lose it. The wedge is that Provenance is the one place a serious collector can maintain a cross-category record that is photo-anchored, condition-aware, exportable, and legible later. Specialist tools go deeper in one domain; spreadsheets stay flexible but sloppy. Provenance wins by being faster than spreadsheets at capture and more credible than both spreadsheets and generic inventory apps at review. The museum-catalog aesthetic only matters if it reinforces that: restrained dark neutrals, strong typographic hierarchy, and layouts that make dates, notes, and evidence easy to scan. Premium here means orderly and serious, not dramatic.

The local-first behavior should be visible in the product, not just in the architecture. Item creation, editing, charting, browsing, classification fallback, and export all need to work offline with deterministic behavior. Photo storage should be managed locally with a clear strategy for cover images, timeline images, and thumbnailing so the app does not silently become a storage problem. The app should also act like a trustworthy local ledger: drafts survive interruption, downgrade never deletes data, and deletion is soft-delete with recovery rather than instant destruction. The future cloud path is important, but it should stay behind a repository boundary and never leak into v1 UX. Later sync should look like optional continuity and backup, not a redesign. The same goes for future market feeds: they should enrich the value ledger, not redefine the product around external intelligence.

The growth mechanism is niche and credibility-driven, not mass social. This app should spread through moments where collectors have to show their records to someone else: insurer conversations, estate planning, selling prep, collector group recommendations, and “what do you use to track all this?” The export is part of growth because it externalizes the product’s seriousness. If the PDF is clean enough that a broker, appraiser, spouse, or buyer can understand it immediately, the app advertises itself in exactly the environments that matter. The viral loop is not posting collections publicly. It is being the tool people recommend when another collector admits their inventory is trapped in Notes.

What will make people keep the app is not the first capture. It is the feeling that each update makes the record more defensible rather than more cluttered. Users will stay if opening an item six months later shows a clear chronology of what changed, what it cost, how its condition evolved, and what value entries were recorded, with photos that still make sense in context. They will stay if the app feels like a durable private archive, not a generic database. They will leave if valuation feels fake, classification feels overconfident, or the detail screen becomes a dumping ground.

The biggest risk in this direction is that the product may feel too restrained to justify a subscription if the user never builds the habit of adding value entries. That is a real objection. The answer is not to fake intelligence. The answer is to make the ongoing paid behaviors genuinely useful even when valuation is manual: unlimited catalog growth, fast assisted intake, periodic export, collection-level value visibility, and a record structure that is visibly better as the collection gets larger and older. If value fields remain sparse in real use, the app should be willing to lean harder into catalog plus condition history rather than pretending it is a market intelligence product.

The detail the final phase output must preserve is this: Provenance deserves to exist only if it becomes the most credible and lowest-friction way to maintain a serious record of valuable physical objects across categories. Not the smartest-looking app, not the most automated app, and not a thin spreadsheet replacement. It has to feel like a private archival ledger that is fast at capture, calm at review, and honest about what is manual versus assisted.

Everything else — condition notes, provenance, purchase price, value entries — is enrichment reachable from the item's detail screen whenever the user feels like it, never a gate in front of the save button. The moment this turns into a five-field intake form before the item exists, it's dead on arrival against the spreadsheet it's replacing.

The competitive wedge is documentation rigor across categories, not identification depth in any one category. WatchCharts knows more about watches than this app ever will; CellarTracker knows more about wine. Provenance wins by being the one place that treats a watch, a case of wine, and a graded card with the same seriousness of record — dated condition photos, dated value entries, a purchase-fact record, and an export that reads like a dossier, not a printout of a spreadsheet. If a later design pass tries to compete on recognition accuracy instead of documentation quality, that's a strategic error worth stopping, because it's a fight this app can't win with on-device Vision alone.

Growth here is niche, not viral in the conventional sense, and I want to be honest about that rather than force a share button that doesn't fit. The realistic mechanism is the export itself as a distribution artifact — a well-made insurance/estate-style PDF report is the kind of thing a user shows a partner, a fellow collector, or a forum thread ("here's what I use to track my collection"), and that's a slower but more durable loop than anything social-feed-shaped. I would not build sharing, comments, or public profiles to manufacture virality; that would contradict the single-user privacy stance this app needs given it's cataloging someone's valuables and what they paid for them. If growth needs a boost, it comes from App Store positioning and word of mouth in collector communities that already exist, not from in-app social mechanics that don't belong in a private ledger.

Local-first is not a checkbox here, it's the trust foundation, and I want three specific things locked as build requirements rather than left as design nice-to-haves: Face ID/passcode app-lock, since this app holds an itemized list of expensive possessions and what the owner paid for them — that data being visible on an unlocked phone is a real-world risk, not a hypothetical; soft-delete with a 30-day recovery window on items, because a slip-of-the-thumb delete of a $9,000 item's entire history is the single worst thing that can happen to this product's credibility, worse than any bug; and a stated, verified position on device backup — SwiftData's default store rides along in standard encrypted iCloud/local backups, and the app should say so explicitly in settings, because "my only copy of my insurance record lives on one phone with no visible backup story" is a five-star-review-killer the first time someone loses a phone. The cloud-ready path is a repository protocol boundary now, with sync and an opt-in market-price feed as clearly-labeled future work behind that same interface — nothing about the MVP should require network access to function, and nothing about the architecture should make adding sync later require touching the view layer.

What actually keeps someone using this past the first week is not the classification assist and not even the chart — it's whether they trust the app enough to make it their only copy of the record. That trust is earned or lost in a small number of concrete moments: does save ever silently fail or lose a photo, does a subscription lapse ever delete rather than hide data, does deleting an item ever feel irreversible without a safety net, does the export actually open and look correct on the first try with zero, one, and many items. Those are the moments I'd test explicitly before calling this done, because a beautiful capture flow sitting on top of an untrustworthy save/export path is a worse product than a plain one that never loses data. The biggest risk to this whole direction is that the team over-invests in the archival aesthetic and Vision assist because they're the fun parts to build, and under-invests in the boring parts — soft-delete, backup confirmation, downgrade data preservation, export correctness at edge-case item counts — which are the parts that actually determine whether a real collector trusts this app with their $40,000 watch collection. I'd rather ship a visually plainer app where every one of those boring guarantees is verified than a gorgeous one where any of them is assumed.
