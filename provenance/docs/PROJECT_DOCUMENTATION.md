The product's identity for this build: a documentation-and-trust ledger that's faster than a spreadsheet at capture and more credible than a spreadsheet at review — not a valuation-intelligence product.

**Target user and use case:** Someone who already has real money tied up in physical goods — watches, vinyl, graded cards, wine, sneakers — and is currently tracking it in a spreadsheet or Notes doc that's drifted out of sync. This only holds up as "real utility" if the ongoing paid behaviors keep earning their keep as the collection grows and ages — bigger catalog, more history, more export occasions — not because the classification or valuation got smarter.

**Competitive wedge:** Not identification depth — WatchCharts will always know more about watches, CellarTracker more about wine, and trying to out-recognize them is a fight this app loses with on-device Vision alone. It beats spreadsheets on capture speed and beats both spreadsheets and generic inventory apps on review credibility.

**Viral or niche growth angle:** Not social, not a share button, not public profiles — that would contradict the privacy stance a private valuables ledger needs. The real risk to that promise isn't underbuilt aesthetics or thin classification — it's under-investing in the unglamorous guarantees (soft-delete, backup confirmation, downgrade preservation, export correctness at every item count) in favor of the more fun parts to build. ## Detailed Discussion

Codex proposed it directly: don't interleave locked items into the active list and don't banish them to a separate destination either — put a clearly-labeled "Preserved Items" section right on the main catalog screen, collapsed with a couple of cover-photo peeks and a count, tap through to a full read-only list.
