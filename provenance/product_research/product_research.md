**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

The likely buyer is not a casual hobbyist browsing collectibles for fun. It is a person with enough value tied up in physical objects that losing track of condition, purchase details, or proof of ownership feels financially irresponsible. That changes the UX bar. They are not looking for delight first; they are looking for confidence, speed at the moment of acquisition, and records that still make sense a year later. The use context is usually one-handed, interrupted, and immediate: standing at a table after a purchase, unboxing at home, logging something before the receipt disappears, or updating condition after noticing damage. A secondary context is slower and more analytical: reviewing the collection, checking total exposure, and producing a report for insurance, estate planning, or resale prep.

The closest alternatives are not just collector apps. They are spreadsheets, Notes, photo albums, Airtable-style personal databases, category-specific apps like watch or wine trackers, and the pattern set by home inventory apps. The spreadsheet and Notes comparison matters most because that is the real incumbent behavior: flexible, ugly, and trusted because the user controls it. That means Provenance cannot win by being more clever. It has to win by making structured record-keeping faster than a spreadsheet without feeling less trustworthy. The category-specific app pattern is also a warning: users in watches or cards may already know tools that go deeper than this app ever will in their niche. So the cross-category pitch only works if the app is visibly excellent at documentation and history, not if it pretends to have specialist identification depth it does not have.

The native iPhone opportunities are strong, but they are narrower than the prompt makes them sound. Camera capture is the real platform advantage because it turns logging into a physical-world workflow instead of a form. Vision can help only if it stays humble and low-stakes: coarse category suggestion, maybe a few labels, never authoritative item identity. Files export and local PDF generation fit the trust model because they create a portable artifact the user can keep outside the app. Widgets are useful only if they reinforce the habit loop; for paid users that is total collection value or recent valuation change, and for free users it probably has to be item count or “last logged item” because the core value metric is gated. Accessibility is not optional here because collectors may be older and detail-heavy screens are exactly where small text and low contrast quietly kill usability.

The biggest market assumption is that serious collectors actually want one cross-category home for records instead of multiple specialist tools. That might be true for users whose problem is household asset tracking, inheritance, or insurance documentation. It may be false for users who are deeply embedded in one category and already rely on niche metadata standards. Another assumption is that manual value entry is enough to sustain engagement before a live price feed exists. I think that is shaky. Many users will record purchase price once and never maintain value history unless the app makes the payoff obvious. A third assumption is that “provenance” as a term helps rather than intimidates. For some audiences it signals seriousness; for others it sounds academic and heavier than the actual workflow.

The most important product risk is that the app tries to look authoritative in areas where it is structurally weak. “Insurance-grade” is the obvious example, but there is a UX version of the same mistake: charts and classification can create a false sense of precision. If the app shows a value trend line based on three manual entries, it may look smarter than it is. If the app suggests a model from a photo, users may over-trust it even with a confidence label. The design has to counter that. It should feel archival, not predictive. That means emphasizing source, date, notes, and photo evidence over algorithmic confidence theater.

Another risk is workflow friction. The app wants photos, condition, provenance, purchase facts, and maybe value, which is a lot to ask right after acquisition. If the first-run experience turns into a long intake form, users will revert to Notes. The first build needs a fast path that captures the minimum viable record in under a minute: photo, title, category, acquisition date, purchase price optional, notes optional. Condition history and provenance depth can expand after save. If that progressive disclosure is not preserved, the whole product becomes homework.

The design pattern I would borrow is less “dashboard app” and more “catalog record.” Each item should feel like a durable file with a cover photo, canonical facts, a dated timeline of changes, and a clean audit trail. The list view should not default to noisy KPI tiles. It should feel like browsing a private archive. The detail screen is the product. That is where trust is built: clear metadata grouping, visible last-updated dates, obvious provenance entries, and a condition timeline that supports photos over time instead of burying them in a generic gallery. The report preview should mirror that structure so the export feels like an extension of the app, not an afterthought.

I would also be careful with dark, museum-like styling. It fits the brand, but it can easily become illegible, especially on chart and photo-heavy screens. The right move is restrained dark neutrals with strong type hierarchy and disciplined contrast, not cinematic darkness. If the visual system makes labels, dates, or value fields harder to scan, it is harming the core job.

For the first build, I would treat the shippable version as a documentation ledger with optional valuation, not a valuation intelligence product. The app should be strongest at capture, structured records, timeline history, and export. Classification assist should be visibly secondary. Value charts should exist, but only after the item record itself feels complete and credible. If there is one detail the final output must preserve, it is this: the app has to make serious record-keeping feel faster than a spreadsheet without ever pretending to know more than the user. That restraint is the whole product.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Independent take on this phase's actual job, before anyone else weighs in:

The realistic audience isn't "collectors" broadly — it's a fairly narrow slice: people who already own a spreadsheet or Notes doc for watches, vinyl, cards, wine, or sneakers and are annoyed by it. That means two very different use moments that the app has to serve differently: a fast, high-friction-tolerance capture moment right after a purchase or unboxing (bad lighting, one-handed, wants to log and move on), and a slower periodic review moment (checking total value before a renewal, reconciling condition before selling, browsing the collection). If the capture flow is optimized for the wrong one — say, a rich multi-step wizard — it'll lose to "just add a row in Excel" on the first try, which is the exact competitor this app has to beat.

For comparable patterns, I'd look at three different buckets rather than one direct competitor, because none of them do the whole thing: home-inventory-for-insurance apps (Encircle, Nest Egg) are the closest match for the "photo + PDF export for insurance" workflow and are worth studying for what makes an export feel credible rather than gimmicky — itemized, dated, photo-anchored, boring in a good way. Category-specific collector apps (CLZ/Collectorz per-category, CellarTracker for wine, WatchCharts/Chrono24 for watches, Discogs for vinyl) show the cross-category promise is genuinely differentiated, but also show why single-category apps dominate: they can lean on a real external database or price feed for identification and valuation, which this app explicitly can't do in v1. General inventory apps (Sortly) show the barcode/folder/low-stock pattern, which is mostly irrelevant here except for the lesson that generic inventory tools feel cheap precisely because they don't specialize — Provenance's "quiet museum-catalog" direction only pays off if the fine details (typography, dates, structured fields) are actually more careful than a generic tool, not just darker-colored.

On platform fit, the thing I'd flag as under-considered in the spec is app-lock. This app is going to hold a structured, itemized list of a user's expensive possessions plus purchase prices — that's exactly the kind of data you don't want visible if someone picks up an unlocked phone. A Face ID/passcode gate via LocalAuthentication is cheap to build and directly reinforces the trust thesis everyone already agreed on; I'd treat it as a near-mandatory addition for the first build even though it wasn't named in the original iOS-native-features list, rather than something to debate away. Second platform point: widgets here have no natural refresh trigger — value only changes when the user manually adds an entry, so the widget timeline needs to reload on local data-write events, not on a timer, or it'll show stale totals and look broken in exactly the way that erodes trust in a "ledger" app.

Assumptions and risks I'd want on record before design starts. One: Vision's built-in classifiers are scene/object classifiers, not fine-grained identifiers — they can plausibly say "this is a wristwatch" or "this is a shoe," they cannot say "this is a 1970s Rolex Submariner ref. 5513" or "this is a first-pressing vs. reissue." If the first build's copy or UX implies anything more specific than broad-category-plus-confidence, real collectors will test it against their hardest item on day one and lose trust immediately. Two: the "authoritative record of insured possessions lives only on this phone" story has a silent failure mode if the SwiftData store isn't confirmed to participate in standard encrypted device/iCloud backups — that's a one-line settings check now versus a devastating support story later, and it should be verified and stated to the user as the actual backup story, not left implicit. Three: the downgrade-preserves-data rule the contract already locked in ("hide, don't delete") still has an unresolved sub-decision — when a user with 60 items drops to the 25-item free cap, which 25 stay visible/editable? That needs a concrete, boring rule now (I'd pick oldest-by-date-added stays active, simplest to reason about and to test) or it'll get improvised inconsistently during implementation. Four, smaller but real: the value-over-time chart has a distinct near-empty state — zero entries and exactly one entry both fail to produce a line chart — and that needs its own designed state, not a fallback to a generic empty view, since "not enough history yet" reads very differently to a user than "you haven't used this feature."

Implication for the first build: keep the category taxonomy a fixed, small enum (watch/vinyl/card/wine/sneaker/other) with a free-text subtype field rather than a dynamic per-category schema — the cross-category promise is the differentiator, but open-ended dynamic fields per category is exactly the kind of scope inflation that turns an MVP into a half-built app. Ship classification as category-only pre-fill with a visible confidence affordance, app-lock as a first-build feature, the downgrade tie-break rule decided now, and the chart's near-empty state designed explicitly — that's a small, concrete list the next phase can act on without having to rediscover any of it.
