On the actual product: the biggest thing I don't trust is the phrase "insurance-grade" and "an actual exportable report insurers/appraisers can use." That's a marketing claim dressed up as a spec line, and if we build toward it literally we'll either scope-creep into legal/compliance territory we can't validate (no insurer has agreed to accept this format) or we'll ship something and call it "insurance-grade" without any basis for that claim. My decision rule: treat the export as a structured PDF containing photos, condition notes, provenance, purchase price, and value history — well-organized and professional — but nothing in the UI or copy should assert that an insurer will actually accept it. That's a claim the product can't back up and it's the kind of thing that erodes trust the first time a user's insurer pushes back.

Second risk: Vision framework classification assist is described as "integral" but the spec's own key-risks section admits it may misidentify niche items — a 1962 dive watch reference number, a first-pressing vinyl vs. a reissue, a graded trading card. Vision's built-in classifiers are good at coarse categories (this is a watch, this is a shoe) and bad at exactly the fine-grained identification that would matter to a serious collector. If the build treats classification as anything beyond "pre-fill a category and maybe a rough label, with an obvious low-confidence affordance and one-tap fallback to full manual entry," it will overpromise and the first niche item a real collector photographs will break trust in the whole feature. Decision rule: classification must never block, delay, or gate the save flow; it's a suggestion with a visible confidence state, and manual override is always one tap away, tested as a first-class path, not an afterthought.

Third, the subscription/free-tier boundary has an unspecified edge case: free tier is capped at 25 items with no valuation tracking; paid unlocks unlimited items and value tracking. What happens when a paid subscriber with 60 items and years of value history lets the subscription lapse? Does the app hard-lock at 25 items and hide the rest, hide value data but keep it recoverable, or something else? The spec doesn't say, and this is exactly the kind of decision that if left ambiguous gets improvised badly during implementation — likely as silent data loss, which for an app whose entire pitch is "trustworthy record-keeping for valuable possessions" would be a serious credibility failure. My rule: never delete user data on downgrade. Items beyond the free cap and value entries become read-only/hidden-behind-paywall but are preserved intact, and re-subscribing restores full access instantly. This needs to be a testable, written-down behavior, not something inferred at build time.

Fourth: the widget for "total collection value" conflicts with the free tier having no valuation tracking at all. A free-tier user's widget has literally nothing to show for the app's headline feature. That's a real state that needs an explicit design, not "we'll figure it out" — probably item count for free, value for paid — and it needs to be one of the tested empty/paid states, not discovered as a bug later.

Fifth, and this one is a genuine data-integrity risk rather than a UX nicety: this is a local-first app with SwiftData and explicitly no cloud sync in v1, holding what the user is told to treat as their authoritative insurance record for expensive physical objects. If the phone is lost, stolen, or the app is deleted, that record is gone. "Future backend roadmap" defers sync, which is fine architecturally, but the MVP still needs some non-cloud safety net — at minimum, the PDF/Files export doubling as a manual backup path, and this should be treated as a production-readiness requirement, not a nice-to-have, given the app's own value proposition is trustworthy record-keeping.

Sixth, photo storage cost is unaddressed: photo-forward cards plus a condition timeline implies multiple photos per item over time, for potentially hundreds of items for paid users. Without a stated compression/thumbnailing strategy, a serious collector's device storage will bloat fast and nobody will notice until app review or a support complaint. This needs a concrete rule: full-res originals get downsampled for storage or explicitly deferred as a known limitation.
