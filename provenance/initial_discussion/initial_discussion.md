### Round 1


**Codex (Product Strategist · User Advocate) — Round 1**

Provenance should be defined much more narrowly than “a collector app.” The product promise I’d lock is: log a valuable physical item in under a minute, then maintain a trustworthy visual record of its condition, provenance, and value over time in one place. That is the thing a spreadsheet does badly and this app can do well. If that sentence gets diluted into “AI-powered valuation” or “insurance platform,” the product gets weaker, not stronger.

The primary user is not a broad “collector.” It is a person with enough money tied up in physical goods that bad record-keeping feels irresponsible, but who still hasn’t found a tool better than Notes or a spreadsheet. Concretely: someone who buys a watch, a few graded cards, a bottle case, or a pair of limited sneakers, means to log it immediately, and usually doesn’t because the process is annoying. The key scenario is right after acquisition, often on a phone, often distracted, often in mediocre lighting. If the app is not better than a spreadsheet in that exact moment, it loses before the later chart, export, and condition history features ever matter.

So the core loop should be brutally simple: capture photo, save item with title and category, then enrich later. Everything else belongs after save. Condition notes, provenance details, purchase facts, and value entries are important, but they should deepen an existing record, not block its creation. The user should feel “my item is safely logged” within the first minute. That is the real product win. Vision assist fits only as silent help on top of that flow, never as a required step and never as something the user has to wait on.

The hard scope boundary I’d set is this: v1 is a documentation ledger, not a valuation intelligence product. That means no pretending to know live market value, no claims of expert identification, no insurer acceptance guarantee, no cross-device sync, no collaborative collections, no dynamic schemas by category, and no marketplace behavior. One fixed set of categories, one local user, one durable item record, one clear export story. That is enough to build a finished app. Anything broader starts to eat the trust and speed that are supposed to differentiate it.

The measurable success criteria should stay user-behavior oriented, not feature-list oriented. A first-time user can create an item with photo, title, and category in under a minute. A saved item can later hold condition history, provenance notes, purchase details, and manual value entries without those concepts feeling buried or ambiguous. A paid user can generate a clear PDF report from local data that reads like a serious record, not a toy export. A free user understands exactly what is limited and never loses data on downgrade. Classification failure never blocks save. Every major screen state is understandable when empty or incomplete, especially the value history state when there is little or no data.

The biggest risk in this direction is that the app may feel too restrained and not “smart” enough to justify payment if value updates are manual and classification is intentionally humble. That is real. But the worse mistake would be compensating with fake sophistication. The first build has to earn trust through speed, clarity, and durable records. If users love it, richer valuation inputs can come later behind the repository boundary. If they do not trust it as a ledger, no amount of later intelligence will save it.

The detail I want preserved into the final output is simple: Provenance only works if capture is faster than a spreadsheet and review is more trustworthy than a spreadsheet. Every scope decision should be judged against that.

**Claude (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

Let me give this a concrete shape before anyone else weighs in, thinking about it as both the person drawing the screens and the person who has to worry about what breaks six months later.

The one-sentence promise I'd lock in: Provenance is a local-first ledger that lets a collector photograph something the moment they get it and end up, over time, with a trustworthy dated record of what it is, what condition it's in, and what it's worth — faster to use than a spreadsheet and more credible to hand someone else than a spreadsheet ever could be. Primary user is the person we already converged on: has 40+ watches, or a wine cellar, or a stack of graded cards, currently tracked in a spreadsheet that's drifted out of sync, who will abandon this app in the first session if item #1 takes more than a minute to create.

Core loop, as I'd actually draw it: capture (photo + title + one of six categories, save immediately, Vision silently pre-fills the category chip in the background without blocking the button) → the item now exists and shows up in the list → enrichment happens whenever the user feels like it, from the detail screen, in three independent tracks that live side by side rather than as one long form: provenance/condition facts (a dated timeline of notes+photos), value entries (a dated ledger that feeds the chart), and purchase facts (price, date, source — entered once, rarely touched again). Review loop: list view for browsing the archive, detail view for trust-building on one object, export for handing the whole thing to someone else. That's the IA in full — home/list, capture sheet, item detail (with condition timeline and value chart as sub-sections, not separate top-level destinations), export preview, paywall, and a settings/app-lock screen. Nothing else needs to be a first-class destination for v1.

Here's the thing I don't think has been said yet and I want on record now because it's an IA decision, not an implementation detail: condition history and value history are two separate timelines that both attach to one item, and the detail screen has to make it visually obvious they're not the same thing — a condition entry ("small scratch on caseback, photo attached, 2026-03-01") is not a value entry ("appraised at $8,200, 2026-03-01"), even though they'll often happen on the same day. If the detail screen collapses these into one generic "activity feed," the record stops being legible, which directly undermines the trust thesis everyone's already agreed is the whole point. I'd design it as two distinct, clearly labeled timeline modules on the same screen, not a merged feed.

Second thing worth raising now: what happens when a user deletes an item. The prior phases locked "never destroy data" as a rule for subscription downgrade and error states, but nobody's addressed intentional user deletion — and for an app whose entire value is "this is the record I'd hand an insurer," an accidental delete of a $9,000 watch's entire provenance history is a catastrophic trust failure, arguably worse than a downgrade hiding it. I think item deletion needs to be a soft delete with a recovery window (a "recently deleted" holding area, purged automatically after some fixed period like 30 days) rather than an immediate hard delete, and that's cheap enough to build now that it shouldn't wait for a v2 nice-to-have list. The boundary has to be explicit too — this is not "trash forever," it auto-purges, so it doesn't become unbounded storage growth.

Third: photos need a defined role, not just a bag of images per item. The list view needs a stable cover photo per item; the condition timeline needs to attach photos to specific dated entries. If the data model treats all photos as one undifferentiated collection, the list view either shows the wrong photo after every condition update or the app has to guess, and guessing is exactly the kind of sloppiness this product can't afford. I'd bake "cover photo vs. timeline-entry photo" into the model now rather than retrofitting it.

Fourth, a smaller one but it'll bite if skipped: capture can get interrupted (phone call, app backgrounded, camera permission denial mid-flow). The scenario to design for explicitly is someone takes the photo, backgrounds the app before tapping save, and comes back later — the draft item shouldn't vanish, and it shouldn't half-save either. I'd rather this either autosaves the draft the instant the photo is taken (title/category still editable, defaulting to "Untitled" + "Other") or holds state reliably in-memory with an obvious resume point — but "silently lost" is not acceptable given the stated bar for production-readiness.

My biggest risk to my own direction: I'm asking for three parallel timelines-plus-a-trash-system on the detail screen of what's supposed to be a fast, minimal-friction app, and there's a real chance this reads as busy or over-engineered rather than "quiet and archival" if the visual hierarchy isn't disciplined. The corrective is that only the cover photo, title, and category need to be visible by default — the timelines and chart can be sections you scroll into, not things fighting for attention on first glance.

Hard scope boundaries I'd hold firm on, consistent with what's already locked: no cloud sync, no live pricing, no multi-user, no per-category dynamic schemas, no search/filter/sort system beyond basic list ordering (nobody's asked for it and it's easy to scope-creep into), no barcode/serial-number lookup, no sharing/collaboration on records, no legal export claims.

Measurable success criteria I'd actually hold this build to: median time from tapping "add item" to a saved item under 60 seconds; every named screen demonstrably reaches all four states (empty/loading/success/error) in testing, not just happy path; zero data loss across delete, downgrade, and force-quit-mid-capture scenarios, verified by test, not assumed; PDF export produces a valid file at 0, 1, and many-items-with-full-history; VoiceOver can complete the full core loop (capture → enrich → export) without a sighted assist. If those don't hold, the app isn't done regardless of how the screens look.
