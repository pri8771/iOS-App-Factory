I want to start from the screen list because I think there's a real trap waiting in this phase: everyone upstream has said "one home screen, not a dashboard," and it would be very easy for a well-meaning design pass to quietly reintroduce a dashboard by giving that one screen too much to look at. Cold launch for a brand-new install goes straight into onboarding, no splash screen worth designing beyond the system launch image — this user doesn't need a logo animation, they need to get to an answer fast. Onboarding is a short sequence, not a form: a single welcome screen stating the promise in one sentence ("Cistern tells you, at a glance, whether you're okay until payday"), then an add-a-paycheck step that's really just the paycheck quick-add sheet presented inline with sensible cadence defaults already selected, then an add-a-bill step that lets you add one or several before continuing, with a visible "skip for now" that's exactly as visually weighted as "continue" — I feel strongly that skip can't look like the lesser option or we've just built a soft version of the confrontation we said we wouldn't build. Then, only after income and bills exist, an optional "want a more accurate number?" balance step with its own equally-weighted skip. Finishing onboarding — whether by completing or skipping the balance step — drops you directly onto the home screen with a real, computed runway already visible. No "you're all set!" congratulations screen in between; the answer itself is the payoff, we don't need a separate celebration screen for it.

The home screen is the one screen this app actually is. At the top, the hero: the plain-language runway sentence, the freshness framing built into that same sentence rather than a separate label, and the balance anchor as a small persistent pill that's tappable from anywhere on this screen — always visible, never scrolled out of view, because we already decided it's the number that decays fastest and burying it behind a menu would contradict that. Below the hero, I want the ambient fill/reservoir visual living as a background or supporting graphic behind or around the hero text, not a separate module competing for attention — it should read as atmosphere, like the color of the sky, not as a second data visualization the user has to interpret. Below that, and this is the part I'd watch most carefully, a compact summary — not a scrolling ledger — of what's coming next: something like "2 bills before your next paycheck" as a single tappable row that expands into the secondary bills-and-paychecks list screen, rather than the home screen itself growing a list. The moment that list starts rendering five or six rows inline on the home screen, we've rebuilt the dashboard everyone agreed to avoid. Two floating or docked quick-add entry points — add bill, add paycheck — live at the bottom of this screen, reachable without scrolling.

Below home, secondary and modal: the bills-and-paychecks management screen (a simple grouped list, tap to edit, swipe or button to soft-delete with the undo toast), the three quick-add sheets (paycheck, bill, balance-update) which double as edit sheets when opened on an existing item, a settings screen that I'd genuinely keep to almost nothing — appearance override if we don't just trust system dark mode, the optional app-lock toggle if that should-have gets built, an about/version row, and an entry point to the Premium stub screen, which is its own simple static screen listing the three paid capabilities with no purchase button that does anything. And one non-screen but essential piece of the inventory: the privacy cover view that swaps in over the home screen's real content the instant the scene goes inactive or background, so the app-switcher snapshot never shows the actual sentence — I'd design this as a branded, calm placeholder (the fill motif alone, no text) rather than a blank white screen, so it still feels intentional rather than like a glitch.

On states, per screen: home has to render five distinct conditions and they need to look and read differently, not just swap a number — zero-data (no bills, no paychecks: an inviting empty state, "Add your first paycheck to get a real answer," with the quick-add button for paycheck emphasized over bill since income is the more foundational entry); bills-only (bills exist, no paycheck scheduled: the sentence explicitly says no income is assumed rather than silently computing against a phantom payday); fresh-and-computed (the normal case); stale (crossing whatever threshold we set, the sentence's own phrasing shifts — "as of what you told me 9 days ago" — plus the ambient fill visual desaturates or dims slightly, so staleness is felt in the atmosphere of the screen, not just read in a sentence someone might skim past); and a brief recompute transition state, which should never be a spinner — it should be an immediate, springy re-layout of the existing sentence and fill level, because a spinner on a local, instant operation would itself read as a small lie about how this app works. Quick-add sheets need their own states: empty/default (cadence pre-filled with a sane guess), a validation-error state for zero/negative entries that's inline and specific rather than a system alert, and a soft-confirmation state for implausibly large amounts that reads as a friendly double-check, not a warning. The bills-and-paychecks list has its own empty state distinct from home's (e.g., "no bills yet" if someone deletes everything after having some), a populated state, and the soft-delete-with-undo-toast state layered on top of it. I'd also flag one state nobody's designed yet: what home looks like if the SwiftData store itself fails to load on launch — rare, but it needs a real, calm error screen ("something went wrong opening your data" with a retry) rather than a crash or a silently empty home screen that a scared user might mistake for a real zero-balance answer.

Visual direction: I want to actively steer away from every finance-app instinct toward red/green traffic-light color coding, because that's exactly the shame-gauge this whole product exists to not be. The fill/reservoir motif should use a warm, low-contrast gradient — something closer to dusk light on water than a status indicator — where the "okay" state is simply fuller and warmer and the "tight" state is lower and cooler, a continuous feeling rather than a binary pass/fail color swap. Typography needs a large, humane, highly legible face for the hero sentence — this is the one place in the whole app where type size and weight are doing the emotional work, so it needs to survive every Dynamic Type size without truncating or breaking into an awkward wrap; I'd design the sentence to gracefully reflow to two or three short lines at the largest accessibility sizes rather than shrinking or clipping. Supporting numerals (the balance pill, the bill amounts) can sit in a calmer, smaller, more neutral weight so they don't compete with the sentence for the eye.

Accessibility notes I'd insist survive into the final spec: the hero sentence must be exposed to VoiceOver as one coherent accessibility element with one label, not fragmented into separately-read text runs by styling views, and when a recompute happens after a quick-add, that change should post an accessibility announcement, not rely on a sighted user noticing the springy re-layout — otherwise a VoiceOver user gets no confirmation their edit actually did anything. Every tappable target — the balance pill, the two quick-add buttons, the bill/paycheck rows, the undo-toast action — needs a real 44pt hit area even where the visual glyph is smaller. Contrast on the ambient fill gradient has to be checked as an actual overlay condition, not just checked as a static swatch, because text sitting on top of a gradient that shifts by state is exactly the kind of thing that quietly fails WCAG AA in the "stale" or "tight" variant even if the "fresh" variant passes.

The biggest risk to my own proposal is scope creep in the wrong direction — that "compact summary row" on home is a real design tightrope, and if whoever builds this defaults to a scrollable list because it's easier to implement than a true single-line summary, we've quietly rebuilt the dashboard. I'd rather this phase commit explicitly to home rendering at most one hero, one fill visual, one balance pill, and one single-line "what's coming" summary row — nothing else — and push every additional piece of information behind that one tap into the secondary list screen, even if it's tempting to surface "just one more thing" on the primary screen later.
