**Claude (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

Reading back through the prior three phases, there's a gap nobody has actually named yet, and I think it's the single most consequential open design question for this app: every discussion so far has been about paycheck cadence and recurring bills, but nobody has said how the runway math gets its starting point. A runway/cash-flow projection is anchor-balance plus scheduled deltas over time — and the anchor is "how much money do you actually have right now." That number can only come from the user typing it in, since we've locked out bank linking. And typing in your real current balance is arguably the single most confrontational moment in the entire app for this exact persona — it's the literal thing they're avoiding when they avoid their banking app. If onboarding just drops a "Current Balance: $____" field on screen one, we've recreated the exact dread this app exists to route around, just with nicer copy.

So here's the core loop I'd actually commit to: open app, see one plain-language answer to "am I okay" (a runway date or day-count, not a dashboard), see how fresh that answer is, and if it needs updating, update it in one or two taps and get a new answer immediately. The whole session should be closeable in under thirty seconds for a user who's already current, and under two minutes for a user setting up from scratch. Everything else — bill lists, income schedule, settings — is architecture in service of that one screen, not co-equal destinations. I'd resist any temptation to make this a tab-bar app with five sections of equal visual weight; a tab bar says "there's a system here to manage," which is exactly the posture YNAB has and exactly what the research phase said to avoid. One primary screen, quick-add as sheets/modals that dismiss back to the answer, is the right shape.

On the anchor-balance problem specifically: I think the honest resolution is to let a user get a provisional answer without ever facing a scary "type in your real balance" field on first launch — start them at zero, let them add income and bills first (that's lower-stakes, almost administrative), and only ask for a starting balance as the last, clearly-optional step, framed as "want a more accurate number? tell me what's in your account today" rather than as a required gate. If they skip it, the app should show a runway number that's explicitly framed as relative-to-zero rather than pretend it's precise. And critically, the "current balance" needs to be a first-class, always-visible, one-tap-to-update affordance on the home screen forever after — not a one-time onboarding field — because that number decays the moment they buy coffee, faster than any bill or paycheck data goes stale. If we don't design for continuous cheap re-anchoring, the staleness problem the research phase flagged doesn't just apply to bills, it applies doubly to the number the whole projection is anchored on.

The other thing I want on the record before design gets going: the name Cistern is a gift here and I don't want it wasted as pure branding — a cistern is a reserve you draw down and refill, which is literally the runway concept, and it gives us a legitimate alternative to the two failure-mode visual languages this category always falls into: the red/green traffic-light gauge (shaming, binary, exactly what we're told to avoid) and the bare spreadsheet number (cold, YNAB-adjacent). A water-level or fill-level motif as ambient visual language — not as the primary information carrier, the primary carrier should stay a precise plain-language sentence and number, because someone anxious about money wants a clear answer, not a metaphor to interpret — but as the supporting color/motion system, gives the design phase somewhere real and distinctive to go instead of default finance-app blue-and-green.

Scope boundaries I'd lock now so later phases don't quietly re-litigate them: single account/single balance, no multi-account or credit-card tracking; no transaction-level ledger or categorization — this is cash-flow-forward, not spend-tracking backward; no bank linking, no notifications this pass (both already decided); CSV import is explicitly optional-and-likely-cut, not core loop; single currency, locale-formatted; no debt payoff planning or category budgeting. The one thing I'd add as a non-goal that hasn't been said explicitly yet: no requirement to reconcile the projected balance against reality — we are not trying to be accurate to the penny, we're trying to answer "roughly how many days of cushion do I have," and the design and copy should say so, because implying penny-accuracy is a promise this manual-entry architecture can't keep and will erode trust the first time it's wrong.

For measurable success criteria I'd propose: a first-time user reaches a runway answer (even a provisional zero-anchored one) within two minutes of first launch without hitting a dead end or forced field; adding a recurring bill or paycheck takes three taps or fewer from the home screen; updating the balance anchor takes one tap plus a number entry, from anywhere in the app; the runway number visibly reflects a manual balance update instantly (no save-and-wait); and data survives force-quit and relaunch with no reset. Those are the things I'd want build verification to actually check, not just "does it build."
