**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Coming at this fresh: the audience here isn't "budget-conscious" in the aspirational sense — it's someone for whom checking their balance is an anxiety event, which means the use context is bursty and avoidant, not the daily-check-in habit that Mint/YNAB/Copilot Money assume. They'll open this app in a moment of dread ("can I afford this") rather than as a morning ritual, and sessions will be short. That has a real design implication I want on record before we get to comparables: if the core interaction requires sustained attention or a learning curve, this persona bounces. YNAB in particular is the cautionary tale — genuinely good product, but its entire onboarding is "assign every dollar a job," and that's exactly the kind of confrontation this user avoids. PocketGuard's "In My Pocket" number and Simplifi's "left to spend" are the closer pattern match: one glanceable number that answers "am I okay" without a spreadsheet. That's the shape I think Cistern's core screen should copy — not the budgeting-category grind, just the single runway number, cash-flow-forward rather than category-backward.

The thing every one of those comparables has that we're explicitly not building is bank sync. That's fine as a contract decision, but I want to name the real cost of it, not just wave it away: those apps' core value prop is "we did the work of knowing your numbers so you don't have to." Ours is the opposite — the user has to manually enter every bill and every paycheck, and if they stop (which, per the persona, they will, because entering financial data is itself a form of the confrontation they avoid), the runway number becomes stale and silently wrong. For most apps stale data is an annoyance; for this app, a wrong "you're fine" number to a financially strained user is the worst possible failure mode, worse than the app not existing. That's my top risk, and it should shape the build, not just the QA checklist: quick-add flows, recurring templates, sane defaults, and probably some visible signal when data is getting stale ("last updated 9 days ago" rather than presenting a stale projection with false confidence) need to be treated as core-loop work, not settings-page polish.

Second risk, and it's a correctness one the contract already flagged but I want to sharpen: paycheck-to-paycheck does not mean fixed-cadence salaried income. A meaningful chunk of this audience has hourly, tipped, gig, or overtime-variable income — the "biweekly $X" model that most cash-flow tools assume will be wrong for them on day one. We should either explicitly support an estimated/range income entry, or explicitly scope out variable income and say so, because silently assuming fixed recurring amounts for a persona that's disproportionately gig/hourly is the kind of gap that makes the app feel like it wasn't built for its own target user.

Third risk is tonal but has to be tested as a real failure mode, not assumed away by good intentions: any notification, reminder, or low-balance alert is one bad copy decision away from being exactly the shaming red-alert experience this app exists to not be. My instinct for the MVP is to either skip proactive notifications entirely in v1, or keep them opt-in and neutrally worded, because we don't have time this pass to tune that copy carefully, and a single "You're about to overdraft!" push notification undoes the entire tonal premise.

On platform opportunity, the one thing I'd fight to keep in scope even under MVP pressure is a home screen widget showing the runway number or days-until-tight. This persona's whole behavior pattern is avoiding opening the app — a widget is the one native iOS capability that actually serves that avoidance instead of fighting it, glanceable without the confrontation of launching the app. Siri Shortcuts ("what's my balance") is a nice-to-have in the same spirit but I'd rank it below the widget. Both should at least not be architecturally blocked even if we don't ship them this pass; I'd rather cut a settings screen than cut widget-readiness from the data layer.

One more thing worth naming honestly, because I think it's a real hole in the premise, not just a risk to mitigate: "avoids their banking app" could mean they avoid the friction/UX of banking apps, or it could mean they avoid facing the numbers at all — and if it's the latter, an app that requires them to manually type in every bill is asking them to do the exact confrontation they're avoiding, just in a nicer font. I don't think that kills the idea, but it means the manual-entry moment itself has to be the calmest, lowest-friction part of the whole app, because that's the actual point of avoidance-friction, not the balance screen.

For what the first build needs to nail, in priority order: the cash-flow math has to be correct under the edge cases already flagged (irregular cadence, month-end, no-income-scheduled) before anything else, because a confidently wrong number is worse than no app; manual entry has to be fast enough that staleness doesn't set in within a week; and a widget (or at minimum a widget-ready data layer) should be treated as near-core rather than a stretch goal, since it's the single highest-leverage native capability for this specific persona. Notifications I'd actually recommend cutting from this pass entirely rather than shipping half-tuned — better a quiet app than a wrong-toned one.
