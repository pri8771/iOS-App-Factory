## Category

Acoustic-aware focus scheduling

## One-Sentence Promise

Learns how your real environments actually sound and negotiates your daily focus blocks around them instead of just measuring decibels once.

## Target User

Remote workers, students, and podcasters in shared or noise-variable spaces (open-plan homes, shared offices, apartments) who need predictable deep-work time.

## Painful Moment Solved

Noise and interruptions wreck long focus sessions, and there's no way to know in advance which hours in which room are actually going to be quiet enough — so people either guess, or get burned mid-session by the espresso machine or a neighbor's call.

## Why It Is Different

Not a one-shot decibel meter: on-device spectral analysis and local pattern clustering build a per-room acoustic profile over repeated short sessions, and the app's daily verb is a scheduling negotiation — it proposes and defends focus blocks against your calendar using what it's learned about when a room is actually quiet, so the value compounds with use instead of being a single diagnostic you run once and forget.

## Why Users Would Pay

Saves real deep-work hours and reduces interruption-recovery cost; subscription unlocks unlimited room profiles, calendar-aware scheduling intelligence, and multi-person/team readiness views for shared spaces.

## Subscription Model

Free tier: one room profile, manual session logging, basic score. Paid: unlimited room profiles, calendar-integrated scheduling recommendations, team/shared-space readiness analytics, historical trend export.

## Local-First MVP Scope

SwiftData models for rooms, sessions, detected noise-pattern clusters, and calendar-linked focus blocks; spectral feature extraction and clustering run fully on-device against locally captured short audio sessions, discarding raw audio after feature extraction.

## Future Backend Roadmap

CloudKit sync across a person's devices; opt-in shared household/team room profiles; policy templates for shared offices — additive, no rewrite needed.

## AR / Local ML / Local LLM Fit

On-device FFT-based spectral feature extraction plus local CoreML clustering for recurring noise-pattern detection — no cloud audio processing, no LLM required.

## Design Direction

Muted steel-and-aurora palette, waveform-to-heatmap card transitions, strict micro-typography scale, calm spring motion — restrained rather than clinical.

**Codex (Product Strategist · User Advocate) — Round 1**

The one-sentence promise is: QuietPilot uses repeated, manual acoustic samples to predict the quietest realistic focus windows per room and then proposes the strongest calendar-friendly focus block with visible confidence and reasoning.

The primary user is a remote worker, student, or podcaster in a shared or noisy-variable space—think an apartment, open plan office, or shared studio—who already schedules deep work in a calendar and wants to avoid getting derailed mid-session by predictable noise patterns like espresso machines, neighbor calls, or shared-room activity.

The core loop is: start a short capture with explicit permission-aware tap, label context (room + activity), extract only spectral features (not raw audio), update per-room noise clusters and confidence, show a current room readiness score for candidate windows, propose one focus block against real calendar availability, let the user accept/reject/shift, and continuously adjust profiles from new captures and outcomes.

The hard scope boundaries are: one local-first app only; explicit start/stop capture only (never background listening); no cloud audio processing; SwiftData models for rooms/sessions/clusters/focus blocks; no team/multi-person analytics in v1; no CloudKit sync in this build; no silent calendar writes, only read + proposal suggestions through EventKit-style integration; and no fake scheduler if confidence is low—downgrade to an explicitly labeled diagnostic experience instead. Free/premium behavior must be enforced in code as part of scope: one room on free, unlimited on paid for scheduling-intelligence features.

The biggest risk is not technical but behavioral: users will stop capturing if the flow is slow, so repeated sessions may never converge and all recommendations become noise. The objection that matters most is “this sounds like a decibel meter with extra pages”; the only way to prevent that is to keep capture friction almost zero and keep uncertainty front-and-center in every meaningful recommendation.

Success has to be measurable by direct behavior: within a 14-day usage window, at least 3–5 sessions per room should reduce prediction uncertainty and produce a calendar proposal with reasoning; users should be able to perform the app’s full workflow without raw audio storage, without crashes, and with explicit states for empty/loading/error; accepted proposals should show a higher observed interruption-free rate than user-selected no-recommendation blocks, and failed/conflicted scenarios must degrade to a clear diagnostic explanation rather than a fake confident suggestion.

**Claude (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

My read on the product promise, stated as tight as I can make it: QuietPilot turns repeated short room recordings into a per-room quiet-time profile with an honest confidence level, and uses that profile plus the user's real calendar to propose one focus block at a time that it can actually defend — not a decibel reading, not a full day-replanner, one negotiated window with visible reasoning behind it.

For primary user and scenario, I want to anchor on something concrete rather than the abstract "remote workers/students/podcasters" list, because a design has to be built for a moment, not a demographic. The scenario I'd design against: someone working from a one-bedroom apartment or a shared house, sitting down around 9am with three or four things they need uninterrupted focus for today, who opens QuietPilot not to check "how loud is it right now" but to ask "when today can I actually trust this room to stay quiet, and can I get that locked onto my calendar before someone books over it." That's a planning-ahead act, not a diagnostic act, and the IA has to reflect that — the home screen's job is to answer "what's my best window today" at a glance, not to be a live meter.

On the core loop, I think there's a real tension in the spec that needs to be named and resolved rather than papered over: the spec calls scheduling negotiation "the app's daily verb," but negotiation has nothing to negotiate with until a room profile has enough sessions to be trustworthy, and no one is going to re-negotiate their calendar five times a day. So I'd design this as two loops running at different cadences, not one loop pretending to be both. The fast loop, which genuinely is daily and sometimes multiple-times-daily, is capture → instant feedback ("logged, here's how this compares to past sessions in this room") — that loop needs Forest/Flora-level satisfaction because it's the thing that has to happen constantly for any of the rest of the product to have data to work with. The slower value loop is: once a room has enough sessions to clear a confidence floor, the app proposes a block against the real calendar with visible reasoning, the user accepts/rejects/shifts it, and later the user (explicitly or implicitly through a follow-up capture) confirms whether that block actually held up, which folds back into the profile. If a later phase collapses these into one screen that tries to always show "your negotiated block" even when there's no data to support one, or always demands a full negotiation interaction on every app open, it'll feel exhausting and dishonest at the same time. That distinction — fast capture loop feeds a slower, gated negotiation loop — is the single most important thing I want preserved out of this phase, because it's easy to lose in a one-sentence "core loop" summary.

Hard scope boundaries I'd lock now, mostly to keep this buildable in one pass and consistent with what's already been decided upstream: the negotiation flow proposes one block at a time for a stated target duration — not a full-day replan, not multi-block optimization, not a general calendar assistant. No automatic writes to the calendar, ever, without an explicit confirm tap. No passive or background listening under any circumstance. Free tier caps at exactly one room, and that cap has to be an actual disabled/blocked creation path in the UI, not a label next to an unlimited-in-practice feature. No notification spam beyond the one negotiated-window notification per proposal cycle. And critically, given everything already established about cold-start and thin data: below whatever minimum-sample threshold gets set in architecture, the app is not allowed to silently show a confident-looking score or a negotiated block — it has to visibly say "not enough sessions yet" rather than dress up a guess as an answer. That's a scope boundary as much as a UX rule, because it prevents a later phase from quietly faking confidence to make a demo look better.

For measurable success criteria, I want things a developer or QA pass could actually check rather than vibes: a capture session must be startable in three taps or fewer from the home screen (and from the widget/App Intent, one tap); a room with at least six sessions spanning at least two different times of day must return a numeric readiness score plus an explicit confidence label for any queried window, computed from real clustering output rather than a static default; the negotiation flow, given a calendar with at least one open slot overlapping a quiet-scored window, must produce a proposal with a reasoning string that references actual session history (not boilerplate text), and must support at least one reject-then-re-propose cycle that doesn't just re-offer the identical rejected slot; the free-tier room cap must be enforced by blocking creation of a second room in the UI itself; and after any capture session completes, there must be zero raw audio bytes left in the app's sandbox, checkable by inspecting the filesystem post-session.

The biggest risk to my own framing is the two-cadence loop — it's the right model of how this product actually works, but it's genuinely harder to design well than a single unified daily loop would be, because the home screen has to gracefully hold "you can capture right now" and "here's your negotiated block, if you have one, and if not here's why" without those two things fighting for attention or making the screen feel like two apps stitched together. I think that's a solvable design problem, but I want it on record that I'm choosing added IA complexity over a simpler-but-dishonest single loop, and that trade needs to survive into the design phase rather than get "simplified" back into one generic daily card.
