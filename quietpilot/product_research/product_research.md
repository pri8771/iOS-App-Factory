## App Name

QuietPilot

## Category

Acoustic-aware focus scheduling

## One-Sentence Promise

Learns how your real environments actually sound and negotiates your daily focus blocks around them instead of just measuring decibels once.

## Target User

Remote workers, students, and podcasters in shared or noise-variable spaces (open-plan homes, shared offices, apartments) who need predictable deep-work time.

## Painful Moment Solved

Noise and interruptions wreck long focus sessions, and there's no way to know in advance which hours in which room are actually going to be quiet enough — so people either guess, or get burned mid-session by the espresso machine or a neighbor's call.

## Why It Is Different

Not a one-shot decibel meter: on-device spectral analysis and local pattern clustering build a per-room acoustic profile over repeated short sessions, and the app's daily verb is a scheduling negotiation — it proposes and defends focus blocks against your calendar using what it's learned about when a room is actually quiet, so the value compounds with use instead of being a single diagnostic you run once and forget.

## Why Users Would Pay

Saves real deep-work hours and reduces interruption-recovery cost; subscription unlocks unlimited room profiles, calendar-aware scheduling intelligence, and multi-person/team readiness views for shared spaces.

## Subscription Model

Free tier: one room profile, manual session logging, basic score. Paid: unlimited room profiles, calendar-integrated scheduling recommendations, team/shared-space readiness analytics, historical trend export.

## Local-First MVP Scope

SwiftData models for rooms, sessions, detected noise-pattern clusters, and calendar-linked focus blocks; spectral feature extraction and clustering run fully on-device against locally captured short audio sessions, discarding raw audio after feature extraction.

## Future Backend Roadmap

CloudKit sync across a person's devices; opt-in shared household/team room profiles; policy templates for shared offices — additive, no rewrite needed.

## AR / Local ML / Local LLM Fit

On-device FFT-based spectral feature extraction plus local CoreML clustering for recurring noise-pattern detection — no cloud audio processing, no LLM required.

## Design Direction

Muted steel-and-aurora palette, waveform-to-heatmap card transitions, strict micro-typography scale, calm spring motion — restrained rather than clinical.

## App Store Positioning

The focus app that learns how your spaces actually sound and negotiates your schedule around them, instead of a decibel meter you check once and forget.

## Invalidation Criteria

If the scheduling-negotiation loop can't be made genuinely useful against real calendars, narrow v1 to room-profile-and-score only and market it honestly as a diagnostic tool rather than a scheduler, rather than overclaiming a weak scheduling feature.

## v1 Build Scope

Manual session capture, on-device spectral clustering for up to 3 room profiles, readiness scoring, one calendar-aware focus-block proposal flow, trend timeline, local export — no team/multi-person analytics in v1.

## Core Workflows

- Start/stop a short acoustic capture session with automatic metadata (time, room, activity type)
- Build and refine a room's noise-pattern profile from repeated sessions
- Get a room readiness score for a given time window
- Get and negotiate a proposed focus-block schedule against the calendar
- Review trends and compare before/after a room change (e.g., moved desks, added a rug)

- BGAppRefreshTask for nightly profile recomputation
- Home/Lock Screen widget for current room score and next proposed focus window
- App Intents for starting/stopping a session from Shortcuts or Siri
- Haptic feedback for threshold crossings
- Local notifications for negotiated quiet windows

- Repeat-use value depends on the scheduling-negotiation loop actually working, not just the initial room diagnostic — if that loop is weak the app becomes a one-time novelty
- Environmental variability (a different noise source each day) can undercut confidence in the profile and needs honest uncertainty framing, not false precision

### Round 1


**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

The best direction is to treat QuietPilot as an “uncertainty-aware planning assistant,” not a loudness monitor. People don’t hire this app to erase sound; they hire it to regain trust in their day planning. If it can repeatedly tell them “this room is likely quiet enough at this time window with X% confidence” and back that with a calendar-aware block they can accept/shift, it earns a permanent habit. If it can’t, it must stop pretending and become a high-quality room diagnostic tool. That keeps scope honest and protects credibility.

The likely market is narrower but valuable: remote workers in apartments/households, students in shared dorms, and creators who alternate between spaces, all of whom already have recurring disruption pain but usually do no predictive analysis. This is not for people in quiet private offices or acoustic professionals; it’s for people who make do and need planning under uncertainty. The strongest hook is “predictable deep work windows” rather than “quiet measurement.”

The audience behavior likely looks like: very repetitive context switching, short bursts of sessions before/after known nuisance events, low tolerance for extra mental overhead, and high sensitivity to “surveillance vibes.” That means the product’s UX must feel deliberate and calm and force explicitness on capture, not automation by default.

Competitive patterns to learn from without copying:
- Calendar-native planners and focus/Do Not Disturb style systems, because scheduling friction and explainability matter.
- Sleep/noise apps, but those usually optimize long-running passive monitoring, which is a bad fit and a trust risk here.
- Energy/fitness journaling patterns, where repeated lightweight logs compound into recommendations.
- Health apps’ “confidence + trend + action” presentation style, because uncertainty labeling is a UX requirement, not a technical afterthought.

The competitor landscape is easiest to dominate by combining local acoustic inference + calendar negotiation. A plain decibel app is weak because it ends at one metric; a generic planner is weak because it ignores acoustic ground truth. QuietPilot’s moat is the coupling layer: schedule reasoning tied to learned room profiles and explicit uncertainty.

Native iOS opportunities are strong and should shape the first build:
- EventKit integration is the credibility centerpiece. If calendar-aware negotiation is built around real events, users can feel the app is truly useful.
- Widgets are the perfect “at-a-glance contract”: current room score and next candidate focus block.
- App Intents are not just a feature checkbox; they are a low-friction path for users to run short captures during transitions.
- BGAppRefreshTask should be framed as nightly model refresh, but with graceful UX if not run soon.
- Haptics and Notification timing should feel like permissioned nudges, not ambient noise alerts.
- Shortcuts and focus modes can be touched lightly via settings deep links, but avoid overreach until core loop is stable.

Design-wise, the first-build IA should be tiny and legible: Home as “what should I do next,” one explicit Capture flow, one Profile/Rollup flow, one Negotiation flow, one Trends/Export flow, plus Settings/Privacy/Gating. Empty/loading/error states must be explicit and differentiated, because failures (no permission, no sessions, weak confidence) are core to trust.

Three major unknowns/risks to label immediately:
1) Whether the spectral clustering signal is stable enough across real rooms to support genuinely useful proposals after a few sessions.
2) Whether users will sustain short capture sessions; if not, all downstream predictions degrade and can look dishonest.
3) Whether recurrent calendar conflicts (recurring meetings, timezone edges, timezone changes, shared calendars) make proposals seem flaky, which could kill repeat use.

A key objection to the direction is that explicit capture can feel burdensome over time, so the first build should bias toward ultra-fast sessions (single-tap start/stop, automatic context capture, instant feedback, immediate trend updates) and clear “why this mattered” text. If friction spikes, retention dies and the product becomes a novelty.

The output for this phase should preserve: the “predictability under uncertainty” positioning, the assumption that EventKit is real calendar input, the privacy boundary as product law, the uncertainty-first UX doctrine, and the explicit fallback-to-diagnostic rule if confidence is weak. Without that, later phases will drift toward a fake scheduler and lose the core product promise.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Thinking about who actually opens this app at 7am on a Tuesday: someone working from a shared apartment or open-plan home, maybe with a partner on calls, roommates, a espresso machine, or a studio apartment doubling as a podcast booth. They've almost certainly already tried the cheap fixes — noise-cancelling headphones, a white noise app, Do Not Disturb — and those didn't fix the actual problem, which is "I don't know in advance if 2-4pm in the living room is going to be quiet." That's a person who's already calendar-literate and probably already blocks focus time manually and watches it get blown up by a delivery or a call. The realistic target isn't "everyone with noise," it's people who've already tried to protect focus time on their calendar and keep losing that fight to their physical space, not just to other people's meeting requests.

On comparable patterns, I'd bucket the competitive landscape into four things this app has to be read against, because each one sets a user expectation QuietPilot will be compared to whether we like it or not. Decibel-meter apps (Decibel X, SoundMeter and the like) set the expectation of "point phone, get instant number" — QuietPilot's manual multi-session capture model is slower than that by design, and if the app doesn't clearly communicate why the slowness is worth it (it's building a profile, not just measuring right now), it reads as a worse decibel meter, not a smarter one. White-noise/masking apps (Noisli, myNoise, Endel) train users to think "solve noise by covering it," which is the opposite posture from QuietPilot's "solve noise by scheduling around it" — that's a real positioning asset if we lean into it, but it also means users' first instinct when annoyed by noise will be to reach for a masking app instead, not a scheduler, so the app has to earn its way into that reflex. AI calendar/time-blocking tools (Reclaim.ai, Motion, Clockwise) already do calendar negotiation — propose blocks, defend them, re-propose on conflict — so users who've touched those tools will have a real bar for what "negotiate my schedule" means, and a QuietPilot negotiation flow that's weaker than a plain time-blocking app's negotiation (e.g. no real defend/re-propose loop) will feel like a regression, not an innovation. And focus/pomodoro timers (Forest, Flora, Session) set the bar for what a "session" feels like — fast to start, satisfying to complete — which is the UX bar the acoustic capture session has to clear, since it's structurally similar (short timed activity, repeated daily) but currently has no reward loop of its own.

The platform opportunities I'd flag without expanding scope beyond what's already locked in the contract: the widget and App Intents requirements are already there, but the highest-leverage native touch is making the capture session itself effectively one-tap from as many entry points as possible — widget tap, Shortcut, lock screen — because the entire value proposition depends on repeated short sessions actually happening, and every bit of friction between "I want to log a session" and "session is logging" directly threatens data volume. I'd also flag Live Activities and Dynamic Island for an in-progress capture or an active negotiated focus block as a genuinely good fit for this app's daily verb, but that's not in the locked v1 iOS feature list, so I'm naming it as a candidate for the design/architecture phase to explicitly accept or reject — not assuming it in.

Now the risks, and these are the ones I think actually threaten the product working at all, not just polish gaps. First, cold start: the core promise depends on "repeated short sessions" building a profile, which means a brand-new user's very first experience with every room is going to be a low-confidence or no-data state, and if that state isn't designed as carefully as the "it's working" state, the app reads as broken or empty for however many days it takes to get enough sessions — this needs an honest, good-looking "building your profile" state from session one, not just a spinner or a bare "not enough data yet" label. Second, statistically thin clustering: on-device clustering with realistically 3-10 sessions per room in the first couple weeks is a small-N problem — whatever clustering approach gets picked needs an explicit minimum-sample threshold before it claims distinct pattern buckets, and below that threshold the app should say so plainly rather than confidently displaying clusters built on four data points. Third, calendar edge cases: a denied EventKit permission, a completely booked calendar, or a calendar with zero free slots in the requested window are not edge cases here, they're going to be common, and each one needs a real, distinct, honest UI response — "we can't see your calendar, here's your room score anyway," or "your calendar has no open windows today," not a silent failure or a block proposed on top of an existing event. Fourth, capture friction is the single biggest threat to the compounding-value thesis: since sessions are manual and short, if starting one takes more than a couple taps or more than a few seconds of setup, users will stop doing it after day one or two, which starves the whole profile-building loop before it can prove its value — this is a QA-testable thing later ("how many taps from home screen to a logged session") not just a vibe. Fifth, mic permission denial needs a real non-crashing, non-dead-end state, since "explicit start/stop" as a trust requirement is worthless if hitting start with mic access off just does nothing or errors silently. Sixth, background refresh is unverifiable in a dev/simulator setting almost by definition — the build needs a manual "recompute now" action independent of BGAppRefreshTask so the recompute logic itself is actually exercisable and demoable, with the background scheduling treated as best-effort and named as such rather than something we can prove fires nightly. Seventh, similar-sounding rooms: if someone profiles two genuinely quiet rooms, the clustering might not differentiate them much, which is fine for v1 as long as each room still gets its own independently computed score rather than literally sharing one number — that's a thing worth checking once building starts, not a blocker now.

For the first build, the implication I'd push hardest is that the cold-start and low-confidence states are not secondary UI work — they're the majority of what a new user will actually see for the first week or two, so they need the same design and engineering investment as the "fully profiled room" happy path, not a bolt-on empty state. Second, the negotiation flow's visible reasoning ("proposing 2-4pm because your last 6 sessions here were quiet on weekday afternoons") should be treated as a first-class, testable UI requirement, not commentary text, because that's literally what separates this from a generic calendar-suggestion feature — if a later phase can strip that out under time pressure and the flow still "technically negotiates," that's exactly the kind of silent downgrade the contract is trying to prevent. Third, whatever clustering algorithm gets chosen needs a named, documented minimum-sample floor before it's allowed to assert confident pattern buckets — that threshold should be decided in the architecture phase, not discovered by accident during QA. And fourth, a manual recompute trigger for the profile logic should be built in regardless of BGAppRefreshTask's presence, both because it makes the feature demoable and because it gives users agency after something changes in their room (moved the desk, added a rug) instead of waiting on an unpredictable nightly job.
