Now the skeptical read. My job in this phase is to make sure the contract this room writes down can't quietly drift into building something easier and prettier than what was actually asked for, because this spec has several places where that drift is the path of least resistance.

The single biggest risk I see is that "scheduling negotiation" is the entire differentiator and also the single hardest thing in the spec, and it's exactly the kind of feature that gets quietly downgraded to "here's a calendar view with some free slots highlighted." The spec itself flags this with its own invalidation criteria — if the negotiation loop can't be made genuinely useful, the fallback is to honestly ship a room-diagnostic tool and market it as such, not to fake a scheduler. That invalidation clause has to survive into the contract as an explicit, checkable decision rule, not get lost. A later phase needs a concrete test for "is the negotiation loop genuinely useful" — something like: given a room's learned noise profile and a calendar with existing events, the app proposes at least one focus block, shows the reasoning (why this window, based on what history), lets the user accept/reject/shift it, and re-proposes on rejection. If the build can't clear that bar with real logic (not a hardcoded "9-11am is usually good" stub), the contract should require falling back to the diagnostic-only framing rather than shipping a scheduler that always outputs the same trivial answer.

Second risk: "on-device spectral analysis and local pattern clustering" is asserted as a differentiator, but a from-scratch build in one MVP pass could easily degenerate into "record decibel level over time and average it" while still calling it a noise-pattern cluster. That would technically satisfy "SwiftData models exist" while failing the actual promise. The contract should require that the feature-extraction step produce something more than a single scalar (e.g., a few band-energy or spectral-centroid-style features per short window, not just RMS loudness) and that the clustering step group sessions into more than one bucket per room when the data supports it — otherwise it's a decibel meter with extra tables, which is precisely what the spec says not to build.

Third, microphone handling is a hard trust boundary, not a nice-to-have. The spec explicitly calls out the surveillance-creepiness risk.
