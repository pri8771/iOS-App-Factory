**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

The product promise should be tighter than the current prompt: Interval is a focus timer that recommends the next best focus block and break based on how you actually perform, not on a fixed preset. That wording matters because “learns your patterns” is too vague unless the user feels the adaptation in the very next recommendation.

The primary user is someone who already wants to focus and has probably tried Pomodoro or other timers, but keeps bouncing off fixed intervals because their real capacity changes by time of day, workload, or mental state. The core scenario is simple: they sit down to work, want the app to tell them how long to focus right now, run the session, quickly log how it went, and then trust the next recommendation a little more because the app can explain it. If the app requires them to manage tasks, build routines, or spend time interpreting charts before they can start, the UX has already failed.

The core loop should stay brutally narrow. Open app, see today’s recommended session and why, start focus block, survive lock/background cleanly, finish or stop early, log a tiny reflection, get an updated recommendation for the next block or break. That is the app. The information architecture should reflect that: a primary timer/home screen, a lightweight insights screen that explains what the app is learning, a history view for recent sessions, and settings/paywall. Anything beyond that needs a high burden of proof.

The biggest scope trap is pretending “adaptive” means lots of controls and analytics. It doesn’t. In MVP, the app should adapt only a few things: suggested focus duration, suggested break duration, and maybe a confidence-aware note about best time windows. That is enough to feel real. A lot of focus apps die because they bury the timer under productivity lifestyle clutter. This one should feel almost spare, but deliberate. If the home screen cannot explain the recommendation in a sentence like “You tend to complete 30-minute sessions most reliably in late morning,” then the adaptation system is too opaque or too fake.

Hard boundaries need to be explicit. No task manager. No calendar. No social pressure layer. No gamified forest, streak empire, or ambient wellness ritual system. No fake AI language. No “personalization” before enough sessions exist to justify it. No cloud dependency. No requirement to create an account. No giant dashboard trying to summarize a life. The app is about adaptive timing, not about becoming the user’s operating system.

The biggest risk in my preferred direction is that it could feel underwhelming in the first few days. A timer that is “still learning” can easily feel like a normal timer with marketing copy attached. So the cold-start UX has to be designed as honestly useful, not apologetic. The app should say, in effect: here’s a solid default, here’s what I’ll pay attention to, and here’s when recommendations become personalized. That honesty is better than pretending. The second risk is over-explaining. If every completed session produces a wall of analysis, the app becomes annoying. The learning has to be legible in one line, with deeper detail available but never forced.

Measurable success criteria should be concrete. A new user can start a session within seconds of first launch. After a small minimum history threshold, the app begins changing recommendations based on actual behavior. The user can see why a recommendation changed without reading documentation. Session completion, early stop, and quick post-session reflection are all persisted locally and survive interruption. The app remains fully functional offline. And most importantly, after several sessions, the recommendation engine produces observably different output for two different behavior patterns; otherwise the adaptation claim is not real.

The detail that absolutely must survive into later phases is this: the adaptive recommendation has to be both visible and explainable at the moment of use, on the main timer screen. If the “learning” is buried in an analytics tab, then the product promise is broken even if the underlying logic exists.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Here's my first pass on turning the contract into a precise product promise and scope, coming at it from what could actually break and what's the smallest thing that truly ships.

The one-sentence promise I'd commit to: Interval is a focus timer that starts you on a sensible default schedule, logs what actually happens when you use it, and after enough real sessions, changes its session-length and break-length recommendations to match your observed behavior — and always shows you why. Notice what's NOT in that sentence: no claim about "timing" recommendations (i.e., telling you *when* during the day to focus) in the first cut. The contract mentions time-of-day performance as one of the inspectable signals, and I'm fine with time-of-day being an input to the adaptation math, but a full "best time of day to start a session" recommendation surface is a second feature with its own empty/cold-start/UI states, its own failure modes (what if the user's schedule is chaotic and there's no real time-of-day pattern — do we show a false-confidence recommendation?), and its own testing burden. If we try to ship both duration-adaptation and timing-adaptation as equally first-class in an MVP, I'd bet money the timing half ends up half-tested or hand-waved under deadline pressure, which is exactly the "half-built breadth" failure mode the contract tells us to avoid. My call: duration/break-length adaptation is the load-bearing v1 feature; time-of-day is at most a secondary insight surfaced in "why this changed" explanations, not its own recommendation engine, unless we have room left after the core loop is airtight.

Primary user and scenario: someone who already wants to do focused work (student, knowledge worker, freelancer) and currently either uses a rigid 25/5 Pomodoro app that doesn't fit them, or uses nothing and just loses time to distraction. The concrete scenario I'd design against: they open the app, tap start, do a session, and either finish it, stop it early, or get interrupted (phone call, notification, walking away). That "did not finish cleanly" path is going to be the most common real-world outcome and the one most timer apps handle badly — so from a QA lens, the early-stop and interruption paths are not edge cases here, they're the main character. If the adaptation logic only has clean, well-labeled data for "completed" sessions and treats everything else as noise or undefined, the model will learn from a biased sample and the whole promise collapses.

Core loop, stated as plainly as I can: pick a session (defaulted, or adjusted) → start → timer runs and survives backgrounding/lock → session ends by completion, early-stop, or interruption, and the app asks/logs which one happened and optionally a quick energy/focus self-rating → that outcome gets appended to local history → before the next session starts, the recommendation engine re-derives session/break length from the accumulated history and shows the current recommendation plus the reason behind it if it changed. That last "show the reason" step is not decoration — it's the only thing that distinguishes this from a settings screen with an auto-slider, and it's also the thing most likely to get cut under time pressure because it's UI/copy work with no obvious "feature" glamour. I want it locked in now as core loop, not polish.

Hard scope boundaries I'd draw, mostly to prevent scope creep I can already see coming: one session type at a time in v1 (no multiple named contexts/profiles — that's a plausible paid feature per the contract, but it multiplies the state space and testing surface for adaptation logic considerably, since now you need per-profile history, per-profile cold-start thresholds, etc.). No manual free-form timer separate from the adaptive one — there should be one timer experience, with the recommendation as the default, and the user can still override before starting, but I don't want two parallel timer subsystems to maintain and test. No streaks, badges, or social framing — nothing that turns this into a habit-gamification app, per the non-goals. No background audio/soundscapes as a v1 feature — that's the kind of "nice to have" that's genuinely orthogonal to the core promise and easy to get sucked into designing.

Measurable success criteria, framed so QA can actually check them rather than just vibe-check them: a fresh install with zero history must produce a usable first session with a clearly-labeled default schedule (testable: does the UI ever claim personalization before the completed-session threshold is met — this should fail a test if it does). After the defined minimum number of completed sessions, changing the simulated history (e.g., in unit tests, feeding synthetic session logs with different completion/interruption patterns) must produce a different recommended duration than the default — this is directly testable and should be an actual automated test, not just something we eyeball in the simulator. A session that is backgrounded or the phone is locked must still fire the correct local notification at the correct time and the app must correctly reconcile state on relaunch/force-quit (was the session completed, abandoned, or still "in progress" when it shouldn't be) — this is the single highest-risk technical bet in the whole app and deserves explicit test scenarios, not just a general QA pass. And a privacy check: an automated or manual audit confirming zero network calls originate from the timer/logging/adaptation code path.

The biggest risk to my own framing here: by cutting timing-of-day recommendations down to a secondary signal, I might be under-delivering on the word "timing" in the original one-sentence promise ("adapts session length/**timing**"), and someone could reasonably argue I'm quietly narrowing the product promise to make QA's life easier. I think that's a defensible tradeoff — a shallow, always-on time-of-day recommendation with no real basis (most users won't have enough same-time-of-day sessions logged for weeks) is worse than not claiming it — but I want that called out explicitly as a scope decision now, not discovered as a surprise later when someone reads "timing" in the promise and asks where the day-scheduling feature is.

## Final Output

**One-sentence product promise:** Interval is a focus timer that starts you on a sensible default session/break schedule, logs what actually happens (completed, stopped early, or interrupted), and — once you've logged enough real sessions — adapts its recommended session and break length to match your observed behavior, always showing you the reason behind any change.

**Primary user and scenario:** Someone who already wants to do focused work (student, knowledge worker, freelancer) and has bounced off rigid fixed-interval timers because their real focus capacity varies by day, workload, or energy. They open the app, see today's recommendation (and once personalized, why it is what it is), start the session, and the timer has to survive their phone being locked or backgrounded — because most real sessions end by early-stop or interruption, not a clean finish, and the app has to treat that as the main case, not an edge case.

**Core loop:** Open app → see current recommended session/break length and, once adapted, a one-line reason why → start (optionally override the suggested duration) → session runs and survives lock/background via local notifications → session ends as completed, stopped early, or interrupted, and a quick outcome (plus optional energy/focus rating) is logged → history updates → recommendation is re-derived for the next session, with an explanation surfaced if it changed.

**Hard scope boundaries:**
- Duration/break-length adaptation is the load-bearing v1 feature. Time-of-day performance can inform the reasoning shown to the user, but it is not its own recommendation surface in v1.
- One session context/profile only in v1 — no multiple named profiles (work vs. study, etc.); that's a plausible future paid feature, explicitly deferred.
- A single timer experience — no separate manual/free-form timer running alongside the adaptive one. The user can override a recommended duration before starting, but there's only one subsystem to build and test.
- No task manager, calendar, social/accountability features, streaks/badges/gamification, background audio/soundscapes, or general "productivity dashboard" sprawl.
- No claimed personalization before the defined minimum completed-session threshold — the UI must honestly distinguish "still learning" from "adapted to you."
- No cloud dependency, no required account, no telemetry/analytics in the core timer/logging/adaptation path.

**Measurable success criteria:**
- A brand-new user can start a session within seconds of first launch, with clearly labeled (non-personalized) defaults; the UI must never claim personalization before the threshold is met — this is a testable assertion, not a vibe check.
- After the minimum-session threshold, feeding different synthetic session histories (varying completion/interruption patterns) through the adaptation logic must produce genuinely different recommended durations — covered by an automated test.
- Any changed recommendation is explainable in one line, visible on the main timer/home screen — never buried in a separate analytics tab.
- Backgrounding or locking mid-session fires the correct local notification, and the app correctly reconciles session state (completed/abandoned/still-in-progress) on relaunch or force-quit — covered by explicit test scenarios, not just a general QA pass.
- The app remains fully usable offline, and an audit confirms zero network calls originate from the timer/logging/adaptation code path.
