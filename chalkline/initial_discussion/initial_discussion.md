### Round 1


**Codex (Product Strategist · User Advocate) — Round 1**

Chalkline exists to let a climber review one attempt and immediately see where their body movement drifted or broke down on that route, so their next try improves with intent rather than guesswork.

The primary user is a frequent boulderer or sport climber (roughly multiple sessions per week, usually 1–5), standing at a wall in a gym, often with a phone propped up while climbing, who already logs routes in some form and wants objective technique feedback that is fast enough to use between attempts. The user story is: they try a route, fail or send, and in under the time it takes to chalk up they want to record/import a clip, run analysis, and get a clear, trustworthy readout tied to that specific route.

The core loop is: open Chalkline, quickly pick or create gym and route context with minimal taps, launch hands-free capture (or import a clip), analyze at a fixed low frame rate on-device, save an attempt with route metadata, and review an overlaid movement trace plus a short explainable efficiency summary; then immediately compare with a previous attempt on the same route and act on one concrete correction before the next attempt. In short: film, analyze, compare, correct, repeat.

Hard scope boundaries are: local-first SwiftUI + SwiftData with gyms, routes, sessions, and attempts (owned video files copied into app sandbox), on-device Vision pose sampling at low FPS, movement-trace visualization always, numeric efficiency only if confidence-weighted, repeatable signals are stable in common gym conditions, and gated subscription behavior that is actually testable (free: capped analysis history; paid: unlimited attempts retained plus comparison feature and plan generation path). Not in v1: CloudKit, community route-sharing, full training-plan generation, Widget, Live Activity, Siri intents, and any claim of lab-grade motion capture. Trace-heavy views also require a textual/VoiceOver summary so the core value is not lost to accessibility.

Measurable success criteria: within a 90-second review window after an attempt, the route context is saved and analysis starts; 95th percentile onboarding users can complete one attempt log + capture with fewer than ~6 taps before recording begins; captured attempts store predictably in local retention policy with clear cap enforcement on free tier; over 80% of processed attempts in test clips show stable frame-by-frame confidence above a defined threshold before any efficiency score is shown, otherwise the app explicitly downgrades to trace-only with fallback messaging logged; and all main states are implemented for visible flows (empty/loading/error/success), including a comparable route attempt review path for paid and a cleanly disabled, explainable comparison path for free users. Biggest risk to this direction is noisy pose tracking in real gym lighting; if we ignore it and ship a number anyway, trust collapses immediately. Final output must preserve this as a hard rule and not as optional behavior.

**Claude (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

Let me stake out the product definition from a design/IA angle, since that's where the ambiguity in the spec actually lives.

The one-sentence promise I'd lock in: Chalkline lets a climber film an attempt at the wall, see within seconds where their movement broke down compared to their own history on that route, and know whether this attempt was actually better than the last — entirely offline, entirely on-device. I'd resist promises that lean on "efficiency score" as the headline noun, because that's conditional per the fallback rule — the promise has to hold even in trace-only mode, so it should center on "where it broke down," which both the trace and the score can serve.

Primary user and scenario: a climber standing at the base of a boulder problem, thirty seconds after falling, phone propped on a bag a few feet away, chalk still on their hands. They are not sitting down to configure an app — they're deciding whether to try again immediately or rest, and they want the review to be available before their arms recover. That's the real scenario, and it should govern every IA decision below.

Core loop, as I'd sequence it: open app (lands on "today at this gym" context, not a global home screen) → select or quick-create a route (must be reachable in 2 taps, with the option to skip entirely and assign it post-hoc) → hands-free capture with a countdown and auto-stop, no further phone contact needed → analysis runs in the background while the user is still recovering/chalking up, not gating them from starting the next attempt → review screen shows trace, confidence, and (if stable) the efficiency delta versus their last attempt on that same route → quick tag (sent/fell, note) → loop repeats, with the comparison view becoming available the moment a second attempt exists on the same route.

Now the part I think the prior phases underweighted: the information architecture has a few load-bearing decisions that need to be made now, not discovered during build, because they change the data model.

First, session scoping. Is a "session" auto-created per gym visit (e.g., first attempt logged today at Gym X opens a session, closes on app background after N hours), or does the user manually start/stop one? Given the "don't make me configure anything before I climb" constraint, I want sessions to be implicit and auto-scoped — the user should never see a "start session" button. Streaks then key off session-days, not manual check-ins.

Second, and this is the one I feel strongly about: capture must not require a route to be pre-selected. The scenario above — someone grabs the phone mid-bouldering-session to film an attempt — means the record button has to be reachable with zero required upstream state, and route/gym tagging becomes a fast post-capture step, not a pre-capture gate. If the build forces "pick a route, then film," it violates the fall-to-analyzed-clip speed commitment that portfolio_selection already locked in, even though it looks like a small ordering detail.

Third, a genuine edge case nobody's named yet: what happens to the comparison view when the two attempts being compared have different confidence outcomes — one produced a stable efficiency number, the other only produced a trace (because lighting was worse, or the fallback triggers per-clip rather than globally)? I think the right answer is the comparison view has to degrade per-pair, not per-app: if either attempt lacks a stable score, the comparison shows trace-overlay-and-phase-summary only, silently and gracefully, rather than showing "62 vs —" like a broken stat. This needs to be a designed state, not an afterthought, because it will happen constantly in real gym lighting.

Fourth, retention policy has a product-correctness issue hiding in it. When the free-tier cap is hit, what gets deleted — the whole attempt record, or just the heavy artifacts (video file, pose trace)? It has to be the latter only. The numeric log entry (route, grade, date, sent/fell, note) is the baseline the user is trusting us to keep forever, per the "route log is just numbers, but at least it's a real log" framing from research — if hitting a video-storage cap silently erases someone's send history, we've broken a more basic promise than the one about pose analysis. So: attempts are permanent, lightweight rows; video+trace are the prunable, capped, compressible layer on top.

Fifth, empty states need to be specific rather than generic. A comparison view with zero prior attempts on this route shouldn't say "no data" — it should say "log a second attempt on this route to unlock comparison," because that's actionable and ties back into the core loop instead of dead-ending it.

Hard scope boundaries I'd draw for this phase: single climber per clip only (no multi-person pose tracking — if two bodies are detected, treat as ambiguous/low-confidence, don't try to disambiguate); no cloud, no account system, no social; training-plan generation, widget, Live Activity, and Siri intents are explicitly out of this pass; comparison is two-attempt-same-route only, not multi-attempt trend charts (that's a natural v2, but scope creep here would blow the build budget).

Measurable success criteria I'd propose, because "it feels fast" isn't checkable: route/attempt logging reachable in ≤2 taps from app open when no pre-context is picked; capture-to-review latency (attempt ends → trace visible) should be bounded and disclosed even if slow — I'd rather commit to "analysis completes before the user finishes their next attempt" as the target than a hard second count, since device performance varies; every one of the ~8 core screens (gym list, route detail, capture, analyzing/processing, review, comparison, session/streak, settings/paywall) demonstrably has empty, loading, success, and error states before this is called done; and the retention/cap behavior must be independently verifiable — someone should be able to hit the cap in testing and see media pruned while log rows survive, not have to trust a description of it.

The single biggest risk to my own framing here: making capture route-optional and session-implicit adds real modeling complexity (orphaned/unassigned attempts, a reconciliation UI for "attempts needing a route"), and if we're not disciplined that reconciliation flow could itself become a second-class citizen full of bugs. I think it's worth the cost because the alternative — forcing pre-configuration — directly contradicts the speed commitment this whole product is supposed to honor, but I want it named as a real complexity trade, not a free lunch.
