Climbing technique & route log with on-device movement analysis

Shows a climber exactly where their movement broke down on a route, not just what grade they sent.

Boulderers and sport climbers who climb several times a week and want to improve technique, not just log send counts.

A climber films an attempt on their phone but has no objective way to see where their center of mass drifted, where they over-gripped, or how this attempt compares to the last one — improvement is guesswork and the route log is just numbers.

On-device Vision body-pose estimation over captured attempt video produces a movement trace and an efficiency score per attempt — a real, deterministic, camera-centric technical capability nobody else localizes in a climbing app; bold gym-signage graphic visual language with chalk-dust texture, route-color-coding, and haptic-tied transitions between attempts.

Subscription unlocks unlimited video-analysis history, side-by-side attempt comparison, and training-plan generation from logged technique weaknesses.

Free tier: route/attempt logging, capped video-analysis history. Paid: unlimited analysis history, attempt-comparison view, generated training plans.

SwiftData models for gyms/routes/attempts/sessions with video references; Vision pose estimation samples each attempt clip at a fixed low frame rate (not full video framerate) and efficiency scoring runs fully on-device against locally stored video.

## Future Backend Roadmap

CloudKit sync for cross-device attempt history; optional gym-community route-sharing later — additive on top of the local-first model.

## AR / Local ML / Local LLM Fit

On-device Vision framework for body-pose estimation during attempt review, sampled at a fixed low frame rate per clip for reliability — the app's defining hard capability, not a bonus feature.

## Design Direction

Bold gym-signage graphic design: chalk-dust textures, high-contrast route-color-coding, kinetic haptic-tied transitions between attempts, energetic but legible type scale.

The climbing app that shows you where your technique actually broke down, using pose analysis no other climbing log does on-device.

Market signal from prompt: this looks like a crossover between (a) climbing log apps (route-centric metadata + grades), (b) camera-based sports coaching tools (Coach’s Eye-style playback + feedback), and (c) premium coaching UX seen in movement drills apps where visual overlays make abstract issues concrete. No major known app appears to do on-device pose-derived technical scoring specifically for climbers, so defensibility is in this vertical specialization + deterministic local ML.

Likely user problem in context: after a hard attempt, they want to know exactly where the motion degraded and whether the next attempt improved; today they get either “I sent it / failed” or fuzzy notes. Video review exists, but most tools don’t make movement comparison repeatable and local-first.

Competition/patterns to borrow from:
- Route logs: TheCrag / 8a-style catalog behavior (hierarchical route metadata, grading, attempts, sends, personal notes).
- Gym coaching workflows: tempo/rep playback and overlay concepts from sports-video tools; side-by-side or temporal comparison patterns.
- Design pattern in high-action apps: strict state clarity (loading/empty/error), quick capture-to-review loop, and immediate “what improved / what worsened” summaries.
- Payment pattern: paid analysis capabilities rather than generic premium, with hard behavior gates so users perceive value in unlocking.

iOS-native opportunities that matter here:

- VisionKit/AVFoundation integration for predictable capture/import UX is essential; keep as one tap from session screen.
- Camera framing aid should feel iOS-native: overlay guidance, focus/exposure tips, and confidence hints while recording.
- Haptics can be used meaningfully for state transitions (capture start/stop, attempt marked, comparison loaded) but shouldn’t overwhelm; they should feel tied to state changes.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Let me think through this from a QA/risk and pragmatist angle rather than a pure market-research angle, since that's the lens I need to hold this phase.

On audience: the target is climbers who go several times a week, which matters a lot for scope decisions because that's a user who will generate a lot of clips fast — dozens per week if they film every attempt on a project — and who will notice immediately if the app's core claim (showing where technique broke down) doesn't hold up after the fifth or sixth use. This isn't a casual logger's audience that tolerates a rough edge here and there; frequent gym climbers are exactly the crowd that already uses Kaya, SendAG, or the Moon Board app for logging, and they'll benchmark Chalkline's log-a-route-and-attempt flow against those existing habits within the first session. If logging a route takes more taps than their current spreadsheet or app, they bounce before ever reaching the pose-estimation payoff. So the practical implication is that the "boring" CRUD flow — gym, route, attempt, grade, tags — needs to be as fast and frictionless as the flashy pose feature, or we lose the audience before the differentiator even gets a chance to land.

On comparable apps and patterns: the closest functional pattern isn't really other climbing apps (which are mostly grade logs and social feeds), it's sports-form-analysis tools like Coach's Eye, Hudl Technique, or golf/baseball swing analyzers that overlay skeletal tracking on slow-mo video. Those apps teach a useful lesson worth carrying into risk planning: users of that category are used to scrubbing frame-by-frame and comparing two clips side by side, and they get frustrated fast when the overlay drifts, jitters, or loses the subject mid-clip — which is exactly the failure mode Vision-based pose tracking on a bouldering wall (partial occlusion by the wall/holds, climber often facing away or sideways from camera, chalk dust reducing contrast, gym lighting is frequently mixed color-temperature fluorescent) is likely to hit. I'd treat that as the single largest unknown for this build, and I want it named as a risk rather than assumed away: we do not know, without testing against real gym footage, whether Vision's body-pose confidence stays usable when a climber is sideways on an overhang with one arm occluded by their own torso. The contract already pre-authorizes the fallback for this, which is good, but the practical implication is that whoever builds this should test pose confidence against a handful of realistic self-shot clips (varied angle, lighting, clothing) as early as possible, not after the whole capture-to-review pipeline is otherwise done — because if the fallback triggers, it changes what the comparison view and the "efficiency" language across the entire app need to say.

Second real risk, platform-specific: this app's core loop is film-yourself-climbing, which means most of the time the phone is not being held by the user — it's propped against a bag, wedged in a cubby, or handed to a friend. That has concrete implications for the capture flow: a framing guide only helps if the user can see the screen while setting up the shot, and once they start climbing they can't touch the phone again until the attempt is over. So the real design/engineering requirement hiding under "capture" is a hands-free countdown-and-record flow (voice cue or big visual countdown, auto-stop after a max duration or fixed timeout) — not just a record button. I'd flag that a Live Activity or watch companion would be the "premium" answer to this problem, but since those are explicitly deferred, the MVP needs a decent phone-only answer (e.g., a stand/prop framing guide plus a generous auto max-length so users don't have to babysit a stop button from fifteen feet away).

Third, on the "why users would pay" logic: capped analysis history as the free/paid boundary only creates pressure to subscribe if the cap is stingy enough to bite for an active climber within days, but not so stingy that free-tier users can't tell whether the pose feature is worth paying for at all. That's a tuning problem more than a design problem, but it's worth stating now as an assumption we'll need to pick a number for (e.g., something like 5 or 10 analyzed clips retained at a time on free) rather than leaving "capped" undefined until it's too late to matter.

On what would make this feel native rather than like a ported concept: haptics tied to attempt-to-attempt transitions and pose-confidence feedback (a distinct "good take" vs "reshoot, confidence too low" haptic) is the kind of thing that's cheap to build well and expensive to get right if postponed, so I'd want it treated as core interaction design, not a decorative afterthought bolted on at the end. Same with the framing-guide screen — this needs live camera preview with an overlay skeleton silhouette, which is a real technical task (AVFoundation capture session plus a live Vision request), not a static graphic, or it won't actually help users get analyzable footage.

The biggest thing I want preserved out of this phase for the next one: the app's credibility lives or dies on whether the pose signal is honest, and the biggest unknown standing between "shippable" and "we have to invoke the fallback" is untested — nobody has pointed Vision's body-pose request at actual bouldering-gym footage yet in this process. Everything else (design system, comparison view shape, retention numbers) is normal product work we can reason about from experience. This one is an empirical unknown that should get resolved with a cheap early spike rather than optimistically assumed, because the entire "why it's different" claim and the subscription value prop both sit directly on top of it.
