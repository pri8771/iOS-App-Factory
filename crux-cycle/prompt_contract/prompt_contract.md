Second, the biggest risk in this specific app is the core premise itself: "rest-day enforcement." Enforcement implies the app can compel behavior, but a phone app cannot stop a climber from going to the gym. If we build this as a nagging notification system, it dies in a week — that's the single biggest commercial failure mode for this category (see every overtraining/recovery app that got uninstalled after the guilt-trip notifications got annoying). The contract needs to define "enforcement" honestly: soft-gating (surfacing risk, adjusting recommended intensity, requiring an acknowledgment tap to override), not literal blocking. Any later phase that designs this as punitive or infantilizing should be flagged as violating user trust, not just as a UX nitpick.

Third, this is a Health & Fitness app, not Medical — but periodization/training-load models (things like ACWR — acute:chronic workload ratio) walk right up to the line of medical claims ("you are at high injury risk"). The additional notes in the parent prompt say medical/health apps must frame outputs as wellness insights and validated risk scores, never diagnosis — I'd apply that same discipline here even though Crux Cycle is Health & Fitness, not Medical, because injury-risk framing is exactly the kind of claim that gets an app flagged by reviewers or, worse, actually discourages someone from seeing a doctor for a finger pulley injury. Non-goal: no injury diagnosis, no medical claims, no "you have tendinopathy" style outputs. Everything must read as training-load guidance, not clinical assessment.

Fourth, local-first + rest-day enforcement collides with a real edge case: what happens when someone reinstalls the app, or uses it on a second device, or the app is deleted and reinstalled to "cheat" the rest-day logic? If the entire training history lives in local storage with no cloud backup, a user loses their whole periodization history to a bad iOS update or a lost phone, and there's also nothing stopping someone from deleting the app to reset an enforced rest day. I don't think we need to solve this with cloud sync in the MVP, but the contract should state explicitly: local persistence must survive app relaunch and backgrounding (not just be in-memory), and the "enforcement" mechanic should be honest that it's a nudge system a determined user can bypass — we shouldn't market it as unbypassable.

Fifth, "structured periodization" implies real domain logic: training blocks (base/build/peak/deload), session logging (boulder grades, route grades, attempts, sends), load tracking over time, and a recommendation engine for when to rest. That's a lot of actual state machine and data modeling, not just a calendar UI. If the build phase ships a pretty calendar with a static rest-day toggle and calls it "periodization," that fails the production-readiness bar. My definition of done for this app: a user can log climbing sessions with grade/intensity, the app computes a rolling training load and flags acute spikes, it recommends (and visually gates, with override) rest versus train days grouped into a periodization block structure, and all of this persists correctly across app restarts, low-storage conditions, and permission-denied states (e.g., no HealthKit access) without crashing or silently losing data.

Sixth, monetization: a subscription needs to gate something with ongoing value, not a one-time unlock. For a periodization app the defensible recurring value is the adaptive/recompute logic — the plan is a real projection recomputed as it is trained through changing load calculation, e.g. adjusting future blocks. If it is just "buy pro to remove ads" that fails requirement 5 (genuine value, not cosmetics).
