The paid value has to be something that gets better the longer she uses it, not something that's the same on day 400 as day 1. Free tier: log sessions, see current 7-day/28-day load, get the basic train/rest signal with the one-line reasoning. That's enough to be genuinely useful and enough to make her trust the number before we ever ask for money. Paid tier (I'd call it Crux Cycle Pro, $6.99/mo or $39.99/yr — this is a niche enough audience that dirt-cheap pricing signals "toy," and climbers already pay $80+/month for gym memberships so this isn't the expensive thing in their life): multi-discipline load separation (see your bouldering load and your route load as separate lines, not just blended into one number, useful the moment she starts trad climbing on weekends and bouldering on weeknights), periodization block labeling that retroactively analyzes her last 90 days and shows her which weeks were actually base/build/peak/deload versus which were just chaotic, a weekly digest that surfaces pattern language a free user doesn't get ("your hardest sends cluster 2 days after a rest day — your data suggests you peak on 48hr recovery, not 24hr"), and CSV/PDF export to hand to an actual coach if she ever gets one. None of that is cosmetic — it's more computation over more accumulated history, so a user who's logged for a year gets a materially better product than one who's logged for a week, which is the honest reason to keep paying.

Core loop is what we already locked: log in under a minute, load recalculates, recommendation shows its reasoning, she overrides or follows it, override feeds back in. What I want to add here as a brief-level commitment: the very first session she ever logs must already produce something that feels smart, even with a data-starved model — if load computation needs 28 days of history before it says anything, she's gone before the first Tuesday. So there needs to be a clearly-labeled "still calibrating" state for the first ~2 weeks that gives provisional, honest guidance ("not enough history yet — here's your load so far") rather than either a fake confident number or a dead blank screen. This is a joy/trust issue, not a technical nice-to-have: the single biggest way this app dies is if the first week feels like using a spreadsheet with extra steps.

Competitive wedge: every existing recovery/training-load app in this space (the Whoop/Oura HRV-strain crowd, the generic "workout tracker with a recovery score" apps) computes recovery from biometrics she has to wear something for, and shows a badge with no visible math — she's supposed to just trust a black-box score from a company that doesn't understand climbing-specific load (a boulder session with 40 hard attempts on 15-foot problems loads fingers and skin completely differently than an hour of easy route mileage, and generic fitness-tracker load models don't know the difference). Our wedge is: climbing-specific load math (grade-weighted, attempt-weighted, discipline-aware), zero wearable dependency, and the reasoning always shown in plain language next to the number. "Better than a badge" is the actual pitch — climbers who are analytically minded enough to worry about overtraining are exactly the people who won't trust a number they can't see the work behind.

Growth angle is niche, not viral-broad, and I think that's correct rather than a compromise: climbing gyms have community boards, Instagram is full of climbing-specific micro-influencers, and there's an obvious shareable artifact — a clean, well-designed "load history" chart or "periodization block" summary screenshot that's genuinely interesting to look at and says something real about training discipline, the way a nice running-year-in-review gets shared. I'd design one specific screen (the load trend chart, styled cleanly, maybe a shareable weekly-summary card) explicitly with export/share in mind, because that's the free marginal cost distribution channel this category actually has — climbing forums (r/climbing) and gym Discord/WhatsApp groups where "here's what my training load looked like this month" is a normal, non-cringe thing to post, unlike a running app's mileage badge culture which doesn't map onto this audience.

Local-first isn't a constraint here, it's a feature worth saying out loud to the user: no account creation, no login wall, works in a gym basement with zero signal, first session logged within 30 seconds of opening the app for the first time. Under the hood that means everything (sessions, computed load snapshots, override history) lives in on-device persistence (SwiftData is the sane default here) with the recommendation engine as a pure function over locally stored history — nothing about the load math should assume a server exists. The cloud-ready path I want preserved into architecture: the data model needs a stable identity/versioning scheme now (UUIDs, not autoincrement, timestamps in UTC) so that a future CloudKit or custom-backend sync layer can be bolted on as an additive sync engine later without a data migration nightmare — this costs us almost nothing today and saves a rewrite later, so it's a rule, not a suggestion.

The single biggest risk to my own pitch: this whole product lives or dies on whether the "provisional calibrating" period is designed well enough that a new user doesn't churn before the load math has enough history to say anything interesting — if the demo/first-run experience is boring, the multi-discipline pro features never get seen. I'd rather we spend real design effort on day-1-through-day-14 than on the periodization visualization, because a beautiful chart nobody sticks around long enough to see is worthless. The other thing that must survive into every later phase: the reasoning-visible requirement isn't a UI nicety, it's the entire trust mechanism that justifies charging money for "more computation over more history" — if a later phase ships a badge/score without the visible why, we've quietly turned this into the exact black-box competitor we're positioning against.

## Final Output

**Target user & use case:** A self-aware intermediate/advanced boulderer or route climber (e.g., V4–V6, training 3–4x/week, has had a minor overuse injury before) who already informally tracks sessions and wants to know, with visible reasoning, whether today should be a hard day, easy day, or rest day. Not a beginner who doesn't know grades, not a sponsored athlete with a coach.

**Paid value / subscription value ("Crux Cycle Pro," ~$6.99/mo or $39.99/yr):**
- Free: log sessions, see current 7-/28-day load and the basic train/rest recommendation with its one-line reasoning.
- Paid: separate load lines per discipline (bouldering vs. route, not blended into one number), retroactive periodization-block labeling over your real history, a weekly pattern-language digest (e.g., "you peak 48hrs after rest, not 24hrs"), and CSV/PDF export for a coach.
- The value compounds with accumulated history — a user with a year of data gets meaningfully more out of Pro than a user with a week — which is what makes this a real subscription, not a skin.

**Core loop:** unchanged from initial_discussion (log → recompute load → recommendation with visible reasoning → follow or override → override writes back into the model). New commitment added this round: the first ~2 weeks need an honest, clearly-labeled "still calibrating" state with provisional guidance — never a blank screen or a fake-confident number — because losing the user before the model has enough history to say anything interesting kills the product before Pro features are ever seen.

**Competitive wedge:** climbing-specific, grade-and-attempt-weighted load math with zero wearable/HealthKit dependency, and the reasoning always shown in plain language next to the number — directly opposed to the Whoop/Oura-style black-box recovery score that doesn't know the difference between finger-intensive bouldering load and easy route mileage.

**Growth angle:** niche, not broad-viral — one well-designed, shareable screen (load trend chart / weekly summary card) built explicitly for export, aimed at climbing gym community boards, Instagram micro-influencers, and forums/Discords where posting your training data is already a normal thing to do.

**Local-first & cloud-ready plan:** no login, no signal required, first session logged within 30 seconds of first open; everything (sessions, computed load snapshots, override history) lives in on-device persistence (SwiftData) with the recommendation engine as a pure function over local history. Data model uses UUIDs and UTC timestamps from day one specifically so a future CloudKit or custom sync layer can be added additively later without a migration rewrite — a hard rule for architecture, not a suggestion.

**Open flag carried forward (not blocking consensus):** design and architecture phases must protect the "reasoning always visible" requirement as the core trust mechanism that justifies charging for Pro — if a later phase ships a bare score/badge without the visible why, that's a deviation from this brief and should be flagged, not quietly shipped.
