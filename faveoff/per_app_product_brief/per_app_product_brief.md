**Codex (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

FaveOff serves casual viewers who regularly watch movies and TV but never built a reliable personal “best of” system: people who finish a binge and can’t honestly say their own top 10 because 4-star ratings flatten nuance. The core use case is a weekly “I watched X last night” moment where adding a few new titles should immediately improve a living ranking and produce something they want to show in a chat, not a clerical review list.

Users pay for FaveOff+ when they hit two friction points in the free flow: they want more than movie-only ranking, and they want utility around depth. The paid unlocks a second ranking track for TV (free users can still log TV titles, but those aren’t included in ranked output), removes list-length caps for ranked tracks, enables genre/decade sub-rank views so “best rom-coms of 2010s” is fast, and unlocks periodic recap cards that feel like a monthly Wrapped moment. This keeps paid utility real and separate from core free value, instead of gating the initial experience.

The app’s core loop is intentionally tiny: add a watched title, resolve a fast head-to-head placement sequence, immediately insert it into a ranked list, then browse and share. The ranking engine is incremental binary insertion (bounded, on-device comparisons) so each new title is placed through a small, predictable number of matchups and never forced through a full round-robin treadmill. The comparison is the product: two posters, two large touch targets, one clean decision, then a clear placement confirmation (“#4 of 12, above ___ below ___”) before returning to browsing. If users cannot compare, a skip path exists that does not break ordering guarantees.

The subscription value is not cosmetic badges or social features; it is extra ranking surface and depth: more tracks, fewer constraints, richer recaps. Free users still get a complete, shareable movie taste loop with Taste Card export and offline-safe progress. If monetization doesn’t add actual decision power, it isn’t real value.

Competitive wedge is simple: this is not IMDb/Letterboxd/Rotten Tomatoes style cataloging and not social proof tracking. Those tools assume people already enjoy logging and rating. FaveOff is the opposite: it asks one tiny question repeatedly and turns that into a trustworthy taste model with native iOS UX and zero social graph. The wedge compounds through velocity and shareability: you don’t need followers to compete, just a well-made card to drop in text threads.

For growth, the strongest loop is share-first, screenshot-ready taste artifacts plus minimal friction referrals: “tap and compare to update your top 10, share your card, invite a friend to build theirs.” The viral moment is not a leaderboard; it’s social comparison by artifact. This keeps it mass-market, because everyone with a phone and a few watched titles can participate instantly.

The experience must feel local-first from first tap: all titles, comparisons, and ranking state are stored in SwiftData and remain usable offline. The network path for poster and metadata retrieval lives behind a protocol, with a local-first stub as primary behavior: manual title entry, clean placeholder posters from title initials and color blocks, and no blocking waits in ranking. Missing metadata should degrade to “compare anyway, enrich later,” never “cannot proceed.” Cloud is a clean extension path only: keep protocol boundaries around persistence and sync services so a future CloudKit layer can mirror local models without changing ranking logic.

The design should be cinematic by function, not just look: dark, high-contrast surfaces; a constrained high-impact red accent; poster-first cards; one-screen compare focus; short transitions that feel like a film reel cut; and immediate progression cues. The critical detail to preserve in later phases is a hard rule: every reachable screen has empty/loading/success/error states and 44-pt controls, with accessibility-first defaults. Biggest risk to this direction is trust collapse from sparse comparisons feeling arbitrary on edge-case pairings; if users stop after a few rounds, the ranking appears random and they quit. To prevent that, preserve the placed-feedback moment, strict comparison bounds, and a skip path that keeps ordering stable.

The cloud-ready path is a repository-boundary bet, not a built feature: the SwiftData models and ranking engine never reference CloudKit or any sync framework directly, so a future sync layer or account system slots in behind the existing repository protocol without touching ranking logic — I'm treating "could add CloudKit later without a rewrite" as an architectural constraint we verify by code review (is anything reaching into SwiftData directly from a view, or does everything go through a repository type), not as a feature we build now.

What makes someone keep using this past the novelty session, which is the thing I actually care about as the risk-focused voice in the room: it's not the paywall and it's not the widget, it's whether the "placed" moment feels genuinely rewarding every single time, because that's the only repeatable reward the app has — there's no streak, no notification nagging, no social feed to pull you back.
