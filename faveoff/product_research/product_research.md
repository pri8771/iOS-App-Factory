# FaveOff

## App Name

FaveOff

## Category

Entertainment / Taste Ranking

## One-Sentence Promise

Rank the movies and shows you've watched head-to-head, and FaveOff builds your real, personal top 10 — then turns it into a shareable taste card.

## Target User

Anyone who watches movies/shows and has opinions but finds star ratings meaningless and social-network movie apps like overhead — mass-market watchers, not cinephile completionists.

## Painful Moment Solved

Rating a movie 1-5 stars and realizing that number means nothing next to the 40 other movies you've also rated 4 stars — you genuinely don't know your own top 10.

## Why It Is Different

Instead of star ratings, shows two watched titles and asks which was better, building an Elo-style ranked list from a handful of quick head-to-head picks — far more decisive than star ratings, ending in a poster-grid 'Taste Card' built for sharing, with zero social graph or account required to get value.

## Why Users Would Pay

Free tier ranks movies only with a capped list length; FaveOff+ unlocks TV shows and music/albums as separate ranking tracks, unlimited list length, genre-filtered sub-rankings, and periodic 'Wrapped'-style recap cards.

## Subscription Model

Freemium, $3.99/mo or $24.99/yr.

## Local-First MVP Scope

SwiftData store for titles, comparisons, and computed rankings; ranking algorithm runs fully on-device; title/poster metadata lookup is the one network dependency, isolated behind a protocol.

## Future Backend Roadmap

Repository boundary allows CloudKit sync across devices and, later, an optional social taste-comparison layer without touching the core ranking engine.

## AR / Local ML / Local LLM Fit

None load-bearing; the hard technical capability is a proper Elo/merge-sort-style pairwise ranking engine, not ML.

## Design Direction

Cinematic dark-mode-first system — near-black backgrounds, marquee-red accent, bold condensed poster-style typography, poster artwork as the dominant visual element, film-reel-inspired transitions.

## App Store Positioning

Star ratings lie to you. FaveOff makes you pick, one match-up at a time, until your real top 10 falls out.

## Invalidation Criteria

If users abandon mid-ranking after only a handful of comparisons and never reach a shareable top-10, the comparison flow is too tedious and needs a faster convergence algorithm or smaller default list size.

## v1 Build Scope

Add titles (movies+TV), head-to-head ranking engine, ranked list view, Taste Card generation + ShareLink, FaveOff+ paywall with StoreKit.

## Core Workflows

- add a watched title
- head-to-head comparison rounds
- browse computed ranked list
- generate & share Taste Card (poster grid of top 10)
- filter rankings by genre/decade

## iOS-Native Features

- ShareLink for Taste Card
- ImageRenderer for poster-grid card generation
- WidgetKit small widget showing current #1

## Key Risks

- The one network touchpoint (title/poster lookup) must degrade gracefully offline (initials/color-block placeholder), never blocking the core ranking loop
- Comparison fatigue on large libraries — needs a smart pairing algorithm scaling ~n log n, not full round-robin n²

### Round 1


**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

The strongest read is that FaveOff should feel like a quick social game, not a database. The audience is surprisingly broad if you frame it right: people who consume streaming content casually and have opinions but don’t want another review/notes app. “Parents, roommates, and college friends can explain this in one line” only if the first launch is: add 2–8 titles, pick winners in 30-second matchups, share a polished Taste Card.

The real market fit is at the edge between utility and social play. The utility is personal taste discovery; the social part is the artifact (top-10 card) and the implicit challenge (“tell me your #1” / “I ranked these in 10 seconds”). The core behavior must reward immediate completion, so every extra tap should feel meaningful.

For competitors/patterns, the obvious comparison set is Rotten Tomatoes/IMDb/Letterboxd-style cataloging, and “this vs that” game patterns from personality quizzes and bracket apps. They’re worth emulating only for onboarding clarity and shareability, not for complexity. Their default behavior is rating-heavy and often taxonomical; FaveOff’s differentiator is pairwise progression, so the UI must make comparisons feel like a ritualized choice, not a form.

The design direction is right: dark, high-contrast, poster-led, cinematic layout gives instant category recognition. On iOS this should map to a TabView with a very light IA: Rank (active flow), Browse (ranked list and filters), and Share/Accountability (Taste Card + paywall/help). Avoid deep navigation; every comparison should be single-task focus with one primary action plus one clear skip/back. If people can’t tell what to do in 5 seconds, they will churn before finishing their first 4 picks.

Platform-specific expectations for iPhone are non-negotiable: offline-first behavior must feel intentional, not fallback-only. If the network fails, they should still add and rank. Use local fallback artwork immediately so first-time value exists without waiting. Accessibility is a feature, not styling: minimum touch targets, readable dynamic type, and clear VoiceOver labels on card choices (e.g., “Select this title over …”). Sharing should feel native through ShareLink and copyable/exportable output; widget value is optional and should never block core flow.

Assumptions (explicit): I’m assuming users do not want to manually type lots of metadata on day one, so defaults matter; I’m assuming “mass-market viral loop” depends on output sharing more than social accounts; I’m assuming comparisons are emotionally easy if they appear like quick “face-off” decisions with immediate progression feedback.

Biggest objection to this direction: this can accidentally become a “mini judge-your-taste test” and feel arbitrary if insertion comparisons are under-explained. To prevent that, keep a lightweight confidence cue (“kept in top 10” / “needs more matchups for stronger confidence”) and show a stable top-N even while adding items so users don’t feel lost.

Key risks/unknowns to preserve for next phase: comparison-fatigue ceiling (how many rounds feels acceptable before stop rate spikes), metadata scarcity/offline mismatch (placeholder vs correct genre/decade), and algorithm trust (users need deterministic fairness more than clever math language). The paywall also risks being perceived as blocking if presented too early; gate premium benefits, not core value.

First build implication: lock the flow to rank-on-add with bounded comparisons, no upfront gauntlet, and explicit empty states (0/1/2 titles, syncing/loading lookup, and no-network fallback). Make “add two, compare one” the minimum viable loop, deliver a shareable top 10 within minutes, and keep StoreKit logic behind a true non-blocking gate so free users can still complete all primary flows.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Audience-wise I think the honest read is: not movie buffs, not people who already keep Letterboxd diaries — this is for the much bigger group who watch a lot but have never systematically thought about their own taste, the person who says "I don't know, I liked both" when asked to compare two movies and finds that genuinely fun rather than stressful. That's the same psychographic Beli captured for restaurants — head-to-head "which was better" comparisons that quietly build a real ranked list, with screenshot-shareable results driving word of mouth. Beli is the closest real-world proof that this exact mechanic works at mass-market scale outside of sports/fantasy contexts, and it's worth treating as the pattern to study: their comparisons are lightweight (two cards, tap one), the running list is always visible so you feel progress after every single tap, and the shareable score card is the entire growth loop. Letterboxd and IMDb are the wrong comps to imitate — they're archive/critique tools for people who already have strong opinions and want to log them, not decision tools for people who don't know their own opinion yet. FaveOff's whole pitch is closer to a personality quiz than a review app, and the build should feel that way.

The biggest risk I see that nobody's resolved yet: the spec has an internal contradiction between v1 Build Scope ("Add titles (movies+TV)") and Why Users Would Pay ("Free tier ranks movies only... FaveOff+ unlocks TV shows... as separate ranking tracks"). Taken literally, that means TV either isn't addable at all for free users, or it's addable but can't be ranked, or it gets merged into the movie list until upgrade — three very different UX flows with very different code paths. I don't think we can leave this for the build phase to guess at silently; it needs a named assumption now. My proposed resolution: free users can add and watch-log TV titles same as movies, but the ranking engine and ranked list view only operate on the movies track for free users — TV titles sit in an "added, not yet ranked" state with an upsell, and FaveOff+ unlocks a second, separate TV ranking track that runs through the same engine. That keeps the free core (movie ranking, Taste Card, sharing) fully real and valuable, which is the non-negotiable per the contract, while making the paywall boundary a track-level gate rather than a feature-level cripple.

Second risk, more of a QA one: binary-insertion ranking is only as pleasant as the comparisons it generates, and log2(n) insertion means a new title will sometimes get compared against something wildly unrelated to it — comparing a rom-com against a slasher film feels arbitrary and erodes trust in the "your real top 10" promise faster than star ratings ever did. There needs to be an explicit "too close to call" or "can't compare, haven't thought about it" escape that doesn't corrupt the sort invariant — probably treating a skip as "insert adjacent to whichever was most recently compared" rather than blocking the algorithm. This is exactly the abandonment failure mode the client's own invalidation criteria warns about, so I'd rather over-engineer the escape hatch than under-engineer it.

Third, the design direction — near-black background, marquee-red accent, bold condensed typography — is a contrast trap if we're not careful. Condensed display faces get worse, not better, under Dynamic Type's larger accessibility sizes, and red-on-black at low weight fails WCAG AA fast. I'd want the condensed face reserved for short display strings (titles, big rank numbers) with a normal-width system-backed style for anything that has to scale to accessibility sizes, verified against contrast ratios before we call any screen done, not after.

Fourth, and this is a build-order point: WidgetKit sharing the SwiftData store means an App Group container, which is real plumbing (entitlements, shared container URL, schema visible to both targets) — it's not "add a widget target and done." Given it's already flagged as first-to-cut, I'd sequence it dead last and not architect around it prematurely; if it slips, the main app's persistence shouldn't have taken on any complexity for its sake.

What the next phase needs to carry forward without re-litigating: the movies-free/TV-paid ambiguity has one resolution now (track-level gate, not feature-level), the ranking engine needs a defined skip/tie escape path as a first-class requirement not an edge case, contrast and Dynamic Type constraints on the condensed typography are a build requirement not a nice-to-have, and Beli is the reference pattern for what "fun, low-friction comparison loop" actually feels like in a shipped consumer app.
