Rank the movies and shows you've watched head-to-head, and FaveOff builds your real, personal top 10 — then turns it into a shareable taste card.

Anyone who watches movies/shows and has opinions but finds star ratings meaningless and social-network movie apps like overhead — mass-market watchers, not cinephile completionists.

Rating a movie 1-5 stars and realizing that number means nothing next to the 40 other movies you've also rated 4 stars — you genuinely don't know your own top 10.

Instead of star ratings, shows two watched titles and asks which was better, building an Elo-style ranked list from a handful of quick head-to-head picks — far more decisive than star ratings, ending in a poster-grid 'Taste Card' built for sharing, with zero social graph or account required to get value.

## Why Users Would Pay

Free tier ranks movies only with a capped list length; FaveOff+ unlocks TV shows and music/albums as separate ranking tracks, unlimited list length, genre-filtered sub-rankings, and periodic 'Wrapped'-style recap cards.

## Subscription Model

Freemium, $3.99/mo or $24.99/yr.

## Local-First MVP Scope

SwiftData store for titles, comparisons, and computed rankings; ranking algorithm runs fully on-device; title/poster metadata lookup is the one network dependency, isolated behind a protocol.

## Future Backend Roadmap

Repository boundary allows CloudKit sync across devices and, later, an optional social taste-comparison layer without touching the core ranking engine.

## AR / Local ML / Local LLM Fit

None load-bearing; the hard technical capability is a proper Elo/merge-sort-style pairwise ranking engine, not ML.

## Design Direction

Cinematic dark-mode-first system — near-black backgrounds, marquee-red accent, bold condensed poster-style typography, poster artwork as the dominant visual element, film-reel-inspired transitions.

## App Store Positioning

Star ratings lie to you. FaveOff makes you pick, one match-up at a time, until your real top 10 falls out.

**Codex (Product Strategist · User Advocate) — Round 1**

You should lock FaveOff to this one promise: turn two watched titles into a reliable personal top ten with fast head-to-head picks, then make that ranking instantly shareable as a Taste Card.  
The primary user is the casual film/TV watcher who has opinions but not a structured system; imagine a Friday-night friend asking “what should we watch this weekend?” and this person wants to produce a confident, defensible top list in minutes without a spreadsheet.  
The core loop is: add a watched title (manual by default), compare it against one existing title in the ranked list via binary-insertion pairings, immediately place it, and keep rankable items surfaced as a living top list that can be filtered and rendered into a card.  
No accounts, no social graph, no network-first backend, no music-album ranking in v1, no ML model in the loop, and no full round-robin matching—everything is local-first with SwiftData-backed titles/comparisons/rank state and merge/binary-style bounded ranking.  
The most important ambiguity to resolve now is that music/albums is v2; this phase ships movies as free-track plus TV entries stored but only ranked in paid track to honor the spec without killing v1 utility.  
The biggest risk is trust collapse from awkward or arbitrary comparisons, so ranking must stay bounded, incremental, and transparent enough that users feel “this got better with each tap” rather than trapped in random admin tasks.  
Success has to be measurable: first-time users should reach a populated shareable top-10 in no more than ~10 added titles and under 5 minutes, with >85% of added titles ranked automatically, no crashes or blocked flows without network, successful local persistence across relaunch, at least one accessible comparison path (VoiceOver + Dynamic Type), and a working offline path where placeholder art still renders and sharing/export still works.  
That last constraint must be preserved in every build decision: if adding anything makes first-run too instruction-heavy or blocks the free loop, it’s out.

**Claude (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

Let me give my independent read on this, thinking about it as the actual screens and flows someone will tap through, not just the pitch.

The product promise I'd commit to: FaveOff turns a handful of "which was better" taps into a real, trustworthy top 10 of the movies you've watched, and hands you back something worth sending to a group chat. If I had to say it to someone at a party in one breath: it's the app that makes you pick, so you finally know your own favorite movies.

Primary user and scenario: someone who just finished watching something, or is scrolling a few days later, and thinks "actually, was that better than [other thing]?" — not a completionist logging every viewing, not someone building an archive. They open the app maybe once a week, add 2-4 things they watched recently, get shown a few quick match-ups, and check where their list stands. The scenario that matters most for scope isn't "onboarding with 20 titles at once" — it's the steady-state loop of "I watched one thing, let me place it," because that's what happens 500 times over the life of the app versus once at install.

Core loop, concretely as screens: Add (type a title, pick from local suggestions or fall back to manual entry with a color-block poster) → Compare (one or a small bounded number of head-to-head screens where you tap the better one, or say "skip/haven't thought about it") → Placed (the new title drops into the ranked list at its resolved position, with a short "here's where it landed" beat — this confirmation moment is the payoff and I don't think the spec history has fully named it as its own screen state) → Browse (scrollable ranked list, filterable) → Share (Taste Card render + ShareLink). The thing I want to flag now, before anyone locks a wireframe: "Placed" needs to exist as a distinct, small celebratory state — even just a card that says "#4 of 12, ahead of X, behind Y" — because without a visible payoff after 2-4 comparisons, the loop is just work with no reward, and that's exactly the abandonment mode the invalidation criteria is worried about. If we skip straight from the last comparison tap back into the plain list view with no acknowledgment, we've built the boring version of this app.

Hard scope boundaries I'd defend: v1 is movies-and-TV-addable but movies-only-ranked-for-free (already decided), one taste-ranking track surfaced at a time in the UI (no split-screen "compare across categories"), no editing/reordering the list by hand — the whole pitch is that you don't get to fudge your own ranking, the comparisons are the only lever, and letting people drag-reorder would undercut the "this is your real taste, not your curated image" promise in the App Store positioning. No notes/reviews/tags beyond what's needed for genre/decade filtering. No onboarding tutorial screens that require reading — if the comparison screen itself doesn't make the two-buttons-pick-one interaction self-evident, that's a design failure to fix, not something to patch with an explainer.

Where I think there's real ambiguity nobody's resolved yet: what happens when someone re-opens the app with zero pending comparisons because their whole library is fully placed? Is there a "re-litigate an old match-up" affordance, or does the app just sit there showing the list with an "add something to keep ranking" prompt? I'd argue for the latter — no forced re-comparison, because manufacturing artificial comparisons to keep engagement up cuts against the "your real opinion" premise and risks feeling gamed. The empty/steady-state screen when there's nothing to compare needs to be a first-class state, not an afterthought, because for an active user this might be the most common thing they see.

Measurable success criteria I'd hold this to: a new user with zero titles can reach a shareable card (even a 3-5 item one, doesn't need to be a full 10) in under 3 minutes of actual interaction time; the ranking after N comparisons should be stable — re-showing the same two titles shouldn't ever produce a contradictory forced order; and the specific abandonment signal called out in the invalidation criteria (quits after "a handful" of comparisons, never reaches a shareable list) needs an actual operational definition before we can call the MVP validated — I'd propose: if a majority of first-session users don't complete at least 5 comparisons, that's the tedious-flow failure state, and the fix path is smaller default list size / faster convergence, not more onboarding polish.

The biggest risk to my own framing: I'm pushing hard against any manual reordering or tutorial screens in the name of "purity of the mechanic," and that's a bet that the comparison screen's affordances are strong enough to be self-teaching. If playtesting (or even just a critical look at the wireframe) shows people genuinely don't know what to do on first launch, "no tutorial" becomes a stubborn ideology rather than good design, and I'd rather we name that tradeoff now than discover it after the screens are built.
