**Claude (QA & Risk · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

The thing that worries me most walking into this phase is that the last two phases described the loop in vocabulary that sounds precise ("prestige by donating a wing," "offline progress within an epsilon") but actually left several load-bearing mechanics undefined at the level a QA pass or an engineer would need. I want to surface those gaps as resolved requirements now rather than let them get discovered during architecture.

First: "donate a wing for a permanent multiplier and a fresh cabinet to fill" is ambiguous between two very different systems — a per-wing lock (that wing becomes permanent, uneditable, contributes its multiplier forever, and you open a new blank wing alongside it, museum keeps growing) versus a classic full-reset prestige (the whole museum wipes back to one empty wing in exchange for a permanent multiplier applied everywhere). These have wildly different data models, wildly different emotional stakes, and wildly different overflow-testing requirements. I'd resolve this explicitly right now: prestige is per-wing and additive — completing and donating a wing locks it permanently into the museum's public gallery (still visible, no longer editable) and grants its bonus permanently, while a fresh wing opens for active curation. Nothing is ever wiped. This matters for the "visually proud of what I built" promise from the product brief — a full reset directly contradicts "the museum has to visibly and specifically change based on her choices" if her hour-3 museum periodically vanishes.

Second, the numeric-overflow requirement from the contract ("won't silently overflow or lose precision through two prestige resets") can't stay abstract — I want to lock the representation now: currency and multipliers should be a mantissa/exponent pair (or Decimal capped at a defined magnitude) rather than raw Double, specifically because Double silently loses integer precision above 2^53 and idle games blow past that within days, not weeks, once multipliers compound. If we leave this to "the architecture phase will figure it out," there's a real risk someone picks Double for convenience and the epsilon test from the product brief passes at 7 days but silently fails at 30.

Third, expedition and offline-progress timing has a clock-manipulation edge case that's specific to idle games: a user can set their system clock backward (accidentally, via timezone travel, or deliberately to test/exploit), or forward and back again. If elapsed time is computed as "now minus stored timestamp" with no floor, a backward clock jump produces negative elapsed time, and depending on how that's handled it either crashes, freezes an expedition forever, or — worse — a forward-then-backward jump lets a player fraud themselves free currency by repeatedly nudging the clock. I want a resolved rule: elapsed time for any calculation is clamped to a minimum of zero, and expedition "return" timestamps are stored as absolute dates and re-validated against a monotonic-ish anchor (e.g., also compare against a last-known-good timestamp saved periodically) rather than trusted blindly from `Date()` alone. This doesn't need to be bulletproof against a determined cheater — it's a single-player local game, self-cheating isn't really a threat model — but it does need to not crash or corrupt state, which is a real one.

Fourth, intake tray overflow: if a Patron user runs two expeditions in parallel and closes the app for a week, the tray could receive more specimens than it can hold. What happens? Silently dropping specimens is a data-loss bug from the player's perspective even though nothing "crashed." I'd resolve this as: the tray has a generous but finite cap, and anything that would exceed it while offline is auto-converted to a small currency refund with a clear "your tray was full, N specimens were exchanged for research funding instead" message on next open — never silently discarded, never silently blocked.

Fifth — and this one is a genuine accessibility edge case nobody's named yet — if color is one of the matching axes for set bonuses (named explicitly in the product brief), that's a problem for colorblind players and a non-starter for VoiceOver users unless every color-coded specimen also carries a non-color cue (icon, pattern, or text label) that's the actual source of truth, with color as decoration on top. This needs to be a resolved requirement now, not a design-phase nice-to-have, because retrofitting non-color affordances onto a color-keyed data model after art is built is expensive.

Sixth: placement needs to be non-destructive. If a player rearranges a completed set and breaks a bonus, that's a real emotional stake in a game about curation — I'd resolve that moving a specimen never destroys it; worst case it goes back to an unplaced holding state, and breaking an active set bonus should show an immediate, undo-friendly signal (the multiplier visibly drops the instant the tile leaves the slot, so cause and effect are legible) rather than the loss being discovered later.

On the monetization side, one App Store risk I don't think has been named: the specimens returned by expeditions are described with "rarity," and if any IAP ever lets someone pay directly for a specimen pull or an expedition re-roll, that crosses into loot-box territory and triggers Apple's odds-disclosure requirements — which contradicts the "sell real utility, not randomized cosmetics" identity anyway. I'd make this an explicit exclusion: the Patron tier only unlocks capacity, speed, and a fully-visible exclusive content region — no IAP ever purchases a randomized outcome.

On data loss more broadly: this app has no cloud sync in v1, which the contract already accepts, but the practical consequence is that uninstalling the app or losing the device means the entire museum — the thing the whole product thesis is built around being "visually proud of" — is gone with no recovery path. I don't think that's acceptable to leave totally unaddressed even in a local-first v1; the cheap mitigation is a manual export/import of the save snapshot (share sheet, iCloud Drive file, whatever) using the same versioned Codable format already planned for future sync. That's a few hours of work riding on infrastructure we're building anyway, and it directly protects the thing players are emotionally invested in. I'd flag it as a should-have for this build rather than a hard requirement, but explicitly decided, not silently absent.

Related: Patron subscription restore-purchases will correctly restore entitlement via StoreKit across a reinstall or new device, but the museum itself will not — someone could restore their subscription and find their exclusive region unlocked with nothing in it. That's a real expectation mismatch worth naming as a known limitation with required messaging ("your Patron benefits are restored; your museum only lives on this device for now") rather than something a support ticket discovers first.

Last edge case worth locking in: each wing type poses a "genuinely different placement puzzle" per the product brief, which is good for variety but bad for a 30-90-second session unless the rule for that specific wing is always visible on-screen, not a one-time tutorial tip — a returning player five wings deep won't remember which of six puzzle types they're looking at from memory alone in a few seconds.

The privacy surface here is genuinely small and I don't want to overstate it: no accounts, no PII, no network calls in the core loop, StoreKit handles payment data entirely off-device from our code's perspective, local notifications need permission but carry no personal data. The one privacy-adjacent decision to make explicit is that there's no server-side receipt validation, so a jailbroken device could locally forge a Patron unlock — I'd name that as an accepted limitation of local-first rather than something we try to defeat, since the threat model here is "casual mobile game," not "content requiring DRM."
