**Claude (Design Lead · Visionary) — Round 1**

Here's my first pass, coming at this as the person who has to decide what the app actually looks like when someone opens it.

My strongest instinct is that the home screen should not be a dashboard — it should be the museum itself. Every idle game I can think of defaults to a stat-panel home screen with a row of icons for "shop," "upgrades," "prestige," and that layout quietly tells the player this is a spreadsheet with skins. If the whole thesis is "you built something you're proud to look at," then opening the app should show you the thing you built, immediately, full-bleed — your wings rendered as rooms, donated wings visibly gleaming and finished in the gallery portion, your current wing mid-curation with empty slots visible as gaps in the display cases. Badges overlay on top of this view rather than replacing it: a small indicator on the expedition desk doorway showing "3 returned," a indicator on the intake counter showing "2 uncatalogued." The player's entire 45-second session happens by tapping around inside one continuous space rather than switching between tabs. I think this is the single biggest lever for making Curio feel like a museum instead of an idle-game skin on a museum, and I'd fight to protect it even though it costs more than a plain list-based home screen.

Screen-by-screen, here's what I think the actual inventory needs to be: a Museum Overview (the hub described above, showing active wings + a glimpse of the donated gallery), an Expedition Desk (dispatch screen — shows destination cards with cost/duration, running timers with live countdowns, the second slot visibly present-but-locked for free users with a Patron upsell that shows what's behind it rather than just a padlock glyph), an Intake Tray (queue of returned, uncatalogued specimens, tray-fullness meter so the cap and overflow behavior in the contract are visible, not silent), a Cataloging sheet (one specimen at a time — image, a quick tap-to-guess-category interaction with gentle correction, not a quiz that punishes wrong guesses, and a "place it" handoff into the relevant wing), the Wing Interior itself (the actual puzzle screen — three visually distinct shells for the adjacency grid, the row-based wing, and the rarity-threshold wing, sharing the same tap-select/tap-place interaction primitive but looking and reading like genuinely different rooms, with the wing's matching rule stated as a permanent on-screen plaque, not a tooltip), a Donate Wing confirmation (shows a before/after preview of what locks and what bonus is granted, since this is irreversible and needs real weight, not a casual alert), the Gallery (read-only, browsable, the emotional payoff screen), a Showcase/Share view (chrome-free single-wing render for screenshotting), the Patron Store (three concrete benefits shown as actual previews — the exclusive region rendered as a dim, tantalizing silhouette behind glass rather than a feature bullet list), and Settings (restore purchases, accessibility toggles, export/import if we keep it). First-run onboarding I'd build as an overlay sequence layered on top of the Overview and one Wing Interior rather than a separate walled-off tutorial flow — the first thing a new player does should be indistinguishable from what they'll do on day 30, just with some fingers pointing at things.

The primary flow for a real session: launch into Museum Overview, see what's glowing (returned expeditions, waiting specimens), walk into the Expedition Desk to redispatch, walk to the Intake counter to catalog what's waiting, get routed specimen-by-specimen into whichever wing they belong to for placement, watch a multiplier plaque update live when a set completes, exit. Nothing in that path should require a tab bar; back-navigation is literally "leave the room."

The moment I'd spend the most design budget on, more than any single screen, is the set-completion beat, because it's the only thing that happens on nearly every visit and it's the entire game's emotional core. I want it to read as a small event, not a number tick: the completed display case gets a focused light change, the plaque animates a flip or an ink-fill reveal of the new multiplier, and there's a distinct sound layered on top of the generic UI feedback so it never gets confused with "you tapped a button." With reduced motion on, this becomes a static highlight-and-settle rather than the camera-push/flourish version, but the plaque-flip content change and the distinct sound stay — reduced motion should never mean reduced *information*.

State model, screen by screen, is where I want to be blunt about honesty rather than glossing states as an afterthought: Museum Overview needs a true first-launch empty state (a bare, mostly-empty room with one obvious highlighted action — "dispatch your first expedition" — not a blank screen with no direction), a loading state for the split-second snapshot decode on launch, the normal populated success state, and an error state that has to be designed for real, not hypothetical: if the save snapshot fails to decode (the crash-safe atomic-write case), the player needs a screen that says plainly "we couldn't load your museum, here's what we're doing about it" rather than the app silently starting a new one and lying about continuity. Expedition Desk needs empty (no destinations unlocked yet), loading (dispatch in flight), success (running timers, ready-to-collect states), and error (insufficient currency or full slots, both with a clear reason attached to the disabled action, not just a grayed-out button). Intake Tray needs empty (nothing to catalog), loading, success (the queue), and its "error" state is actually the overflow-refund message — I'd treat that explicitly as this screen's error/edge state, since it's the one place data-loss anxiety could creep in and it needs a designed, reassuring surface, not a toast that vanishes. Cataloging needs empty (nothing left in queue, dismiss back to Overview), loading (rare, but the classification-confirm beat), success, and there isn't really a failure mode here worth inventing one for. Wing Interior needs empty (freshly opened wing, all slots bare, rule plaque prominent since this is the first thing a player needs to understand), loading, success (partially or fully filled), and an edge state for a broken set (the visible multiplier-drop moment) which I'd treat as its own designed state, not a subset of success. Gallery needs empty (no donated wings yet — first prestige hasn't happened) and success; no real error state exists once data's already validated on load. Patron Store needs loading (products fetching from StoreKit), success, and a real error state for StoreKit unavailable/network-down during purchase, plus the specific restore-mismatch message already locked in prior phases ("your Patron benefits are restored; your museum only lives on this device").

Visual direction: I want this to feel like brass, glass, and old wood under warm directional light — a real cabinet of curiosities, not a flat vector-icon mobile game. Specimen cards read like hand-typed museum labels (a slightly imperfect serif or typewriter-adjacent display face for labels, clean system font for functional UI text so Dynamic Type doesn't fight the aesthetic). Rarity and category signals need to be legible through shape/icon/label first and color second per the standing requirement — I'd actually lean into that as a visual strength rather than a compliance checkbox: each specimen gets a distinct pictographic glyph baked into its card, and color becomes an accent wash behind that glyph, so a colorblind player and a sighted player are looking at functionally the same information.

The risk I'll name against my own strongest idea: the museum-as-navigable-space home screen is real engineering cost and real accessibility cost if built as a freeform pannable canvas — custom spatial layouts are exactly the kind of thing that's brutal to make VoiceOver-navigable in a sane reading order, and this app has a hard, tested VoiceOver requirement, not an aspirational one. My fallback, which I'd accept without feeling like we lost the idea: build the Overview as a vertically scrolling sequence of illustrated wing "rooms" (List-based under the hood, fully linear VoiceOver order, each wing rendered as a wide illustrated card you tap into) rather than a true free-pan map. It keeps the "you're looking at your museum, not a dashboard" feeling and keeps VoiceOver trivial, at the cost of losing literal spatial navigation — I think that trade is correct and I'd make it now rather than discover the accessibility cost three weeks into building a canvas-based map.

The one detail I most want preserved into the final output no matter how the debate goes: the home screen shows the museum, not a menu of buttons to get to the museum. If that gets negotiated away for engineering convenience, we've quietly rebuilt the generic idle-game dashboard we spent five phases trying not to build.
