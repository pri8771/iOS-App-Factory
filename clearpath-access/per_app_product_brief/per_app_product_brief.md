**Codex (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Clearpath Access should be built for people whose walking route can fail because of one bad segment, not because the destination is far. The first user is a wheelchair user planning a short urban trip to something necessary like a clinic, pharmacy, office, or transit stop. The second user is a caregiver or family member planning on someone else’s behalf. That second user matters because it points directly at paid value: the app is not just “better maps,” it is a confidence tool for people who cannot afford to guess wrong.

The free product needs to do one thing well: let someone enter a destination, fetch Apple walking routes, score them against a small on-device obstacle dataset, explain the safest option in plain language, and let the user add a new obstacle report that immediately changes future route recommendations on that device. If that loop is not fast and legible, the app has no business existing. The route recommendation cannot be a black box. It needs to say things like “Recommended because this path avoids reported stairs and has one fewer severe barrier.” Users will keep the app only if it reliably reduces unpleasant surprises before they leave, not because it has a map and pins.

The paid product should be a subscription built around planning and trust, not cosmetics. The strongest MVP subscription is caregiver route sharing plus saved accessibility profiles. A subscriber can save a rider profile like manual wheelchair, walker, stroller, or low-step tolerance, save frequent destinations, and share route plans and obstacle notes with one or more family members or aides. That is real utility people will pay for because it removes repeated coordination work and reduces risk for recurring trips. A good premium extension is downloadable local coverage packs with curated seed data for specific districts, campuses, or medical corridors, but I would not make that the only paid hook because it depends too much on content expansion. The subscription has to unlock function: shared planning, profile-based route scoring, recurring route presets, and trip confidence for people other than the phone owner.

The competitive wedge is not “we have accessibility data.” Big map apps can claim broad coverage and still fail on the exact block that matters. The wedge is narrower and more defensible: Clearpath Access is built around explainable accessibility friction, not generic ETA optimization. It scores routes using a constrained, auditable obstacle vocabulary, report severity, and recency, then tells the user why a route is safer. That is meaningfully different from general-purpose navigation and more buildable than pretending to own a full accessibility-grade pedestrian graph.

The growth mechanism is niche but real. This is not a mass viral social app. Growth comes from repeated use in high-need corridors and from caregiver invitation. If one person uses the app to plan trips for a parent, partner, patient, or child, they naturally pull another person into the product. The other organic loop is local advocacy and institution adoption: rehab centers, accessibility offices, senior living communities, university disability services, and neighborhood mobility groups can recommend it because it is useful even before network effects arrive. That is the right kind of growth here: trust-based distribution in small, relevant communities.

Local-first has to be strict in v1. Reports, saved destinations, profiles, and route history live on-device and the app works without an account. Seeded sample obstacles are bundled so the experience is not empty, but the UI must be explicit that these are local or bundled reports, not live citywide community coverage. Cloud comes later as a sync layer, not a dependency: the data model should already use stable IDs, timestamps, source metadata, verification state, and ownership fields so CloudKit or a custom backend can later sync reports, shared routes, and caregiver access without rewriting the domain. The product story for cloud should be additive: cross-device sync, family collaboration, verified partner layers, and moderated report sharing.

The biggest risk is false confidence. If the app looks more authoritative than the data deserves, it becomes unsafe and untrustworthy. The product brief must preserve three things to avoid that failure. First, the app is a route-confidence tool, not a guarantee. Second, obstacle data is user-submitted or bundled and unverified unless explicitly marked otherwise. Third, the recommendation is only as good as the known reports in that corridor, so empty coverage and stale data have to be shown honestly. If those constraints survive later phases, this app deserves to exist because it solves a painful, recurring decision with more honesty and more usefulness than general map products do.

**Claude (Product Strategist · User Advocate) — Round 1**

I'll open with who this is actually for, because if I get vague here everything downstream gets vague too. The user is a wheelchair or rollator user in a mid-size city — not a tourist, not an abstract "person with a disability," but someone with a standing list of three or four destinations that matter: their clinic, their pharmacy, a specific transit stop, maybe a parent's or partner's place. They already know their neighborhood better than any map ever will. What they don't have is a way to check, before they leave the house, whether today's route to that clinic still has the temporary ramp closure they hit last month, or whether a new route someone else logged around the construction on 5th is actually better than the one they've been taking out of habit. The use case is pre-trip confidence, not in-trip navigation. If I imagine this person standing at their door with their coat on, phone in hand, the app's job is to answer one question in under ten seconds: "is my usual way still good, or should I go around?"

Paid value has to come from something Apple Maps structurally cannot offer, because "better walking directions" alone will never clear a subscription bar — free is right there on the same phone. The wedge is that Apple Maps has zero concept of accessibility friction and never will, because it's not their business to model curb cuts and stair obstacles. So the free tier of Clearpath is: one destination at a time, route with obstacle overlay, ability to report. That's useful but it's a utility, not yet a reason to pay monthly. The paid tier is where this becomes a tool for people who plan mobility around other people, not just themselves: saved accessible-route profiles for your recurring destinations (so "route to clinic" is a one-tap favorite that always reflects the latest obstacle data, not a fresh search every time), and caregiver/family route sharing — a parent, aide, or adult child can see the same obstacle-aware route their family member is planning, get the same plain-language explanation, and optionally get notified if a saved route's obstacle picture changes (a new report just got added to a route someone relies on weekly). That second piece is the real subscription engine, because it turns a single-player utility into something with a second stakeholder who has both money and motivation — an adult child paying $6-8/month to feel like they have a small window into a parent's day-to-day mobility risk is a completely different, much stronger willingness-to-pay than "pay to see nicer obstacle icons."

The core loop for the free experience has to stay exactly as tight as the earlier phases already locked it: pick a destination, get a route scored on obstacle friction with a one-line plain-English reason, inspect the flagged segment, optionally report something new, watch that report immediately change the score if you re-request. I'd add one more beat that I think is currently missing and matters a lot for retention: after a trip-relevant route is requested, let the user save it as a named favorite with one tap ("Route to Dr. Patel's office"), because the habitual nature of this user's trips is exactly what turns a one-off utility into a daily-open app. Nobody opens a fresh navigation app every day for the same four walks; they open a saved shortcut. That favorite-route object is also, not coincidentally, the natural unit the caregiver-sharing subscription feature hangs off of later — one saved route, one share target, one "this route changed" notification.

The competitive wedge is explainability plus a self-reinforcing personal data loop, not coverage. I want to be honest with myself about the risk here: without real crowd density, a skeptical user's first reaction on opening the app in an unfamiliar area will be an empty map, and an empty map next to a promise of "obstacle-aware routing" reads as vaporware. The way to defuse that isn't to fake data, it's to make the empty state itself the pitch — first launch should seed a small, clearly-labeled sample obstacle set in one bounded demo area (a specific few-block corridor, presented as "starter data for [neighborhood]," never dressed up as live activity), and everywhere else should say plainly "no reports yet here — be the first" rather than showing nothing and letting the user infer the app is broken. The real differentiation shows up over the first few weeks of a single user's own use: their own reports accumulating on their own regular routes is what makes the app get visibly smarter for them specifically, which no generic maps app will ever do, because generic maps apps aren't built to let you teach them your own path constraints.

Growth here is niche, not viral in the broad sense, and I'd resist any pressure to bolt on a social feed to manufacture virality — that directly conflicts with the no-fake-community-activity boundary we already locked. The realistic mechanism is caregiver and support-network word of mouth: this is exactly the kind of tool that gets shared inside a Facebook group for parents of kids with CP, a local independent-living center's newsletter, or a physical therapist recommending it to patients — one credible source recommends it to fifty people who all have the same three trips they worry about. That's small-scale but high-intent, and it's a much better growth shape for this app than trying to go broad, because broad users with no mobility constraint have no reason to open it twice.

Local-first has to mean the free tier is fully real without touching a network beyond MapKit's own routing calls — SwiftData holding obstacle reports, favorite routes, and report history, all on-device, no account required to use the core loop at all. The cloud-ready seam is the account layer that the paid tier needs anyway: a lightweight CloudKit-backed identity for exactly two things — syncing a user's own favorites/reports across their own devices, and the caregiver-sharing link, which is inherently a two-party feature and can't be faked locally. That's a clean boundary: free tier never needs an account or the network beyond routing; paid tier's account requirement is justified by a feature that's actually impossible without it, not a paywall bolted onto local functionality that used to be free.

What keeps someone opening this app weeks later isn't the map, it's the accumulating sense that it knows their walks — the favorite route to the clinic showing "no new issues since your last report" is a small, quiet moment of reassurance that a generic maps app structurally cannot produce, and that's the thing I'd protect hardest against feature creep: every future addition should be judged by whether it deepens that specific feeling for this specific user, not by whether it makes the app look more full-featured.
