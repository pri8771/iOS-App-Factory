### Round 1


**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

The cleanest version of this app is not “accessible maps.” It is: a person with mobility constraints needs to get from A to B on foot, and before they leave they want to know which of two or three plausible walking routes is least likely to contain a known problem. That framing matters because it keeps the app out of impossible territory. If we describe it as general navigation, we invite scope creep into live turn-by-turn, nationwide trust, and routing intelligence we cannot honestly support in an MVP.

So the one-sentence promise I would lock is: Clearpath Access helps mobility-constrained pedestrians choose the least risky walking route by combining Apple walking directions with locally stored obstacle reports and plain-language accessibility warnings.

The primary user is someone whose walking route can fail because of one bad segment: wheelchair users first, then cane users, rollator users, and caregivers planning on someone else’s behalf. The core scenario is not casual exploration. It is “I need to get to this clinic, store, office, or transit stop, and I need to know whether this route has stairs, closure, rough surface, or curb issues before I commit.” That gives the app a serious tone and a very specific UX hierarchy: destination first, route comparison second, obstacle trust/explanation third, reporting flow fourth.

The core loop should stay brutally simple. Enter or choose a destination. Fetch a walking route. Overlay known nearby obstacles and compute an accessibility friction score. Present a recommended route with a plain-English reason like “2 fewer high-severity obstacles and no reported stairs.” Let the user inspect flagged segments. If they encounter a new problem, they can submit a report in a fast flow with obstacle type, severity, optional note, and optional photo if that is low-risk to include later. Then that report immediately affects future route guidance on-device. If the app cannot do that loop cleanly, it does not have an MVP yet.

The hard scope boundary I want preserved is that this is a route-confidence tool, not a real-time navigation platform. No pretending we have authoritative accessibility coverage. No fake “community activity” feed. No social layer. No broad place discovery. No custom pedestrian graph. No promise that the app can find a fully accessible path everywhere. The UX has to say, in effect, “here is what is known locally, here is what is unverified, and here is why this route is the safer bet among available options.” That honesty is part of the product, not legal cleanup.

The biggest risk in this direction is that the app could still feel hollow if the map opens and there is not enough useful data to influence a route. Seed data helps, but seeded data alone can also feel fake if the coverage pattern is random. So the MVP needs a deliberately bounded geography or corridor strategy and a first-run explanation that makes the limits feel intentional: for example, a defined neighborhood, campus district, or downtown zone with meaningful starter reports. If we skip that, the user experience collapses into “pretty map, no trust.”

Another risk is that route scoring can become opaque or manipulative. If we tell users a route is “best” without showing why, we lose trust immediately. The recommendation has to be inspectable: number of obstacles, severity, freshness, and maybe whether the issue sits directly on the path or merely nearby. The detail the final output must preserve is that explanation layer. The differentiator is not just that we score routes differently; it is that the user can understand the score in two seconds.

Measurable success criteria should be concrete. A user can search for or select a destination and receive at least one walking route on a map. If there are relevant stored obstacles intersecting that route, the route is visibly flagged and the app explains the impact in plain language. A user can submit an obstacle report in under 30 seconds, and that report persists across relaunches and changes later route recommendations. The app handles no-permission, no-route, no-nearby-obstacles, and geocoding failure states cleanly. VoiceOver can identify the route summary, warnings, and reporting actions without dead ends. If those things work, we have a real product promise. If not, we have a concept demo.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Here's my read on this, coming at it from what could break and what's actually buildable in one pass.

The one-sentence promise I'd lock in is: Clearpath Access helps a pedestrian with mobility constraints plan a walking route that accounts for known physical obstacles, and lets them report new ones so their own future routes get smarter — not a live crowd network, a personal-plus-seeded trust layer. I'd resist any temptation to word it more ambitiously than that, because every extra word of ambition ("real-time," "crowd-verified," "smart accessible routing") is a promise QA will later have to prove false.

Primary user: someone who uses a wheelchair, walker, cane, or pushes a stroller, and for whom the shortest route and the usable route are frequently different things. The concrete scenario is mundane and that's the point — they need to get from a transit stop to a clinic, or from home to a pharmacy, and the thing that ruins their day isn't distance, it's discovering a set of stairs or a torn-up sidewalk mid-route with no way to have known in advance. The secondary user worth naming explicitly, even if we don't build for them yet, is a caregiver or family member planning on someone else's behalf — because that's where the monetization hook lives later, and if we don't name the user now we'll accidentally design against them.

Core loop, stated as plainly as I can: pick a destination, get a route that's scored not just on time/distance but on obstacle friction, see why it was scored that way in plain language, walk it (or not — we're not doing live GPS tracking), and optionally report something you encountered so the next route request for you (and only you, in this MVP) is better informed. That loop has to close within a single app session for it to feel real in a demo: request route → see flagged obstacle → see alternate scored route → report a new obstacle → request the same route again → see the new report reflected. If that six-step loop doesn't work end to end on a fresh install, nothing else in this build matters.

Now the part I want to be the skeptic about. The biggest risk to this specific phase's output is scope creep disguised as precision — someone will want to define "accessibility-aware" in a way that implies terrain analysis, slope data, sidewalk width, curb ramp databases. None of that exists as a free, on-device data source, and building it is a research project, not an MVP feature. My scope boundary: the app reasons over a small, fixed set of obstacle types a person can self-report (steps/stairs with no ramp, steep grade, uneven/broken surface, construction/blocked path, no curb cut, temporary hazard), each with a severity, and that's the entire vocabulary the scoring engine understands. Anything the obstacle taxonomy can't express doesn't exist in this app, and I'd rather have five obstacle types that are fully wired through reporting → persistence → scoring → route explanation than fifteen types where half are decorative.

Second scope boundary, and this one's about honesty rather than effort: because there's no real crowd data source, the "measurable" part of success can't be "routes are accurate" — nobody can verify that against ground truth in a QA pass. What I can actually measure is behavioral: does a route that crosses a seeded/reported obstacle visibly differ in its score and explanation from one that doesn't; does a newly submitted report change the score of a route requested immediately after, in the same session, without a restart; does data survive a force-quit and relaunch; does the app degrade honestly (not crash, not silently show nothing) when location permission is denied, when no route is found, or when the device is offline. Those are pass/fail checks a build-verification phase can actually run, versus "the routing feels smart," which nobody can adjudicate.

Third boundary: single trip planning only, one route at a time, no saved trip history dashboards, no multi-stop itineraries, no live position tracking during the walk. The temptation with a navigation app is to build the whole navigation experience; the pragmatic cut is that this app answers "which of these ways should I go and why" — it does not attempt to be a turn-by-turn companion for the walk itself. If someone later wants live tracking, that's a v2 conversation, not something to half-build now and leave broken.

The detail I most want preserved into later phases, because it's the thing most likely to get quietly eroded under design and implementation pressure: the app must always be honest with the user about what it doesn't know. Empty map areas, unreported obstacles, and stale reports are not corner cases to paper over with confident-looking UI — they're core to the product's credibility, and the failure mode I'd bet money on if we don't name it now is a beautiful map screen that implies far more certainty about safety than the underlying data supports.
