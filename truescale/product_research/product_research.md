Truescale captures site issues faster than camera-plus-notes and turns them into structured, client-ready reports — with true-to-scale AR measurement attached to every photo on supported devices.

Independent contractors, home inspectors, property managers, insurance adjusters, and punch-list-oriented design/renovation professionals documenting issues on-site from an iPhone.

## Painful Moment Solved

Field documentation today means switching between a camera, a separate measuring tool, and a notes app, then rebuilding a structured report at a desk — or paying for team-collaboration SaaS (CompanyCam, Fieldwire) that bills per seat and assumes a company account even for a solo operator.

## Why It Is Different

The foundation is a complete, fast, structured capture-and-report tool that already beats camera-plus-notes and undercuts per-seat SaaS pricing for solo users, fully functional with manual measurement entry alone. On top of that, on supported hardware it attaches a true-to-scale AR/LiDAR measurement directly to each photo — a capability gap no competitor offers at any price, never a dependency the app needs to function.

## Why Users Would Pay

Free tier caps active projects per month; paid tier removes the cap and unlocks custom report branding, richer export formats, and AR-measured capture on supported devices — ongoing value is unlimited professional output plus a precision feature no free tool offers.

## Subscription Model

$14.99/mo or $119/yr Pro: unlimited projects, custom report branding, advanced PDF/CSV export, AR measurement capture, priority updates. Free tier: 2 active projects/month, manual measurement entry only.

## Local-First MVP Scope

Offline project creation, area/room grouping, issue capture with photo plus structured metadata (severity, status), manual measurement entry as the default input, optional AR/LiDAR measurement on supported devices, structured branded PDF report generation, project library, filtering and history — all on-device.

## Future Backend Roadmap

Optional cloud backup/sync for device loss protection; team collaboration, client portals, and signed reports later as explicit opt-ins without changing the local project/observation model.

## AR / Local ML / Local LLM Fit

AR measurement is an additive precision layer, not a dependency — the app is complete and production-ready without it. Where available (iPhone 12 Pro+), it's a genuine capability gap versus every existing competitor. No LLM needed for v1.

## Design Direction

Crisp, technical, trustworthy, 'instrument panel' feel — strong hierarchy, large touch targets, bright-light legibility, glove-friendly one-handed capture.

## App Store Positioning

The fastest way to document site issues and send clean, accurately measured reports from your iPhone — no team account required.

## Invalidation Criteria

Kill or descope AR if measurement accuracy can't be validated within professional tolerance — the base app still stands alone. Kill the whole product if structured capture isn't clearly faster/cleaner than camera-plus-notes, or reports feel too amateur to send to a client.

**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

The likely buyer is not “inspectors” as a broad category. It is a solo or very small-crew field professional who already documents punch items, damage, deficiencies, or install issues from an iPhone and then burns time reconstructing that into something presentable later. The strongest first-use contexts are residential renovation walkthroughs, rental turnover/property management, insurance damage documentation, and home inspection follow-up lists where speed matters more than a formal compliance template. That matters because these users are usually standing, moving, outdoors or in poor lighting, often one-handed, sometimes gloved, and they do not want to think in software terms like records, forms, or workflows. They want “project, room, snap, tag, done.”

The real competitor is less a single app and more an ugly habit stack: iPhone camera, tape measure or laser measure, Notes, Photos favorites, and then a desktop PDF hack later. Paid competitors exist, but many of them pull the user toward team collaboration, cloud sync, markup-heavy construction workflows, or seat-based SaaS assumptions. The patterns worth learning from are fast capture, photo-first issue lists, room grouping, and report credibility. The patterns to avoid are enterprise setup friction, giant form schemas, hidden exports, and anything that makes capture feel like filling out a compliance database. The app should feel closer to a field instrument and closer to how Halide or a surveying utility treats capture than how a generic project-management app treats records.

On iPhone, the native opportunity is strong if the UX stays disciplined. Camera capture has to be the center of gravity, not buried behind project detail screens. AR measurement should behave like an optional precision tool layered onto the same capture flow, not a separate mode that fractures the product. Location stamping is useful as quiet metadata, not something the user must manage. PDF export and share sheet are valuable because they let the app finish the job on-device. App Intents are a good fit if they shorten time-to-first-photo, for example “Start project” or “New issue in last-used project,” but only if the core IA is already clean. The first build should assume intermittent connectivity, glare, wet-thumb interaction, and the fact that many users will be on non-Pro iPhones, so the manual measurement path has to feel first-class rather than like degraded mode.

The biggest UX risk is trying to make one observation carry too much structure. If every issue asks for too many fields, the app loses to just taking a photo and typing later. Severity and status need to stay narrow and obvious. Area assignment should be fast and forgiving. Notes should be short and optional at capture time, with editing later. The second big risk is AR trust. Even if the measurement tech works, professionals will distrust it if the UI doesn’t make source and confidence clear. The app cannot present manual and AR values as equivalent without labeling how the number was obtained. The third risk is report credibility. If the exported PDF looks like a mobile app dump instead of a document a contractor can send to a client, the whole value proposition collapses. A fourth risk is library sprawl: once projects, rooms, statuses, and issue history accumulate, the browsing model can get messy fast unless filtering and grouping are decided early.

My working assumptions are these: most users will create a project for a site visit or job, then create a handful of areas, then capture a burst of issues in sequence; the free-tier cap should be enforced on concurrently active projects, not a calendar counter; the first export format that matters is a polished PDF, not CSV; and AR-capable devices are a minority path in v1, so the app must still feel premium without them. I’m also assuming the winning capture flow is area-first, because rooms/areas are how users mentally organize findings on site, but that needs to stay lightweight enough that a user can add an area inline during capture rather than backing out to manage structure.

For the first build, I would bias hard toward a small number of excellent screens: a project library that clearly shows active versus archived work, a project workspace grouped by area, a fast capture screen that starts with photo and only exposes the minimum tagging needed, an issue detail/edit screen for cleanup after the fact, and an export/report preview flow that proves the output is client-ready. The key interaction detail to preserve is that manual and AR measurement must converge into the same observation model and the same report layout. If the app creates two different mental models of “issue capture,” it will feel bolted together. The other detail that must survive this phase is that speed is the product: any screen, field, or transition that does not help the user produce a cleaner report faster should be cut before v1.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Reading this straight through the field-use lens rather than the feature list: the actual operating environment for this app is a job site, not a desk, and that changes what "risk" means here. The user is often one-handed (other hand holding a ladder, flashlight, or clipboard), possibly gloved, in direct sun where screens wash out, moving between rooms/areas with spotty or zero connectivity, and getting interrupted mid-task by phone calls, clients, or just walking to the next room. Any assumption that a capture flow can be leisurely or that state can live only in memory until "save" is pressed is wrong for this context.

On comparable apps: CompanyCam is the closest real analog for the photo-first, tag-by-project structure, and its lesson is that the photo-plus-tag loop has to be nearly zero-friction because volume is high (dozens of photos per site visit). Fieldwire's lesson is the opposite — it shows what happens when a punch-list tool tries to be a full project-management platform, which is exactly the "scope can bloat into inspection/compliance software" trap this spec already warns about. HomeGauge/Spectora are inspection-report tools that lean on heavy structured templates and desk-based finishing — useful as a negative example, since if Truescale's report generation starts requiring desk cleanup before sending, the core promise of eliminating that step is dead. Magicplan/Canvas are the nearest AR-measurement precedent, but they're built around scan-to-floorplan, a different product surface than "attach one true-to-scale measurement to one photo" — worth noting because it means there isn't a ready-made UX pattern to copy for this specific interaction; it has to be designed from scratch and kept simple.

Platform-side, the native opportunities that actually matter for this use case: App Intents/Shortcuts for a zero-navigation "start new project" or "new issue" entry point matters more here than it would in a typical app, because the "faster than camera-plus-notes" claim is measured in taps and the normal project→area→issue navigation chain will not feel fast on its own. PDFKit and the share sheet are straightforward wins. Location stamping via CoreLocation is nice-to-have but must degrade invisibly — plenty of interior job sites (basements, windowless commercial spaces) will have poor or no GPS fix, and that cannot block or delay capture.

Now the risks I actually want on the record before build starts, because they're concrete failure modes, not vague concerns. First: this is a fully local, no-backup-in-v1 app storing photos that are the entire work product of a client-billable site visit — if the phone is lost, dropped in water, or the app has a data-loss bug, a user loses a full day's documentation with zero recovery path, and that's a materially worse failure mode than almost any SaaS competitor has, since they at least have server-side copies. That's an accepted tradeoff per the roadmap (cloud backup is explicitly future work), but it means local persistence robustness and autosave-on-every-step are not "nice to have" — they're the whole safety net. Second: draft persistence during an interrupted capture is a real edge case, not a hypothetical — a phone call or a client walking up mid-tag-selection is normal in this workflow, and losing an in-progress photo+note because the app got backgrounded would directly contradict the "faster and cleaner than camera-plus-notes" claim, since camera-plus-notes at least never loses your photo. Third: AR/LiDAR tracking degrades in the exact conditions this app will be used in — dim utility rooms, textureless drywall, crawlspaces — so the AR path needs a real "tracking lost, here's your fallback" state, not just a device-capability check; a capability check alone tells you the phone can theoretically do AR, not that it can do AR in this specific room right now. Fourth, and I want this named plainly: there is no physical LiDAR-equipped device and no camera/AR support in the Simulator in this build environment, so verification of the entire camera and AR capture path in this loop is limited to code-path/logic review and injectable-service testing, not actual on-device behavior. That's consistent with what the prompt-contract phase already flagged, but it's worth restating here because it directly shapes what "comparable apps" research can validate — I can reason about UX patterns from competitor apps, but I can't observe real bright-sunlight legibility or real one-handed reachability in this environment, so those need to be design principles applied carefully, not claims verified against a device screen. Fifth: free-tier report output needs to still look credible enough to send to a client, or the paywall guts the exact "prove it's better than camera-plus-notes" moment that has to happen before anyone pays — custom branding can be the paid differentiator, but a stripped-down free report that looks amateur would violate the phase-one contract's own invalidation criterion.

For implications on the first build: treat the AR module as a genuinely separable capability plugged into a measurement-input abstraction, not a parallel code path duplicated through the app, so a "descope AR" decision later is a deletion, not a rewrite. Build autosave/draft persistence into the issue-capture flow from the start, using SwiftData (or equivalent) as the source of truth from the very first screen rather than in-memory state that gets persisted "later" — because the interruption risk above is a day-one concern, not a v2 hardening pass. Get photo storage sizing right immediately (sensible downsampling/compression for stored images) since there's no cloud offload valve in v1 and storage growth is unbounded across projects otherwise. And build the quickest possible "new issue" entry point (even a minimal App Intent) into MVP scope itself, because "faster than camera-plus-notes" is a testable claim about tap count and navigation depth, not a vibe — if the only way to start an issue is drilling into a project, then an area, then a screen, then the camera, the core promise is unproven regardless of how nice the report looks at the end.
