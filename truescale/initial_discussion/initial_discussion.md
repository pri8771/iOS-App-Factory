Free tier caps active projects per month; paid tier removes the cap and unlocks custom report branding, richer export formats, and AR-measured capture on supported devices — ongoing value is unlimited professional output plus a precision feature no free tool offers.

Optional cloud backup/sync for device loss protection; team collaboration, client portals, and signed reports later as explicit opt-ins without changing the local project/observation model.

**Codex (Product Strategist · User Advocate) — Round 1**

Truescale’s product promise should be tightened to this: the fastest way for a solo field professional to turn on-site issues into a client-ready report from an iPhone, with every finding captured as a photo-backed record and measured either manually or, on supported devices, with AR.

The primary user I’m keeping in the room is a solo contractor, inspector, or property manager walking a site with one hand free and no patience for software ceremony. The scenario is not “later at a desk.” It is standing in a bright driveway, a dim mechanical room, or a half-finished bathroom, needing to document a problem before moving on. If the app makes that person think too much, tap too much, or clean up too much later, it has already lost to Camera plus Notes.

The core loop needs to be brutally simple: open or start a project, pick or add an area, capture a photo, assign severity and status, attach a short note and measurement, then move immediately to the next issue. After the walk, the user reviews by area or urgency and exports a report that is already credible enough to send. That means capture speed is the product, not just a UX preference, and report quality is the proof that the speed didn’t come at the cost of professionalism.

The hard scope boundary I would hold is this: v1 is a solo documentation tool, not inspection software and not team software. No backend, no collaboration, no client portal, no signatures, no sprawling checklist taxonomy, no compliance templates, no task-management detour. Severity, status, area, note, photo, and measurement are enough. Manual measurement is first-class and complete on every device. AR is a premium layer, not the identity of the product, and it should disappear cleanly wherever support or trust is weak.

The measurable success criteria should be concrete. A new user can create a project, add an area, capture several issues, relaunch the app, and still find everything intact offline. They can export a report from the phone without desk cleanup. Unsupported devices never hit a dead end because manual measurement always works. The free tier still produces a report that feels sendable. Most importantly, the capture flow should feel meaningfully faster and cleaner than the user’s current camera-plus-notes habit; if it doesn’t, the app is strategically wrong even if every feature technically exists.

The biggest risk in this direction is pretending AR is the wedge when the real wedge is structured speed. If AR adds hesitation, unclear trust, or branching complexity, it weakens the app instead of differentiating it. The detail the final phase output must preserve is that Truescale wins or loses on the manual path first. AR can make Pro more valuable, but it cannot be the thing the whole app leans on.

**Claude (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

Looking at this from the screens-and-flows side: the product promise I'd commit to is "Truescale turns a walk-through into a client-ready report before you leave the site — snap, tag, and it's done, with true-to-scale measurement attached where your hardware allows it." The key word I want to protect is "before you leave the site" — that's the actual differentiator over camera-plus-notes, which requires desk time later. If the report still needs cleanup after the visit, we haven't beaten the incumbent habit no matter how nice the PDF looks.

Primary user and scenario: a solo contractor or inspector walks a property alone, room by room, moving fast, sometimes interrupted. The scenario that should drive every screen decision is not "user sits down to log an issue" — it's "user is already looking at the defect, phone half-raised, and has maybe 8-10 seconds of attention before moving to the next thing or getting pulled away." Every extra decision point (which project, which area, which severity) is a tax on that window. The IA has to front-load context so those decisions are almost pre-selected: last-used project and last-used area should be the default landing state, not a picker you have to navigate through every time.

Core loop as I'd structure it: open app (lands on active project, last area) → tap capture → photo fires immediately (camera is the first thing on screen, not a button you find) → severity/status as single-tap chips, not a form → measurement either skipped, typed, or AR-tapped → done, back to a state ready for the next capture. Then, separately and less time-pressured: browse/filter by area or severity, and generate/export the PDF. Two very different tempos — capture is a sprint, review/export is a walk — and the design has to not force capture-tempo UI onto the review screen or vice versa.

The systems-level risk I want flagged now, because it's easy to lose in a UX conversation: the "core loop" as stated implies state transitions that need explicit handling before anyone writes a line of SwiftUI — what does it mean to capture an issue with no project open yet (cold start), what happens to an in-flight capture when the app is backgrounded mid-photo (this was already flagged as a real risk in product_research and it's a scope boundary, not a nice-to-have), and what's the actual first-launch experience — does a user get a project immediately or do they hit an empty library first? If first-launch dumps someone into an empty project library with a "+" button, that's one more decision before their first success moment, which contradicts the whole speed promise. I'd resolve this now: first launch should let a user create their first project and land directly on the capture screen for it, no intermediate empty states to parse.

Hard scope boundary I want made explicit for this phase specifically, since it's this phase's job to draw it: the core loop is capture → organize → export. Anything that isn't feeding one of those three verbs doesn't belong in v1 — no team features, no compliance templates, no annotation/markup-on-photo tooling (that's a Magicplan/Fieldwire feature creep trap), no multi-project batch export, no report customization beyond a logo/business-info header for Pro. If a feature request in a later phase doesn't reduce time-to-report or increase report credibility, it's out.

Measurable success criteria I'd hold this to, stated so they're actually checkable rather than vibes: (1) from cold app-open to a saved, photographed, tagged issue takes at or under roughly 4 taps on the manual path — project/area already defaulted, photo, severity chip, done; (2) a project with a handful of areas and a dozen issues can produce a PDF that a user could plausibly attach to an email to a client with zero follow-up editing; (3) killing the app mid-capture and reopening it recovers the in-progress draft rather than losing it; (4) every one of project library, area list, capture screen, and issue list has a real empty state with a clear next action, not a blank screen.

The detail I most want carried forward, because it's the one a later architecture phase could quietly break: manual and AR measurement have to terminate in the exact same data shape and the exact same place in the capture flow — a single "measurement" slot that's either manual-entered or AR-captured, never two parallel UI paths the user has to choose between up front. If the capture flow ever asks "manual or AR?" as an early branching decision, that's a design failure — it should be one tappable field that quietly offers AR when the device supports it and tracking is stable, and falls back invisibly otherwise.
