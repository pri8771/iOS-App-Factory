Let me put a stake in the ground on who this is actually for, because "10-12 player cooperative party game" is a category, not a user, and I think the brief falls apart if we don't get concrete here.

The target user is someone I'll call Priya-the-host archetype: late 20s to 40s, runs a recurring social ritual — monthly game night with the same rotating 8-14 friends, a family that does a big holiday gathering every year, or an office social committee person planning a team offsite. She already owns board games, she's tired of the ones that require one person to "run" the game as a judge (that's exhausting and not fun for the judge), and she's hit the ceiling of what physical party games can do with a truly large group — most party games top out around 6-8 before someone's sitting out or the game mechanically breaks. Her actual use case tonight is: everyone's phones are already in their pockets, everyone's already installed the app because she sent a link that morning, and she wants something that turns "twelve people standing in a kitchen" into an experience with a real beginning, middle, and payoff in 20-35 minutes — not a two-hour board game commitment, not an app where six people watch two people play. If the app can't credibly deliver "all twelve people are doing something meaningful in every phase of the round," it has no reason to exist over just playing Codenames with a subset of the group and letting the rest refill drinks.

Core loop, stated the way I think it actually needs to work for that host's night to go well: host opens the app, taps "host a mission," picks a scenario pack (say, a heist, a missing-persons case, a sabotaged spaceship — genre reskins over the same true mechanic), the app auto-generates a nearby session, everyone else opens the app and sees "Priya's Session" and taps join — no typing room codes, no account creation, because if there's ANY friction here, three people are still fumbling with it while the other nine are standing around awkwardly and the energy's already dead before round one starts. Once everyone's in, the host locks the roster and starts the mission: every player gets a private fragment (a name, a timestamp, a location, an object — something concrete, not abstract "clue points"), the shared board shows the mission's central question everyone's trying to answer, and a countdown timer starts. Players don't take turns in the traditional sense — anyone can post a short structured clue about their fragment at any time, but every clue is automatically checked against a hard rule at the type-level: you cannot submit a clue that isn't literally true about your fragment (the app enforces this by having players compose clues from constrained building blocks tied to their actual dealt data, not free text, so lying isn't just discouraged, it's structurally impossible). The board fills up in real time as everyone watches clues land, which is what keeps twelve people looking at their phones and at each other instead of at their phones alone. When the group feels ready, anyone can propose a solution; it takes a majority or full-group confirm to lock it in (that's a deliberate "we decide together" moment, not one person guessing), and then the debrief screen shows every player's full fragment side by side so the room gets the "wait, YOU had that the whole time?" payoff, which is the actual retention hook — that reveal is the moment people decide whether to immediately hit replay with the next scenario.

Where the money is, and I want to be blunt about why this is defensible rather than just "subscriptions because SaaS": the free tier needs to include enough that a host can run a genuinely great first three or four nights, or nobody ever recommends it to their group — I'd ship 2-3 full scenario packs free, because a host who feels bait-and-switched after one free mission will never open the app again, let alone bring eleven friends back. What she'd actually pay for, on a recurring basis, is being the person who always has a fresh mission when the group meets again: a scenario pack subscription (new genre packs monthly — think how Codenames Duet or Exploding Kittens sell expansion decks, except ours can't run out of shelf space), a mission variety that ages well because packs get shuffled/regenerated within fixed content so groups can replay the "sci-fi sabotage" pack without everyone just reciting the solution from memory, and a "campaign mode" where a paid pack links several missions into an ongoing storyline across multiple game nights with persistent stakes — that's real utility, it's the difference between a party game you play once and a hobby you keep coming back to, and it's squarely host-side spend, never a guest paywall, which protects the thing that makes this viral in the first place: anyone can join any session for free, forever, full stop, because the day a guest can't join without paying, hosts stop inviting people and the whole loop dies.

The competitive wedge I actually believe in is this: every adjacent cooperative game (Hanabi, Codenames Duet, Decrypto, Spaceteam, Keep Talking) assumes a small table and live speech as the communication medium, which caps them at 2-6ish players before the "everyone talking over each other" chaos becomes the point (Spaceteam) or the game just stops working (Hanabi past 5). Crosswire is the first of these where the phone isn't a companion to the conversation, it IS the conversation — which is exactly why it can scale to 10-12 without collapsing into noise, and it's also why it works in a loud bar, includes a deaf or hard-of-hearing player on completely equal footing, and travels (no board, no cards to lose, no setup) in a way a physical hidden-info game can't. That's a mechanical delta, not a skin. The risk I'd flag against my own pitch, loudly: silent-and-text-only can very easily tip into "twelve people quietly doing homework on their phones," which is socially dead even if the puzzle logic is flawless — so the brief has to insist on a shared live-updating board (so people react out loud to clues landing in real time, "wait WHO has the warehouse key?"), a visible ticking countdown that creates real urgency, and short haptic/sound pings on new clues, or this becomes a solitaire game that happens to have other people in the room.

For growth: the actual viral mechanic is structural, not a referral gimmick bolted on — every session requires guests to install the app to participate at all, so a single host running one game night is a guaranteed multi-install event every time it happens, and the debrief "reveal" screen is inherently shareable (screenshot-bait: "here's what everyone was secretly holding") without us needing to build any dedicated sharing feature for v1. Niche-first is fine and honestly correct here: this doesn't need to be for everyone, it needs to be the go-to for people who already host recurring group gatherings, and word of mouth inside that niche (game night Discord servers, r/boardgames, corporate culture/HR communities looking for remote-friendly-in-person team building) is more valuable than broad awareness.

Local-first isn't a technical footnote here, it's core to the promise: the entire mission — session creation, roster, fragment dealing, clue exchange, resolution, debrief — runs over MultipeerConnectivity with zero internet dependency, which matters concretely because half of this app's real use cases (basements, backyards, cabins, offices with bad wifi) are places connectivity is unreliable, and "works with zero bars" is a genuine selling point against anything cloud-dependent. The cloud-ready path that doesn't compromise v1: scenario packs themselves are static content bundles that can obviously be fetched from a remote store later without touching the transport layer at all, and the transport is already contracted to sit behind an abstraction — so a v2 "play with a remote friend" mode or leaderboard sync is an additive feature behind that same interface, not a rewrite. I'd resist any temptation in this phase to promise more than that; the local session IS the product, cloud is purely a distribution/content channel bolted on later.

The real reason this deserves to exist, if I had to defend it in one line to a skeptical version of myself: there is no other app right now that lets a host turn "everyone's already got their phone out at a party" into a genuinely well-designed twelve-person cooperative mystery with zero setup, zero lying, and a payoff that gets talked about after the phones go back in pockets — that's a real gap, not a reskin, and it's the thing worth protecting fiercely as we move into design so it doesn't get diluted into "generic clue app."
