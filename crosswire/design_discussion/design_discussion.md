Here's the objection I'd raise against my own plan before someone else has to: tap-and-hold-to-reveal as the sole mechanism for protecting a player's secret fragment is in direct tension with the accessibility requirement that's already locked as a hard requirement. A VoiceOver user can't "tap and hold to peek without anyone noticing" — VoiceOver announces content aloud and requires different interaction gestures entirely, so a sighted-only gesture pattern either breaks VoiceOver users out of the core secrecy mechanic or forces them to out themselves by fumbling with an alternate control in front of the group. I don't think this is a minor accessibility footnote to patch later; it's a real conflict between two things this phase's own instructions call hard requirements, and I want it named now: my proposed resolution is that the reveal control is a proper accessible element (a button/toggle with a clear accessibility label like "Reveal your fragment," not a bare gesture recognizer with no VoiceOver equivalent), with the "protect from onlookers" property achieved instead through auto-hide timing and haptic-confirmed private reveal rather than relying on the gesture itself being secret — VoiceOver users get the same privacy guarantee through timing and audio-routing (private haptic/audio cues) rather than through gesture obscurity, since gesture obscurity was never actually accessible to begin with.

The other risk to my own direction, stated plainly: the corkboard/evidence-board visual metaphor is more design and engineering effort than a plain list, and if we're not disciplined it becomes style dressing over a data model VoiceOver can't parse and Dynamic Type can't reflow. What has to survive into the final design output no matter what gets argued next: the single persistent Mission screen as the core IA decision (board, fragment tray, composer, timer, and solution-confirm all live on one canvas, not spread across navigable screens); the Mission Briefing as a shared simultaneous ritual beat between lobby-lock and fragment-dealing; the accessible-reveal-control fix for the tap-and-hold conflict, since that's a genuine hard-requirement collision, not a nice-to-have; the visible (not hidden) reconnect-in-progress state for other players' grace periods, since that's cheap and turns a resilience rule into shared tension instead of an invisible background process; and the instruction that the visual metaphor must degrade gracefully to a fully accessible linear structure under VoiceOver, Dynamic Type, and Reduce Motion — the boldness lives entirely in the skin, never in what's required to understand the game.
