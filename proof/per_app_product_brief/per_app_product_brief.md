**Claude (Product Strategist · User Advocate) — Round 1**

Picture Maren, who makes small-batch cold-process soap and sells at two farmers markets a month. She's been pricing at $8 a bar because "that's what everyone charges," she just spent a Saturday afternoon measuring lye and oils and cutting 24 bars, and she has genuinely no idea whether that $8 covers her three hours of work. That's who Proof is for — not an Etsy power-seller with a bookkeeper, not someone who already has a spreadsheet, but someone who has never once multiplied their own hourly rate by the time a batch actually took and subtracted it from revenue. The app's entire job is to end one sentence: "I don't actually know if I'm making money." If she opens Proof, builds her soap recipe, logs the Saturday afternoon, and sees "$4.12 per bar, sell at $6.50 minimum for 40% margin" — that's the whole product. Everything else is retention plumbing around that one moment.

The core loop has to respect that she is not going to sit down and pre-populate a material library before she's convinced this is worth her time. First product: name it, add materials as she thinks of them (name, what she paid, how much that bought, how much this batch used — four fields, repeat per material), enter labor hours and her hourly rate (which defaults to something like her state's minimum wage or just a blank she's nudged to fill in, never silently zero), hit calculate, see cost per unit and a suggested floor price. Five minutes, no unit-conversion prompts, no category pickers. The reusable material library only becomes visible value on her second product or second batch of the same product, when Proof says "you already told us lye costs $0.18/oz, want to reuse that?" — that's the moment she feels the app getting smarter about her business specifically, not a form she had to fill out to be allowed to use the app.

Paid value has to be real, not a nag screen. Free gets exactly one product, but that one product must be fully-featured — full cost calc, full suggested price, full trend history for that product's batches — because the free tier is the trust-building demo, not a crippled trial. The moment she wants to track her second product (soap AND candles, or lavender soap AND unscented soap as genuinely separate lines), she hits the wall, and by then she's already seen the app tell her something true and useful about product #1. That's a much stronger conversion trigger than "unlock more features" — it's "I trust this number, now let me use it on my whole business." The reusable material cost library and profit-trend-across-products reporting are the second reason to pay: once she has three or four products, being able to say "coconut oil got 12% more expensive this year, here's every product that touches it" is real business intelligence a spreadsheet person would have to build by hand.

The competitive wedge against "just use a spreadsheet" or a generic small-business costing app is that Proof refuses to let labor be invisible. Every general-purpose costing tool treats labor as optional or an afterthought line item; Proof makes it a first-class, named, always-visible part of the number, and visually calls it out when it's missing or zero — "this is a materials-only estimate, your real cost is higher" — rather than quietly presenting an incomplete number as gospel. That's the honest, ledger-like thing the design brief wants, and it's also the actual product safety mechanism: the single biggest way this app could fail is if users stop entering labor time because it's tedious or because the number it produces is discouraging, and if that happens silently the app becomes a materials calculator that lied about being more. So the UI needs a persistent, unmissable "labor: not included" state, not a footnote.

Growth here is niche and community-shaped, not viral-loop shaped — I wouldn't force a share mechanic that doesn't fit how makers actually behave. The one built-in growth surface that's genuine is the share-sheet export of a priced product card: a clean, ledger-styled card showing "Lavender Soap Bar — true cost $4.12, priced at $7" that a maker might post in a Etsy-seller Facebook group or a craft-fair Discord when someone asks "how do you price your stuff." That's not a growth gimmick bolted on, it's the same artifact she'd want to screenshot for herself, and it happens to be shareable because the whole product is built around a trustworthy number worth showing off. I'd resist adding any referral/invite mechanic beyond that — this user base doesn't compete with each other for the app, they compete for customers, and over-engineering virality here risks feeling exploitative to a target user who is already anxious about money.

Local-first isn't just an architecture preference for this app, it's part of the trust story: her cost data is her actual business margins, and "this lives on my phone, nobody else sees it" should be something the app can honestly say in v1. The cloud-ready path (repository protocol boundary, no real backend yet) matters for later — multi-device sync so she can log a batch on her phone at the market and check trends on an iPad at home — but v1 should never make her feel like she's waiting on a server or that her data isn't fully hers.

What makes her keep the app past the first "aha" isn't the trend chart, it's whether every batch after the first one takes less time to log than the last, because the material library and the recipe she already built are doing the remembering for her — if batch #5 still takes as long as batch #1, she'll stop before the trend line has enough points to mean anything. The biggest risk to this whole brief is the one the spec itself names: if labor time entry feels like homework, she'll stop entering it within a few batches and the number quietly degrades into "materials cost," which is a different, weaker, already-available product. I'd rather ship a slightly rougher labor-entry UI that takes ten seconds (a stepper or a "how long did this take" chip set: 30 min / 1 hr / 2 hr / custom) than a precise-but-slow timer-based flow, because precision that gets abandoned is worth less than a rough number that keeps getting entered.

Proof makes labor a mandatory, always-visible part of the number and visually and unmissably flags "labor not included" whenever it's zero or unset — refusing to let an incomplete materials-only number pass as "true cost." **Why it deserves to exist:** It ends one specific, common, quietly damaging self-deception — sub-cost pricing — for a user group that has no realistic path to figuring this out via a spreadsheet, a bookkeeper, or a general-purpose costing app that treats their own time as free.
