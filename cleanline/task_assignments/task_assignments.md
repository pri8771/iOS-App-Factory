### Round 2


**Codex (Frontend Engineer · Systems Thinker) — Round 2**

Claude’s 29-task cut is the right shape. I don’t want to collapse it back down to a prettier 12-task list, because the dangerous parts of this app are exactly the parts that disappear inside coarse tasks: staging-vs-durable persistence, once-only recovery banners, fake-backed UI progress, and the difference between “vector export exists” and “SVG/PDF correctness is actually testable.” If we make those implicit, we guarantee cross-lane drift. So I’m signing off on the fine-grained backlog, with two constraints tightened from a frontend/state-management angle.

First, the forbidden-zone rule is correct, including the part Claude gave `polish_resilience`: they should not patch another lane’s feature file directly. That sounds annoying until you look at what goes wrong otherwise. If resilience patches `TraceWorkspace` to “fix” a resume-banner race while `primary_ui` is simultaneously changing mode-switch state, we end up with a ghost fix that passes one test run and corrupts the ownership model. Same reason I want one more explicit forbidden-edit zone: `App/AppModel.swift`, `App/TraceSession.swift`, and `App/AppRouter.swift` belong to `primary_ui` alone once T-006 starts. Services can define protocols and contracts; they do not reach into session orchestration. That boundary matters because offline behavior, resume routing, and shared canvas state are product behavior, not just plumbing. Likewise, `Packages/CleanlineModels/Sources/CleanlineModels/*` need to stay lane-owned exactly as already split in T-001/T-002/T-003; nobody “just tweaks a field” in another lane’s model file during implementation. If a lane needs a contract change, they raise it in-thread, and the owning lane makes the edit. That is the only way we avoid three silent schema forks.

Second, I want the merge protocol made explicit enough that workers can actually start without stepping on each other. The app should live at `/Users/pchordia/Documents/iOS-App-Factory/cleanline`, and everyone branches from `cleanline/main`. Long-lived lane branches are `lane/data-domain`, `lane/primary-ui`, `lane/services-utilities`, and `lane/polish-resilience`. Actual work happens on short task branches like `task/T-006-app-shell` forked from the lane branch, merged back into the lane branch by the lane owner, then lane branches merge into `cleanline/main` only at milestone boundaries after the dependency gate passes. `project.pbxproj` stays effectively integrator-owned even if one person physically edits it during a milestone sweep; nobody opportunistically edits target membership mid-task unless their task explicitly requires new files to compile, and if it does, they queue those additions into the current sweep owner’s batch instead of hand-merging blind. Communication should be dead simple: every task start posts “claiming T-0xx, editing [files]”; every contract concern posts before code, not after; every handoff includes what is stable, what is fake-backed, and what is still blocked. Daily sync question is not “what did you do,” it’s “did any owned file boundary or task dependency become false.” If yes, stop and resolve that first. That’s the conflict-prevention plan in practice.

Testing responsibility also needs to stay with the lane that creates the risk, not dumped onto resilience at the end. `services_utilities` owns unit and fixture tests for persistence, normalization, gate, vectorization, edit, and export as part of the task that introduces the behavior. `primary_ui` owns view-state and navigation correctness for the screens and shell they build, including fake-backed demos before real services land. `polish_resilience` owns cross-cutting system verification: kill/relaunch matrix, accessibility passes, privacy manifest audit, state coverage audit, and device perf validation. `data_domain` owns codable round-trip and schema stability. If everybody follows that, the build workers can start immediately and nobody is waiting for a mystery “QA pass” to discover structural bugs two milestones late.

**Claude (Backend / Systems Engineer · Closer) — Round 2**

Codex, I read your cut and the coordinator's right that you and I converged on the same five-milestone spine but landed on very different granularity, and I want to push back hard on the granularity choice specifically, not just wave it through for the sake of converging fast.

Look at your T-003: it bundles `Fixtures/**`, `CleanlineCapture/**`, `CleanlineQualityGate/**`, and `CleanlineVectorize/**` into one task called "Produce locked fixture set and fake service implementations." Those are four completely different things with four completely different timelines — fixtures have zero code dependency and can start today, capture is a bounded AVFoundation wrapper, quality-gate is deterministic threshold logic, and vectorize is the thing your own prose calls out as needing a spike-then-commit gate. If I assign that as one task to one build-worker, either they block on the slowest piece (vectorize) before reporting anything done, or they silently only do the easy 80% and the acceptance criteria stop meaning anything specific. That's exactly the "hides uncertainty in vectorization behind polish" failure mode you yourself warned about in the project_plan phase — except here it's happening at the task-granularity level, not the milestone level. I'd rather have four small tasks with sharp acceptance criteria than one big task where "done" is ambiguous.

Same problem, more serious, in your T-007: "Spike and then implement production vectorization engine" as a single task with a single acceptance-criteria list that mixes "simplest icon fixture completes end-to-end spike" with "production vectorization covers all locked fixture classes." The whole point of the locked project_plan — the thing both of us signed off on in the prior phase — was that the spike is a hard go/no-go checkpoint specifically so the team can renegotiate scope *before* committing to the production estimate. If it's one task, there's no natural place to stop, report the spike's actual result, and decide whether lettering/closed-shape/messy-notebook are even reachable in the time budgeted. You'd be building the exact ambiguity back into the schedule that the plan phase spent two rounds removing. I've got this split as T-014 (spike, go/no-go, blocking) and T-015 (production, depends_on T-014) specifically so that gate is structural, not just a note in the description.

And here's the one I think actually matters most for how we avoid stepping on each other: your T-012, "Integrate project wiring, target membership, and final branch stabilization," assigns `Cleanline.xcodeproj/**`, `Package.swift`, and `App/**` to `polish_resilience` as an owned task. But your own prose two paragraphs earlier says "one integrator should own target membership and package graph updates, and everyone else should avoid casual `.pbxproj` churn" — which I read the same way, and said explicitly in my own round 1. If you put that file ownership inside a `polish_resilience`-lane task in the backlog, you've made `polish_resilience` the permanent integrator by construction, not a rotating role, and you've also just told `primary_ui` they can't touch `App/**` even though `App/AppModel.swift`/`TraceSession.swift`/`AppRouter.swift` are explicitly `primary_ui`-owned everywhere else in both our plans. That's a direct contradiction inside your own task list. I think the fix is: don't put project-wiring in the task backlog as a lane deliverable at all — it's a standing cross-cutting responsibility that whoever's integrator-for-the-week does continuously, and it doesn't need its own acceptance criteria beyond "the project builds," which is already implicitly checked by every other task's "compiles" criterion.

Where I do want to move toward you: your `cleanline/integration` branch is a genuinely better idea than what I proposed last round, and I'll say so plainly since you asked me to actually change my mind when someone's got the better answer. I had lane branches merging straight to `main` at milestone boundaries only, which means nothing gets integration-tested together until a whole milestone's worth of work lands at once — that's a big-bang integration risk sitting right in the middle of the plan we just spent two rounds trying to de-risk. Your model is better: task branches merge into their lane branch, lane branches merge continuously into `cleanline/integration`, and *that's* where cross-lane breakage gets caught early and often. The only change I'd make is keeping `main` itself gated on milestone-boundary promotion from `cleanline/integration` — so `main` stays the "this milestone's exit criteria are proven" line, while `cleanline/integration` is where the actual day-to-day collision-catching happens. That gives us both things: your continuous cross-lane visibility and my discipline about what's allowed to call itself a milestone-complete `main`.

So here's where I land, concretely, and I think this closes it: keep my 29-task backlog with its file-level ownership (because ambiguous multi-package tasks are a real risk, not pedantry), adopt your `cleanline/integration` branch as the continuous merge target for all four lane branches, gate `main` on milestone-boundary promotion out of `cleanline/integration` once that milestone's exit criteria pass, and drop the idea of a discrete "project wiring" task entirely — it's a standing integrator responsibility that touches `project.pbxproj`/root `Package.swift` only, checked at every merge into `cleanline/integration`, never assigned to a single lane as owned deliverable work. If you're fine with that split, I think this phase is done — the remaining difference between our two lists isn't a disagreement about direction, it's my list being the direct execution of the same principles your prose already states, just carried all the way through the task boundaries instead of stopping at five milestones with twelve tasks.
