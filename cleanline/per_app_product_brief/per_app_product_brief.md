### Round 1


**Codex (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

cleanline should be positioned as a narrow production tool, not a magical “scan to vector” toy. The user is a solo illustrator, hand-letterer, or print-on-demand seller who already works from paper first and loses time cleaning up phone photos, retracing in Illustrator, or paying for desktop software just to get one usable vector asset. The use case is immediate and transactional: they made one sketch, they need one clean SVG or PDF today, and they need the app to be honest about whether the photo is good enough before wasting their time.

The free product has to fully complete that job once at a professional-enough baseline: capture or import a sketch, run the local correction gate, show the cleaned black-line preview, trace it into vector paths, allow the small cleanup actions we already scoped, and export usable SVG/PDF without watermarking or format bait-and-switch. If that free path does not produce something a seller could actually upload or refine downstream, the app has no right to exist. Subscription value should live where recurring utility actually exists: higher-fidelity tracing presets for different source types, batch processing, reusable export presets for Cricut/laser/POD workflows, stronger recovery tools for tough photos, and future pro editing like anchor-level path editing. That is functional leverage, not cosmetic lock-in.

The core loop needs to stay brutally short and failure-aware: new trace, camera or photo import, immediate quality gate with specific retake guidance, automatic rescue pass for perspective/shadow/background/threshold, auto-vectorize, before/after review, light cleanup for actual failure modes, export, save to recent traces. The retention loop is not “come browse your gallery.” It is “next time you sketch, this is the fastest trustworthy path to a sellable vector.” That only works if recent projects reopen instantly, edits autosave, exports are predictable, and the app remembers the user’s preferred trace/export settings so repeat use gets faster over time.

The competitive wedge is not raw tracing sophistication in the abstract. cleanline wins if it is better than scanner apps and less annoying than desktop cleanup for this exact paper-to-vector moment. The differentiator is bounded capture rescue plus honest rejection: it handles the common bad-but-fixable phone-photo problems on device, and when the photo is outside supported bounds it says so clearly instead of producing junk. The biggest risk is overclaiming here. If the app markets “clean vectors from your sketch” but regularly outputs fragmented paths, noisy specks, or over-pointed curves that nobody wants to edit, the whole product collapses. The final brief must preserve that the app is only as good as its exported path quality and its willingness to fail early on bad input.

Local-first behavior should be real, not rhetorical. Source photos, processed previews, vector outputs, recent-trace metadata, and edit history all live on device with no account required. Processing is deterministic and offline-capable after install. Privacy messaging should plainly say sketches never leave the device in v1. Cloud-ready architecture still matters: persistence, vectorization jobs, export artifacts, and project metadata should be separated cleanly so future sync, backup, cross-device handoff, or shared project review can be added later without rewriting the tracing pipeline. But none of that should leak into the MVP surface.

The growth mechanism is niche but credible. This is the kind of tool that spreads through output: Etsy sellers, lettering artists, Cricut communities, and illustrator TikTok/YouTube workflows naturally show before-and-after transformations and time-saved demos. The app does not need social features to be viral; it needs exports good enough that users willingly post “paper sketch to vector in two minutes” clips. The real reason users keep paying is simple: it removes repetitive, annoying cleanup work every time they make a new sketch, and it does it fast enough on a phone that it becomes part of their actual creation workflow instead of a one-off novelty.

**Claude (Product Strategist · User Advocate) — Round 1**

Let me picture the actual person who opens this app for the first time. She's a print-on-demand seller — call her the type who draws lettering and small illustrations for mugs and t-shirt listings on Etsy or via Printify. She's sitting at her kitchen table with a notebook page she just finished, a phone, and a listing deadline tonight. She does not own a scanner, does not own Illustrator's Image Trace workflow patience, and has tried "just use a scanning app plus manually retrace in Illustrator" and hated every minute of it. That's the user cleanline is for, and the brief has to be written for her, not for a hypothetical power user with a Wacom tablet.

The paid value has to be obvious the first time she hits a wall, not buried in a settings screen. Here's the wall she'll hit fast: she has more than one sketch. The free tier does exactly what we already locked — one sketch in, one clean export out, unlimited times, because gating basic SVG export would be a bait-and-switch and we already ruled that out. But she's a seller, which means she doesn't have one design, she has a backlog — a dozen sketches from a weekend of drawing that she wants turned into listings before a product drop. That's where batch processing earns its keep as a subscription feature: photograph or import a stack of sketches, walk away, come back to a set of exports instead of babysitting one trace at a time. The second real paid lever is higher-fidelity tracing presets — a "fine detail" mode for denser linework and lettering with serifs, which costs more processing time and is a legitimate quality tier, not a fake toggle. Third is the pro editing tier the contract already reserved: real anchor-point/Bezier dragging for the rare case where auto-trace plus the free cleanup tools genuinely isn't enough — this is a real workflow upgrade for someone who's decided this app is now part of her production pipeline, not a paywall on core functionality. Cloud backup/sync of her recent-traces history is a fourth, lower-drama lever — sellers lose phones, and "your last three months of traces survive that" is worth a few dollars a month on its own.

The core loop is the one we've already converged on, and I want to hold the line on it staying exactly that short: open app, capture or import, quality gate, auto-correct, auto-vectorize, before/after, light cleanup, export. Every screen in between has to answer "why am I still here" in under a second — a progress indicator that says what's actually happening (correcting perspective, isolating linework, tracing paths) rather than a generic spinner, because this pipeline takes real seconds and silence during real seconds reads as broken.

Competitive wedge: against a generic scanner app, we win because we don't stop at a flat image — we hand back paths she can actually use. Against Illustrator's Image Trace (the "real" competitor, honestly), we win on the fact that she never leaves her phone, never emails herself a photo, never manually deskews or paints out a shadow in Photoshop first — the capture-rescue layer does that work automatically, and it does it in the three minutes she has before she needs to upload a listing, not the twenty minutes a desktop round-trip costs her. That's a real, defensible wedge as long as the capture-rescue bound-testing from the contract actually holds up — if shadow correction only works in a demo and fails on her actual kitchen-table lighting, we've lost the one thing that makes us better than "scan app plus desktop," so I'd treat that as the single riskiest promise in this brief, not a solved problem.

Growth angle here is niche, not broad, and I think that's correct rather than a weakness: POD sellers already cluster in tight online communities — Etsy seller Facebook groups, POD subreddits, TikTok accounts that document "how I make my listings." One seller posting a fifteen-second video of "sketch on paper → phone photo → SVG in my cart" in front of that audience is worth more than generic App Store discovery, because the audience is small, dollar-motivated, and trusts peer recommendations over ads. The product itself needs to produce a shareable moment for this to work — the before/after screen (rough pencil sketch next to clean vector lines) is the natural screenshot people post, so it should be good enough, visually, to want to show off, not just functionally correct.

Local-first is not a compliance checkbox for this user — she's often working with unreleased product designs before a listing goes live, and "your sketches never leave your phone unless you turn on backup" is a genuine trust point, not just an architecture decision. Cloud should show up later as opt-in sync for the recent-traces history and possibly a way to pick up an in-progress batch job on another device, but v1 has to work completely offline, including export and share-sheet, since she may well be drawing somewhere without reliable signal.

What makes her keep the app past the first export: the second and third sketch have to go faster than the first because now she trusts the quality gate and doesn't scrutinize every trace as hard, and the recent-traces list has to actually save her real time when she needs to re-export a design for a different product size. If by trace number three she's still manually double-checking every path the way she did on trace one, we haven't actually earned trust and she'll churn back to her old manual process the next time she's in a hurry. That trust curve, more than any single feature, is the thing I'd protect above all else in design and build.
