### Round 1


**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

BatchBridge should be a local-first PO review workbench for a small operations team that needs to turn messy supplier PDFs into exportable structured orders fast enough to matter, with enough evidence and control that a human will actually trust approving the result.

The primary user is an ops coordinator, customer service rep, or order-entry specialist at a manufacturer or distributor who receives purchase orders as inconsistent PDFs and currently spends too much time retyping, cross-checking addresses, and fixing extraction mistakes before anything can enter downstream systems. The scenario is not “fully autonomous document AI.” It is “I have a stack of POs, I need to get them into a usable format today, and I need to see what the model guessed before I sign off.”

The core loop should be brutally simple: upload a PO PDF, let the system OCR and extract, land immediately in a review screen that shows the PDF beside structured fields with confidence and source evidence, fix only what is flagged or obviously wrong, confirm or override the Ship To match, approve, then export JSON or CSV. If that loop takes more than a couple of minutes for a normal one- or two-page PO, the product is missing the point.

The hard scope boundary I would enforce is this: BatchBridge is a single-user or lightweight local team MVP for PO intake and correction only. No ERP push, no email ingestion, no supplier portals, no multi-step human workflow routing, no auth complexity, no invoice support, no training studio, no analytics rabbit hole beyond the required dashboard, and no “AI copilot” features that distract from review throughput. Even within the required feature set, I would keep the UI centered on three surfaces only: a dashboard, a document inbox/list, and a document review workspace. Prompt config and Ship To catalog can exist as supporting settings screens, but they should not feel like separate products.

The most important UX decision is that the review screen is the product, not the upload form and not the dashboard. If the review experience is weak, the whole app collapses into a generic extraction demo. Every extracted field needs visible evidence, editable state, confidence treatment that is actually useful, and a clear distinction between original extraction and corrected value. Low-confidence highlighting cannot just be cosmetic; it should drive the operator’s path through the document. The operator should be able to scan flagged fields first, inspect source text quickly, and move on without hunting through tabs. Raw OCR and raw JSON belong there for trust and debugging, but they must stay secondary or the interface will feel like an internal tool instead of a premium product.

The biggest risk in this direction is overcorrecting toward “trust and auditability” and building a review screen that is so dense it becomes slower than manual entry. It is easy to say “show evidence for everything” and accidentally create a wall of metadata. The fix is not to remove evidence; it is to progressively disclose it. The default view should help an operator decide fast, while deeper evidence is one click away. If every field shows every attribute all the time, the design will sabotage the workflow it claims to improve.

Another risk that needs to stay explicit: Ship To matching can quietly become the most painful part of the app if it is treated as a side widget. Operators will not trust fuzzy matching unless the match explanation is concrete. “Confidence 0.82” is not enough. The UI needs to say why a match was suggested and what alternates were close, otherwise the user will re-check addresses manually every time and the feature becomes decorative.

Success is measurable in a few concrete ways. A new user should be able to run the app locally and complete the full sample flow without touching code. For a standard sample PO, the path from upload to approved export should be fast and obvious, with the system surfacing OCR, extraction, and match issues rather than hiding them. Every required field and line item must either contain a value with evidence metadata or be explicitly flagged for review; nothing should disappear silently. Failed OCR, invalid PDFs, and invalid LLM JSON should produce recoverable UI states rather than dead ends. On the UX side, a reviewer should be able to identify all fields needing attention at a glance, inspect the supporting source text quickly, and export both original and corrected outputs after approval.

If there is one detail the final output absolutely needs to preserve, it is this: BatchBridge is not selling “AI extracts POs.” Plenty of products can claim that. It is selling “I can trust, correct, and approve extracted POs quickly because the review experience is evidence-driven and built for exceptions.” If that promise gets blurred, the app loses its differentiation and becomes interchangeable.

Here's my take, coming at this from the angle of what will actually break and what's the smallest thing worth shipping.

Not "AI extracts your POs" — that's the easy 80%, and every competitor claims it. The promise is specifically about the last 20%: the fields that are wrong or ambiguous, and how fast a human can find and fix them with evidence in front of them. If a demo can't show a deliberately messy PO going from "3 fields flagged needs_review" to "approved and exported" in under a couple minutes of human effort, the app hasn't delivered on the promise, no matter how clean the happy path looks.

Primary user: an ops/AP coordinator at a small-to-mid manufacturer or distributor who receives PO PDFs from 20-50 recurring customers, each with their own layout quirks, and currently either retypes them by hand or fights a rigid legacy OCR tool. The scenario that matters most isn't the pristine, perfectly-formatted PDF — it's the scanned fax-quality PDF, the multi-page PO with line items spanning pages, the customer whose "Ship To" field is a nickname that doesn't match the catalog verbatim. That's where value either exists or doesn't.

Core loop: upload → automatic pipeline runs (convert, OCR, extract) → operator lands on a review screen where low-confidence fields are already visually flagged and pre-scrolled to the first problem → operator corrects or accepts, resolves Ship To (accepting the suggested match or picking an alternate) → approve → export. The loop needs to be tight enough that a repeat user does it from muscle memory, not by reading UI labels every time.

Where I'd push back preemptively: the biggest risk to this whole plan is treating "LLM extraction" as a solved black box. Two failure modes will bite in practice — the model returning malformed JSON (the spec already accounts for one repair retry, good, but what happens on the second failure? that needs a defined terminal state, not a hang or silent 500), and the model returning confidently wrong values with high stated confidence, which is worse than low confidence because it won't get flagged for review. I don't think this phase needs to solve that, but the success criteria for this app cannot just be "extraction accuracy" — it has to include something like "percentage of wrong fields that were still caught before export," because a real ops user's trust is destroyed by silent wrongness, not by visible uncertainty.

Hard scope boundary I want to nail down now, before design phase invents features: single-user, single-tenant, no auth beyond maybe nothing at all — this is a local tool one operator runs, not a multi-user system. No batch-upload-and-forget queue; the review step is synchronous and in-the-loop by design, not an afterthought. Only PDF input, only the PO document type, only the fields already named in the spec — nothing about invoices or BoLs sneaking in later because "it's basically the same pipeline."

Measurable success criteria I'd hold this to: (1) a fresh operator can go from a raw sample PDF to an approved, exported JSON+CSV pair in under 5 minutes on first use, no docs needed beyond on-screen guidance; (2) every field in the export carries its confidence/source/page provenance — zero fields silently missing that provenance; (3) at least one deliberately malformed/scanned-garbage sample PDF in the repo demonstrably fails gracefully (needs_review flags, no crash, no silent data loss) rather than just being a happy-path demo; (4) Ship To fuzzy match surfaces a defensible "reason" string for its top pick on a fuzzy/nickname input, not just a similarity score with no explanation.
