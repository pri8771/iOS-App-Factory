If someone wants to re-see a dose they already computed, that's not a workbench, that's just tapping back into that test's row in history — the trace lives with the test it was computed from, not floating in its own tab waiting to be re-triggered.

On reminders and compliance — Codex, splitting those into "Reminder Screen" and "Compliance & Lifecycle" as separate top-level sections is where I think you're over-building the IA for what's actually a small amount of content. Walk through what's really in there: a notification toggle plus a next-due date, and separately, export/import plus entitlement status plus delete-tank. That's maybe four rows of settings total, once tank management is already accounted for. If we give reminders their own screen and give export/paywall status a second screen, we've built three settings-adjacent destinations for a total of one screenful of controls, and now Dana has to remember which of three places has the thing she wants — is the "add a second tank" upgrade prompt under Compliance or under wherever tank list lives? I don't think there's a real answer to that question the way you've split it, which is exactly the kind of ambiguity that turns into an inconsistent build. I'd fold all of it into one Settings tab with clear section headers inside it — Tank(s), Reminders, Data (export/import), Subscription — rather than multiple flat top-level screens. That's not me being precious about tab count for its own sake, it's that a lean single settings surface is genuinely easier for Dana to build a mental map of, and easier for whoever's implementing this to keep state-consistent, since there's one screen's worth of empty/loading/error states to get right instead of three.

I do want to give Codex real credit on one thing that's sharper than what I had: the "always-visible policy row on dose cards when multiple parameters or unsupported reductions are involved" — naming it as a persistent, structural element of the dose card rather than something that only shows up conditionally when it happens to apply. I described the same behavior functionally (the multi-parameter stagger warning, the NO3/PO4 "not modeled" note) but Codex's framing that this needs to be a recognized row-type in the card's layout system, not an ad hoc banner we bolt on, is the right instinct — it means whoever builds the dose card component needs to design for "this card can contain 1 to N advisory rows" from day one rather than "trace plus maybe an alert." I'm adopting that framing.

One more place I want to push back, gently, on Codex's inventory: onboarding as three explicit steps (tank, first test, product setup) is more ceremony than I think this deserves. Product setup — picking or confirming a default product per parameter — doesn't need to be a dedicated onboarding step; it can be a sensible default silently applied (the seeded seed product) and surfaced as an editable field right there on the dose recommendation screen itself ("Product: Brand X Alk Buffer, 1.4 mEq/mL — change"), the same way I want unit selection to be inline rather than upfront. Splitting this into a three-step wizard adds a screen Dana has to get through before she's seen any value, and the whole design bet we're making is that she should reach a real dose number in as few taps as possible. I'd compress this to two things: tank setup (required, blocking, because volume gates everything) and first test (which naturally flows into the dose screen as its own reward) — product config rides along inside the dose screen rather than gating entry to it.

So here's the navigation shape I want the group to lock: four tabs — Dashboard, Tests, Livestock, Settings — with Dose Recommendation and Applied-Dose-Log existing only as pushed screens off the Tests flow (reached by saving a test, or by tapping back into a historical test's trace), Compatibility Check existing only as a pushed screen off Livestock (reached by tapping a candidate), and everything reminders/export/entitlement/tank-management related consolidated into one sectioned Settings tab. That's a navigation model with exactly four destinations you can get lost in, and every other screen in both of our inventories is reachable as a direct consequence of something Dana just did, which is the property I actually care about protecting.
