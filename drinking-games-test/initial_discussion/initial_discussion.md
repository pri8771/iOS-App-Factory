## Original Prompt

```
Build Kings — a multiplayer drinking game app for iOS (local pass-and-play).

Core mechanics:
- 2–6 players draw cards from a deck in turn order
- Each card (A–K, plus Joker) triggers a rule: e.g. "2 = You" (drinker picks someone), "5 = Guys", "K = King's Cup" (build communal cup with dares), "Joker = Make a rule"
- Players drink per the card rule; when the cup is empty or 10 turns pass, game ends with a recap
- Pass phone between players; each player sees only their own card until they pass

Key features:
- Local only (no server, no login)
- Customizable rules: edit card meanings before each game, save rulesets
- Game history: recap shows who drank most, funniest rule, timeline of rounds
- Sober mode toggle (default): uses "dare" language instead of drinking; activating drinking mode (17+ gate) swaps copy to drinking references but same mechanics

Tech:
- SwiftUI, local-first with CoreData for game history
- Phone must stay face-down while other player holds it; privacy shield until handoff
- Export game recap as a sharable image (name, date, player list, highlights)

Scope: MVP with Kings rules only (not Hearts, Asshole, or other variants). One ruleset bundled; let users customize before start.
```

**Claude (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

Here's my independent take, coming at this as design lead with a systems-thinking hat on.

Product promise, one sentence: Kings is the app you pull out at a party that runs a complete, private, pass-and-play King's Cup game on one phone — nobody sees a card that isn't theirs, nobody argues about the rules, and everybody gets a recap to laugh about after.

Primary user and scenario: a host with a phone and 2–6 friends around a table who already know how to play Kings and want the phone to be a silent referee, not a teacher. The scenario that matters most isn't "opening the app" — it's the moment the phone physically changes hands. That's the one interaction that happens 20-60+ times in a session, and it's the one place where a UX mistake becomes a trust-ending event, not a minor annoyance. Design-wise, I'd treat "the handoff" as the primary screen of the app, not a transitional state between the "real" screens.

Core loop, stated as a state machine since that's how it'll actually get built: Shielded (card hidden, big "pass to [Name]" label, confirm-tap required) → Revealed (card + rule text, drink/dare framed per current mode) → Acknowledged (player taps done, cup/turn counters update) → Shielded again for the next player, repeating until one of the three end conditions fires (4th King, 10 full rounds, deck exhausted) → Recap (highlights, funniest-rule ballot or empty state, end-reason stated in plain language) → Share/History. I want to flag one IA detail that I don't think has been made explicit yet: the transition screen between players needs to *be* the shield, not a separate step before the shield. If "tap to pass" and "shield covering the next card" are two different screens, you've created a gap where the outgoing player's card is still technically visible while the UI is mid-transition — that's exactly the kind of one-frame leak Codex and I both flagged as the top risk in research. The shield should render before the pass-confirmation UI even appears, so there's no sequencing where a card could theoretically bleed through.

The other systems-level thing I want on record: the round counter and the King's cup fill are two independent progress signals that both need constant on-screen visibility (not just at end-game), because otherwise "10 rounds or 4 Kings, whichever comes first" is an invisible rule players can't reason about mid-game — someone will ask "how much longer is this" and the app should already be answering that with a persistent, glanceable indicator (e.g., cup fill as a real visual, round X of ~10 as text), not force them to guess. And since the last round can end mid-round (the 4th King doesn't wait for the round to finish), the recap timeline UI needs to visually show a partial final round rather than silently rendering an uneven row that looks like a bug.

Hard scope boundaries, restating what's already been ratified so it's load-bearing for this phase too: single device, local pass-and-play, 2–6 players, Kings only — no Hearts/Asshole/other variants, no networking or accounts, no mid-game resume across a relaunch (an interrupted game is simply gone, by design), no content moderation of custom rule text even though it can appear in the shareable export, no telemetry-driven success metrics.

Measurable success criteria, kept binary and QA-checkable since there's no telemetry to lean on: a full 2-player and a full 6-player game each complete end-to-end with zero crashes and zero visible card content across at least 50 handoff cycles under adversarial conditions (backgrounding, rotation, rapid double-tap, incoming notification banner); ruleset edits and completed game history both survive a force-quit and relaunch; the three end conditions each produce their own distinct, correctly-labeled recap end-reason with no generic fallback text; export either succeeds and produces a correct image or fails with a visible, actionable error — never a silent no-op.

The biggest risk to my own framing here: I'm proposing the handoff-as-primary-screen mental model as if it's obviously right, but it does add a small amount of friction to every single turn, and if that friction is annoying by round 8 of a 6-player game, people will find workarounds (tapping through blind, not actually looking away) that quietly defeat the privacy guarantee the whole app is built around. I don't think that's a reason to weaken the shield — I think it's a reason the confirm-tap gesture itself needs to feel fast and satisfying (haptic, immediate) even though it's mandatory, so speed and privacy aren't fighting each other in the interaction design.

It introduces a layer of complexity and subjectivity that we shouldn’t be adding to this game. The core mechanic is a deterministic flow – card draw triggers a rule, the rule executes, and the game state updates. Adding a subjective “funniest rule” element adds unnecessary friction and potential for arguments. Let’s stick to a simple, deterministic mechanism: the last Joker rule invoked wins. It’s clear, it’s measurable, and it avoids any potential for disputes or abuse. And frankly, if we’re going to create a “funniest rule” mechanism, let’s keep it contained within the game itself – a simple UI element that displays the rule that triggered the most laughter, not a potentially problematic export image. That’s transparency – a key element of trust.

I also want to reinforce the critical point about the turn-cap/cup-fill mechanic. Gemini’s initial proposal – a quarter-cup per King, fourth King ends it – is a clever attempt to make the game more dynamic, but it’s still fundamentally flawed. We’re adding a layer of calculation to a game that’s already inherently chaotic and unpredictable. The key is to embrace the ambiguity. The current plan – 10 turns or cup empty – is simple, it’s clear, and it’s robust. Let’s not try to “optimize” the game by adding arbitrary mathematical rules. We’re building a party game, not a complex simulation. A fourth king draw *should* end the game – that’s the most reasonable interpretation of the prompt.

Finally, about those percentage-based success metrics – let’s just scrap them. Measuring user engagement in a local-only game is a fool’s errand. We can’t track how many people actually play, let alone how successfully they play. The only metric that matters is whether the game is fun. Let’s focus on concrete, testable outcomes – a successful completion of a two-player game, a successful completion of a six-player game, rule modifications persisting across app restarts, and the image export working correctly. We don't need a full-blown analytics dashboard; a simple count of games played would be enough. The key is to use that data to *improve* the game – not to judge it.
