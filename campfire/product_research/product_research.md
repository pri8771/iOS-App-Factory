Pass one phone around a group and Campfire runs the icebreaker game, tallies the votes, and hands you a shareable results card.

Awkward silence at a group hangout, or reaching for a party-game app only to realize everyone needs to download it and make an account before the game can even start.

Free tier includes a rotating set of prompt packs; Campfire+ unlocks all prompt packs (family-friendly, spicy, work-safe, couples), custom prompt creation, and unlimited saved session recap cards.

Freemium, $2.99/mo or $19.99/yr — deliberately cheap as an impulse/occasion purchase.

All prompt packs and session history stored locally; no accounts or networking needed for the core loop at all.

Repository boundary allows an optional future server-hosted seasonal prompt pack downloader and, later, an opt-in synchronous multi-device mode — neither required for v1 value.

## AR / Local ML / Local LLM Fit

None — intentionally excluded; LLM-generated prompts would break the offline-anywhere promise for no real gain over a well-curated static prompt library.

High-energy saturated party palette — campfire-orange to hot-pink gradients, bold rounded slab type, oversized tap targets for pass-the-phone play, playful bounce transitions, confetti-style haptic-synced reveals.

The party game that starts in 5 seconds on one phone — no downloads for your friends, no account, just pass it around.

### Round 1


**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

The first product decision is to treat this as a “party object” not a game menu: the app should feel like a single physical card deck you pass around, because the whole value proposition is reducing setup friction in group moments. Best direction is a frictionless loop: pick mode in <5 seconds, set names, then one giant prompt card per round with clear “next player” transitions and a strong wrap-up card that’s obviously worth sharing. That keeps the viral loop alive through social proof.

Audience is broadly “roomful of people with phones nearby” more than “app users”: families at holidays, dates, roommates meeting, office teams between activities, high-school or college friend groups, and people age ~13+ with iPhone access. This is not a niche personality quiz; it is a social ritual enhancer, so defaults must be warm, quick, and obvious even to someone who just opened the app for the first time ten seconds ago.

Primary competitors/patterns are Jackbox Party Pack (low-friction social reveal mechanics but usually multi-device), Jackbox/House party style prompt apps, Kahoot (timer, host/players split expectations but usually networked), Cards Against prompts, and generic icebreaker cards in chat apps. The gap to win is the one-device physical handoff model + shareable end card + no setup burden. If it feels like “another prompt app,” it loses; if it feels like a polished social prop, it wins.

Platform expectations on iPhone are strict here: iOS users in party contexts punish friction. So screen states must be legible under movement and dim light, button targets must be oversized, and mode transitions need immediate feedback. Haptics + subtle bounce is good, but avoid anything that feels like gamepad lag; motion should confirm action, not delay it. Accessibility has to be intentional, not bolted-on: large labels, high contrast despite saturated theme, Dynamic Type legibility for spoken prompts, and VoiceOver flows that still make sense when one user is holding the phone for another person. Because it’s offline-first, startup speed and deterministic behavior in airplane mode are part of the UX contract.

Big risks/unknowns that should be front-loaded as explicit assumptions:  
- Prompt quality is the core product moat, not the scoring logic. Without hand-curated, tone-appropriate packs, it becomes “just another icebreaker list” and retention dies after one awkward use.  
- One-screen-at-a-time pass-and-play UX can still fail if turn ownership is ambiguous; if players don’t know who is up now, social momentum breaks immediately.  
- “Viral loop” can underperform if recap card looks generic. A beautiful card with context (mode, winners, best answer, player count, time, custom pack tag) and instant sharing cues is the critical difference between a memory artifact and throwaway screenshot.

Assumption set that must be explicit: we should cap initial scope to three strong modes over four weak modes if quality suffers; only ship modes with stable answer/vote semantics; no account/network required even for history; local persistence can be simple and local from day one; StoreKit paywall must block real content paths, not just cosmetic UI. Also assume a single recap format for v1; do not overfit mode-specific card templates until flow proves.

For information architecture, one-screen depth is healthier than nested setup: top-level mode browser → player setup (count + names) → active round cards → reveal/tally → round-by-round summary chip → final recap with one tap to share and one tap to “save + play again” in same session. Any extra navigation stack is friction and should be avoided.

Design implication for first build: invest in a strict visual system before feature breadth. Use the campfire-to-pink gradient and rounded slab type, but enforce contrast-safe tokens and reusable large components; do not let motion/color become inaccessible noise. The first pass should feel like a party object: bright, tactile, predictable, and shareable.

Highest-stakes unknown for this phase is not technical debt, it is social behavior: does a casual user feel comfortable passing the phone and pressing a few large buttons for ten minutes with a group watching. If we don’t validate that flow mentally and in edge-case states (2 players, 10+ players, mid-session interruption, zero completed rounds), we risk shipping a pretty but socially broken app.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Audience is clearer than most of the packs in the spec suggest: this gets opened at the exact moment a room goes quiet — pregame before a party, dinner table lull, first date, new roommates, holiday gathering with mixed generations in the room, work happy hour. That "mixed generations/mixed comfort levels in one room" detail matters more than the spec treats it — it's why the family/spicy/work-safe/couples pack split exists at all, and it means the free tier needs at least one pack that's safe to pull out in front of anyone, or the zero-friction promise dies the first time someone opens it at Thanksgiving and gets a spicy prompt.

The closest real comp is Heads Up! — single device, pass-and-play, category packs as the monetization unit, dead-simple UI, huge in exactly this "party prop, not a utility" space. That's the bar to study for tap-target sizing and how fast you get from cold launch to "someone's holding the phone playing." The cautionary comps are the App Store's current glut of "Never Have I Ever" / "Most Likely To" list apps — most are a scrollable list of strings behind a paywall, often ad-supported, low production value. That's actually good news for differentiation (the bar is low) but bad news for anyone tempted to cut corners on content, because "we're better than a free list-app" is the entire pitch and there's zero room to ship something that reads the same as the bottom of that pile. Paired is worth a glance for how a cheap-subscription prompt-pack product frames its paywall moment, even though it's asynchronous, not pass-and-play.

Two platform-native things I want on record before build starts because they're easy to forget and both would break the demo: the app needs `isIdleTimerDisabled = true` while a round is active, since someone reading a prompt out loud for 30-45 seconds on a phone with a normal auto-lock will hit a black screen mid-round — that's a first-session-breaking bug, not a polish item. And the recap card has to be verified as an actual rendered image on a physical device, not just in Xcode preview — `ImageRenderer` on gradient-heavy, SF-Symbol-laden views has known edge cases (blank or clipped output) that don't always show up in simulator. Both of these are cheap to get right and expensive to discover after the app is "done."

The risk I most want named as an explicit assumption before anyone starts building: the spec says "3-4 game modes" and separately lists "family-friendly, spicy, work-safe, couples" as pack types, and it's easy to accidentally collapse those into the same thing. I'm reading modes as distinct game mechanics (e.g., a ranking/voting mode, a reveal/confession mode, a rapid-fire this-or-that mode) each of which can draw prompts from any of the content packs — not "spicy" itself being a mode. If that distinction gets blurred, you end up with either four content flavors wrapped around one thin mechanic, or four mechanics each starved for content, and either failure looks fine in a screenshot. This needs to be locked as a named assumption now, not discovered during build.

Second concrete risk: pass-and-play turn state is exactly the kind of thing that looks trivial and isn't. A mode like "who's most likely to..." needs a target player distinct from whoever's answering — with 2 players that's forced, with 5+ it needs deliberate non-repeating pairing logic, not "pick random and hope it's not the same person twice in a row." A single source of truth for current player/turn order, tested at the 2-player boundary and at a double-digit count, needs to be a build-time discipline, not a demo-day fix.

Third risk, and it's a product-economics one rather than a code one so it belongs in this phase's notes for whoever builds the paywall UX: this is occasion-based, not daily-habit, usage — someone downloads it the night of a party and might not open it again for three weeks. A monthly subscription default is a mismatch for that usage pattern and invites fast cancel/refund behavior; the paywall moment should probably anchor on annual or frame around "unlock this pack" rather than lead with $2.99/mo. Not something to fix in this phase, but if nobody writes it down now it'll get built exactly as literally specified and nobody will ask the pricing-anchor question until App Review.

One more assumption worth stating explicitly since it's implied but never said outright: no ads, anywhere, ever, in this app. It's implied by "no downloads for your friends, no account, just pass it around" positioning and by the airplane-mode-proof requirement — an ad SDK either needs network (breaking offline-proof) or fails/hangs silently offline (breaking the "just works" promise), and it'd violate the standing no-third-party-package-unless-it-reduces-risk rule. I'd rather this be a named non-goal now than have someone propose it later as a free-tier revenue lever.

For the first build, given the tie-breaker already locked in (3 finished over 4 thin), I'd commit now to picking exactly which three mechanics so nobody re-litigates it mid-build: one voting/ranking mode, one reveal/confession mode, one fast binary-choice mode. That combination covers meaningfully different pass-and-play rhythms (slow deliberation vs. quick reflex vs. group-judgment) rather than three variations on the same "read a card, move on" loop, which is what'll make the recap card interesting to look at across a full session instead of one mode's data dominating it.
