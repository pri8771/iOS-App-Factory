**Codex (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Choicepoint should be built for people who make the same kind of meaningful calls over and over and are tired of learning nothing from them: founders deciding whether to ship or kill something, managers making hiring calls, consultants choosing bets, people making career or relocation decisions. The use case is narrow on purpose. This is not for “daily reflection.” It is for the moment when someone is making a call that will matter later, has maybe 20 seconds, and needs to lock in what they think will happen before hindsight rewrites the story.

The product only deserves to exist if it creates a durable before-and-after record that other tools don’t. Notes apps can store thoughts. Journals can store feelings. Task apps can remind you. Choicepoint’s wedge is that it captures a prediction at decision time, preserves the reasoning with a timestamp, and then forces a later comparison against reality. That is a different job. If the app stops being strict about that, it becomes a weaker version of tools the user already has.

The smallest version worth shipping has one core loop and it has to be real end to end. Capture a decision with just three required fields: the decision, the prediction, and the revisit date. Let context, options considered, and confidence exist, but never block first save. Schedule a real local notification. When that reminder is tapped, open the exact decision and show the original prediction and reasoning before asking for the actual outcome. Once resolved, feed that record into calibration stats that are based only on completed revisits. If any part of that loop is fake or softened, the value collapses. A generic reminder list is not enough. A stats screen with placeholder numbers is not enough. A note without a prediction is not enough.

People would pay for utility, not sentiment. Free should be enough to prove the loop works: five active decisions, basic revisit reminders, and a simple history. Paid should unlock things that materially improve judgment practice for someone who uses the app seriously: unlimited active decisions, deeper calibration views over time, custom revisit templates and multi-step revisit schedules, export for review with a coach, manager, or personal archive, and richer filtering across domains like hiring, product, finance, or personal life. The subscription value is that the app becomes a serious judgment ledger instead of a small reminder utility. If paid only changes colors or removes a cap without adding analytical leverage, it will feel thin.

What keeps users is not daily engagement. It is trust. They need to believe the app will be there at the exact moment they need to capture a decision, and later at the exact moment they need to confront whether they were right. That means local-first is not just an architecture choice, it is part of the product promise. Every decision, revisit state, and stat should work fully on device with deterministic persistence, offline, with no account required. Local notifications should power the revisit loop. Widgets should read upcoming and overdue revisits from the same local store. Siri/App Intent capture should hit the same validation and cap enforcement as the main app, or it will create edge-case corruption and bypass the free limit.

The cloud path should be an extension, not a dependency. The data model should assume stable IDs, clear event timestamps, and separation between decision records, revisit records, and computed analytics so sync can be added later without rewriting the app. The first cloud feature should be backup and multi-device sync for the same user, not collaboration. Shared journals for co-founders or partners are plausible later, but they are not where the risk is today. The risk today is getting a single-user loop to close reliably.

The growth mechanism is niche and credible, not broad and gimmicky. This is the kind of app that spreads through professional circles where people already care about decision quality: founders, operators, investors, coaches, team leads. Export is part of growth because it creates artifacts people can discuss privately: “Here’s the decision I made, what I predicted, and what actually happened.” The product can also grow through reputation in communities that value judgment calibration, but it should not contort itself into social sharing. Public posting would poison the honesty of the input. Private usefulness is the engine; selective professional sharing is the spillover.

The biggest risk is that revisit completion stays low and the app turns into a graveyard of unresolved decisions. If people capture but never close the loop, the calibration claim is false in practice even if the feature exists. So I would bias the brief toward boring reliability over ambitious breadth. Overdue revisits must stay visible. Notification-denied states must be handled explicitly. Revisit should feel neutral and forensic, not guilt-inducing. The product should make it easy to resolve a decision quickly, even if the outcome is messy or ambiguous.

The detail that needs to survive into the final phase output is this: Choicepoint is not a decision journal. It is a judgment-calibration tool whose value comes from preserving a pre-outcome prediction and later comparing it against reality. Every product, design, and monetization choice should be judged against that. If a feature doesn’t strengthen capture, revisit, honest outcome logging, or calibration, it should be cut.

Core loop, stated as the thing that has to survive contact with a distracted real person: you're mid-decision, you open the app, you type the decision and your prediction, you set when to check back (sensible defaults like 30/60/90 days plus custom), and you're done in under 20 seconds — three fields, everything else optional. Weeks later a notification arrives that isn't vague ("time to reflect!") but specific ("You predicted: [prediction]. Check in on this now?"). Tapping it goes straight to that decision, shows the original reasoning verbatim, and asks what actually happened plus whether the prediction held. Choicepoint's wedge is being the only app where the prediction and the forced follow-up are the entire structure of the product, not a feature bolted onto journaling. Nobody else makes "did you turn out to be right" the core mechanic instead of an afterthought.

Growth is going to be niche, not viral in the broad social-share sense — and I'd actively resist trying to make it broadly viral, because anything that nudges toward "share your prediction publicly" turns this into a social feed, which is exactly the gamified/social direction the spec forbids. The real growth mechanism is word of mouth inside professional communities that already prize calibration as a virtue — management circles, startup/founder communities, poker and forecasting-adjacent crowds who already use the word "calibration" unprompted. The export feature doubles as organic marketing: when someone shares an exported calibration record in a coaching session or a leadership offsite, the artifact itself explains the product better than an ad would. That's a slow-burn, credibility-driven growth loop, not a k-factor play, and I think that's the honest and correct fit for this app rather than forcing virality it doesn't want.

Local-first is non-negotiable for v1 per the earlier phases, and it's also the right trust posture for the user I'm describing — these are often sensitive professional judgments (hiring calls about real people, business bets) that someone would be uneasy putting in a cloud service by default. The cloud-ready path should be architected but inert — stable UUIDs on every decision record from day one, a persistence layer that doesn't assume single-device state (no reliance on device-local timestamps as the sole ordering key, for instance), so that when cloud sync or the "shared journal for co-founders" idea gets built later, it's additive infrastructure, not a data-model rewrite. The biggest risk to my own direction here: I'm assuming the "professional reflection / export for coaching conversations" angle is a real enough paid hook, and I haven't validated that against the alternative reading that this is mostly a personal-growth tool for individuals, in which case export matters less and the paid hook should lean harder on unlimited tracking and richer analytics alone. I think professional export is the stronger, more defensible paid feature because it's the one thing a spreadsheet or Notes app genuinely can't replicate for this user, but I want that flagged as my own judgment call, not settled fact.

The detail I don't want lost by the time this becomes a design doc: the paid tier's value has to be earned by data volume and time, not by unlocking a screen that could have existed for free. If calibration analytics look identical whether you have 5 decisions or 50, the subscription has no functional teeth and it becomes a "please donate" paywall instead of genuine value — and that's the acceptance check this whole phase should be judged against.

## Final Output

**Target user and use case:** Someone who makes recurring, consequential decisions professionally or personally — hiring calls, product/ship-or-kill bets, vendor picks, career or relocation choices — and currently has zero real evidence of whether their judgment is any good, just a vague feeling. Not a daily journaler, not someone tracking trivial choices. The moment that has to work is the unglamorous one: mid-decision, ~20 seconds of attention, typing three things before moving on, then weeks later confronting what they actually predicted before hindsight got to rewrite the story.

**Core loop:** Capture (decision + prediction + revisit date required, ~20 seconds; context/options/confidence present but optional) → real scheduled local notification → tapping it deep-links into that exact decision, showing the original reasoning before asking what actually happened → outcome logging feeds a calibration view built only from resolved decisions. Every beat must be real, not simulated — a fake notification, a generic-list deep link, or a hardcoded stats number breaks the promise, not just weakens it.

**Paid value and subscription value:** Free tier (5 active decisions) exists to prove the loop works, not to be a crippled preview. Paid unlocks (a) unlimited active decisions, (b) calibration analytics that only get meaningful with volume and time — confidence-bucketed accuracy, patterns across categories — which is precisely why it can't be free at n=5, (c) custom revisit schedules (a hiring call might want 90 days, a vendor pick 30), and (d) export, which doubles as the professional-use artifact (a real track record to bring into a coaching conversation or leadership review). The subscription has to earn its price through data volume and elapsed time, not by unlocking a screen that could just as easily have been free — that's the test any later design has to pass.

**Competitive wedge:** Everything else in this space is either generic reflection (journaling apps that never force a structural revisit against a stated prediction) or heavyweight forecasting tools (too numerical/spreadsheet-like for someone's actual hiring decision). Choicepoint's wedge is making "did you turn out to be right" the entire structure of the product, not a bolted-on feature.

**Growth angle:** Niche and credibility-driven, not broadly viral — deliberately so, since public sharing of predictions would poison the honesty of the input and drag the product toward the social/gamified territory the spec forbids. Growth happens through word of mouth in professional communities that already prize calibration (founders, managers, operators, forecasting-adjacent crowds), with exported calibration records acting as organic marketing artifacts when shared in coaching or leadership contexts.

**Local-first and cloud-ready plan:** V1 is fully on-device — no account, no server dependency, deterministic local persistence, local notifications, local calibration math. This isn't just an MVP shortcut; it's the right trust posture given how sensitive these decisions often are (real hiring calls about real people, real business bets). The data model should use stable UUIDs and event timestamps from day one, with decision records, revisit records, and computed analytics kept separable, so that cloud backup/sync (the natural first cloud feature — not shared/collaborative journals, which stay further out) can be added later without a data-model rewrite. Nothing in v1 should behave differently because cloud might exist someday.
