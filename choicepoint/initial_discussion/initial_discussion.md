**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

Choicepoint’s product promise should stay brutally specific: it is a private decision ledger that captures what you thought would happen before reality answers back, then brings you back at the right moment to see whether your judgment held.

The primary user is someone who repeatedly makes consequential calls and wants to get better at judgment, not someone trying to “reflect more” in a vague way. The key scenario is not leisurely journaling at night. It is being in the middle of a real decision, having maybe 20 seconds of attention, and needing to log the call fast enough that the app does not lose the moment. If the interaction assumes patience, the product breaks at the exact point of use.

The core loop needs to be tighter than the prompt initially makes it sound: capture the decision, capture the prediction, pick when reality should be checked, get a prompt later, then record what actually happened while seeing your original reasoning in front of you. That “while seeing your original reasoning” part matters. If revisit is just a blank outcome form, the app becomes clerical. The emotional payoff is confronting your past self clearly and calmly.

Hard scope boundaries need to protect speed. First save should require only decision, prediction, and revisit date. Context, options considered, and confidence should exist, but they cannot block first save. I would also draw a hard line against turning the home screen into a dashboard soup. This app needs three surfaces and no more in v1: a decision list organized around upcoming and overdue revisits, a fast capture/edit flow, and a revisit/stat view. If we start adding rich tagging, folders, long-form journaling, collaboration, or “insights” written in self-help language, we will dilute the product into a generic productivity artifact.

The biggest UX risk is not just “logging must be fast.” It is that revisit can feel accusatory or annoying, which will tank completion. The reminder and revisit flow have to feel neutral, almost forensic: “Here is what you expected. What happened?” Not “time to see if you failed.” The second risk is information density. A ledger-like premium design can easily become visually severe and harder to scan. If the app looks serious but is slow to parse, it loses the moment-of-decision use case.

Measurable success criteria should be behavioral and product-facing, not vanity. A user can create a decision in well under 30 seconds on the minimum path. A revisit notification opens the exact decision and outcome flow, not a generic screen. Resolved decisions leave the active cap, so free tier remains usable. Stats are computed only from completed revisits and clearly explain what they mean. And every primary screen has a credible empty state, because an app about judgment looks unserious the moment it fills blank states with fake sample wisdom.

The detail I want preserved in the final phase output is this: this is not a decision journal in the broad sense. It is a before-vs-after calibration product. If any later choice weakens that contrast, even if it adds polish or features, it is the wrong choice.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Here's my independent take, coming at this with an eye toward what actually breaks and what's the smallest thing worth shipping.

The product promise, stated as tight as I can make it: Choicepoint lets you write down a real decision and your prediction the moment you make it, then forces a scheduled confrontation with what actually happened, so over time you get an honest, data-backed answer to "am I actually getting better at this." Not a journal, not a planner, not a habit tracker — a prediction ledger with a mandatory follow-up.

Primary user and scenario: someone who makes decisions that matter enough to regret getting wrong later but not so exotic that they need spreadsheets — a manager deciding whether to hire a candidate, a founder deciding whether to keep or kill a feature, someone deciding whether to take a job offer or move cities. The scenario that has to work is the boring one: they're mid-decision, phone in hand, thirty seconds to spare, and they type three things before doing whatever they were doing anyway. Six weeks later a notification interrupts them, and tapping it puts their own past reasoning in front of them before they've had a chance to rationalize the outcome after the fact.

Core loop, and I want to be precise about the four beats because each one is a place this fails in practice: capture (decision + prediction + a revisit date, sub-fields optional), wait (a real scheduled local notification, not a soft in-app reminder that only fires if the app happens to be open), confront (tapping the notification lands you on that exact decision's original reasoning plus a form to record what actually happened), and calibrate (a stats view that aggregates prediction-vs-outcome across everything you've resolved). If any one of those four beats is fake — a notification that doesn't survive a force-quit, a deep link that dumps you on a generic list instead of the specific decision, a stats screen seeded with placeholder numbers — the whole promise is fraudulent, not just incomplete. This is the kind of app where a demo can look done while the actual mechanism is hollow, and I don't want that gap surviving past this phase.

The biggest risk to the whole premise, which the spec itself admits via its own invalidation criteria: nobody comes back for the confront step. People are great at capturing intentions and bad at closing loops on inconvenient reminders about decisions they've already moved past emotionally. That means the smallest viable version of this app is not "add more capture fields," it's "make the reminder impossible to ignore without at least seeing your own prediction once." Practically: overdue revisits should stay visibly surfaced (not silently expire), and re-engaging with a missed revisit should take one tap, not a hunt through a menu.

Hard scope boundaries, stated as things I will actively refuse to let creep in: no cloud sync or accounts, no multi-user/shared journals, no streaks/badges/points/social feed, no general note-taking or task-list functionality, no dependency on an on-device LLM to make the core loop function (voice-note parsing is a someday feature, and if it's not trivial to build well it doesn't ship at all in this pass). Widgets and Siri capture are real deliverables but they're satellites — if either one threatens the reliability of notification delivery or the fast-capture path, it gets cut or simplified, not the other way around.

Measurable success criteria I'd actually hold this to, because "feels calm and premium" isn't verifiable and I want things that are: a user can go from empty state to a saved decision in under 15 seconds with only three fields touched; a scheduled notification actually fires after a full app force-quit and device lock (this has to be manually verified on a real device, not assumed from simulator behavior, since simulator notification timing is notoriously unreliable); tapping that notification opens the specific decision's outcome form, zero clicks of navigation required; the free tier blocks a 6th active decision but a resolved decision measurably frees a slot; and the calibration screen shows a real empty state with zero resolved decisions and only shows a percentage once there's at least one resolved outcome to compute it from — never a hardcoded "73% accurate" sample number sitting there to make the screen look alive.

The detail I don't want lost by the time we get to build: this is a two-speed app by design. Fast capture in the moment, rich reflection only if the user opts into it, and the entire value proposition rides on a background OS mechanism (local notifications) that is easy to half-implement and hard to verify without actually testing on-device with the app killed. That's the part of "production ready" that's most likely to get hand-waved, and it's the one I'd refuse to sign off on without seeing it actually fire.

**One-sentence product promise:** Choicepoint is a private decision ledger — you capture a real decision and your prediction the moment you make it, and it forces a scheduled, honest confrontation with what actually happened, so you build real evidence of whether your judgment is improving.

**Primary user and scenario:** Someone making recurring, consequential personal or professional calls (hiring, product bets, job/relocation decisions, etc.) who wants to get better at judgment, not "reflect more" in the abstract. The scenario that has to work is the unglamorous one: mid-decision, phone in hand, ~20-30 seconds of attention, typing three things before getting back to whatever they were doing — then weeks later, an interruption that puts their own past reasoning in front of them before they've had a chance to rationalize the outcome after the fact.

**Core loop (four beats, all of which must be real, not simulated):**
1. **Capture** — decision, prediction, revisit date (context/options/confidence available but never required for first save).
2. **Wait** — a real scheduled local notification that survives force-quit and device lock, not a soft in-app-only reminder.
3. **Confront** — tapping the notification deep-links straight into that exact decision, showing the original reasoning alongside a form to record what actually happened. A generic list or home screen instead of the specific decision is a broken loop, not a lesser version of one.
4. **Calibrate** — a stats view aggregating prediction-vs-outcome across resolved decisions only, from real stored data.

**Hard scope boundaries:**
- No cloud sync, accounts, multi-user/shared journals, streaks/badges/points/social feed, general note-taking or task-list functionality.
- No dependency on an on-device LLM for the core loop to function — voice-note parsing is someday-future, never a blocker.
- Widgets and Siri capture are real deliverables but are satellites: if either threatens notification reliability or fast-capture speed, they get simplified or cut before the core loop is touched.
- Three surfaces only in v1: the revisit-organized decision list, the fast capture/edit flow, and the revisit/stats view — no dashboards, tags, folders, or "insights" copy that drifts into self-help language.
- Overdue revisits stay visibly surfaced rather than silently expiring; re-engaging a missed one is one tap.

**Measurable success criteria:**
- A user can go from empty state to a saved decision in well under 20 seconds touching only three fields.
- A scheduled notification actually fires after a full force-quit and device lock — verified on a real device, not assumed from simulator behavior.
- Tapping that notification opens the specific decision's outcome form with zero extra navigation.
- The free tier blocks a 6th active decision, and a resolved decision measurably frees a slot.
- The calibration screen shows a genuine empty state with zero resolved decisions, and only ever computes a percentage from at least one real resolved outcome — never a hardcoded sample number.

The one detail everyone wants carried forward without dilution: this is a before-vs-after calibration product, not a general decision journal. Any later choice that softens that contrast — even in the name of polish — is the wrong choice.
