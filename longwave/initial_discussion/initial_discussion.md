**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

The product promise should be brutally simple: Longwave is an idle strategy game where you place and tune relay towers to keep a growing signal network efficient under interference pressure.

The primary user is someone who likes optimization games but does not want a spreadsheet or a fake idle loop. The first-session scenario should be obvious: they open a clean map with one demand zone and enough currency to place a starter relay, they connect it, throughput starts flowing immediately, then they hit the first bottleneck fast enough to learn that placement and tuning matter more than just buying another node.

The core loop needs to stay tight or this turns mushy. Earn budget from delivered throughput, place a new relay or upgrade an existing one, watch coverage and interference rebalance, fix the new bottleneck, then let the network run while offline and come back to the consequences. The key is that every action must change the visible network state. If the player cannot look at the map and tell why income rose or fell, the game has already lost its identity.

From a UX and information architecture standpoint, I would keep the MVP to three real surfaces. The main network map is the product. It needs to show relay range, active links, congested segments, and interference hotspots without making the player decode RF jargon. A side control panel handles build, upgrade, and per-node tuning. A compact progression screen shows objectives, unlocked node types, and offline gains summary. Anything beyond that is probably vanity for MVP.

The hard scope boundaries should be explicit. One map theme, not multiple biomes. Three node types at most: a cheap broad relay, a focused high-throughput relay, and a stabilizer or booster that reduces interference locally. One main resource, likely “bandwidth credits,” not a pile of currencies. No tech tree maze. No story campaign. No PvP, no cloud sync, no social layer, no procedural world generator, no literal RF simulation. Most importantly, no mechanic where the optimal play is just tapping upgrades in a menu while ignoring the map.

The biggest risk in the obvious direction is hidden math. “Interference” sounds interesting, but if it is just an invisible debuff under the hood, the player will experience it as random punishment. The final decision must preserve that interference, congestion, and coverage are visual first and numeric second. Red overlap zones, link saturation indicators, and before/after feedback on placement are not polish here; they are the mechanic.

The other quiet failure mode is overloading the first ten minutes. If the player has to understand routing, bandwidth classes, terrain attenuation, directional antennas, and offline yield penalties immediately, the app will feel like homework. The MVP should teach one tradeoff well: broader coverage creates more overlap and worse efficiency, while tighter routing boosts throughput but leaves dead zones. That alone is enough to make the game feel distinct.

Measurable success criteria should be concrete. A new player can explain the game in one sentence after two minutes. Within the first session, they make at least three meaningful placement or tuning decisions with visible outcomes. They can create a bad network state and recover from it without restarting. Offline progress after a few minutes away reflects actual network performance, not a flat fake reward. By the point they stop, they have learned that expanding coverage blindly is suboptimal. If those things are true, the app has a real spine; if not, it is still just an idle skin.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Here's my independent read on this, coming at it from what could break and what the smallest shippable version actually looks like.

The one-sentence product promise I'd commit to: Longwave is an idle game where you build a radio-relay network across a constrained map, and the interesting decision every session is trading off coverage breadth against interference and throughput — not "wait for numbers to go up," but "reroute this link because it's choking on interference from that other relay." If a build of this app can't produce that decision moment inside the first five minutes of a cold launch, the app has failed regardless of how much content exists behind it.

Primary user: someone who already plays idle/incremental games (Cookie Clicker descendants, Cell to Singularity, Mini Metro fans specifically) and checks in for 2-5 minute sessions several times a day, plus a "come back after 8 hours and see what accumulated" moment once or twice a day. This is not a hardcore strategy player and not a casual match-3 player — it's someone who likes systems that reward a few minutes of thinking, then leaves it alone. That scenario shape has direct consequences: the UI has to make "what should I fix right now" obvious within seconds of opening, because that's the actual usage pattern, not long sit-down sessions.

Core loop, stated plainly: place a relay, it starts producing signal/throughput toward a demand zone; as you add relays, interference and bandwidth limits start capping your effective output; you spend currency to upgrade or reposition relays, unlock a new relay type, or expand into new coverage area; each of those choices trades something against something else (more coverage now costs more interference, or more throughput on one link starves another); progress accumulates while the app is closed and you come back to collect it and make the next round of decisions. That's the whole loop. Anything added to it should be tested against "does this create a new tradeoff, or is it just a bigger number."

Now the risk case, because this is where I'd push back if I were reviewing someone else's plan: the single most likely way this MVP quietly fails is by having two tradeoffs half-built instead of one tradeoff fully built. The contract mentions "interference vs throughput" and "coverage vs efficiency" as if either is fine — for an MVP I want exactly one of these picked as the spine, built completely, tuned, and legible, with the other omitted or reduced to flavor text. Two shallow tradeoffs will read as more content but will actually be less coherent and harder to balance in the time available. My recommendation is interference vs. throughput as the core one, because it's the one that most naturally produces a visible, moment-to-moment feedback loop (a relay's number visibly drops when you place another too close), whereas coverage vs. efficiency is more of a map-level, longer-horizon decision that's harder to make legible in a small UI.

Second risk: offline-progress math and save persistence are the two things every idle game gets subtly wrong, and they're also the two things a build agent will be most tempted to fake or approximate under time pressure because they don't show up in a screenshot. I want it on record now, before design/architecture phases run, that "offline earnings calculated correctly for elapsed wall-clock time including backgrounding, force-quit, and clock changes" and "save/load survives repeated relaunch with zero silent data loss" are both first-class acceptance criteria, tested explicitly, not assumed to work because the happy path looked fine once in the simulator.

Third risk, smaller but real: idle games live or die on numeric pacing, and pacing is invisible in a code review — a build can compile, run, look polished, and still be un-fun because the curve is either trivially solved in ten minutes or grindy and flat. I don't have a way to fully solve that in this phase, but I want a placeholder acceptance check now: there must be at least three distinct "next goal" moments reachable in a single sitting (first relay placed, first interference conflict resolved, first new relay type or region unlocked), each within a few minutes of each other, so a tester can verify pacing exists rather than just trusting the spreadsheet.

Hard scope boundaries I'd lock in for this pass: one starting map/region for the MVP, with at most one additional unlockable region gated behind progress or the paid tier — not an open-ended world. A small, fixed roster of relay types (three to four, not a tech tree with a dozen). No live-ops content, no server-driven events, no leaderboards or social comparison of any kind (that's explicitly other portfolio slots' territory and also just scope we don't have time to test properly). No ad-based monetization loop with rewarded-video mechanics — if monetization is a paid tier, keep it a single IAP or subscription unlocking a defined second layer (second region, extra relay type, deeper offline cap), not a store screen with five SKUs. No random-events or procedural-generation system for the MVP; the constraint system (interference/throughput) should be deterministic and inspectable, both because it's easier to test and because a player needs to be able to reason about it to feel smart.

Measurable success criteria I'd actually hold a build to, stated as things a tester can check rather than vibes: cold launch to first placeable relay in under 30 seconds with no tutorial wall; first interference-driven decision point reachable within 5 minutes of first launch; a save/relaunch cycle run 20+ times in a row produces byte-identical or correctly-progressed state with no resets or duplication; an offline period of at least 8 hours (simulated by manipulating elapsed time, not by actually waiting) produces earnings matching the documented formula exactly; the app survives being force-quit mid-placement and mid-upgrade without corrupting the save; every one of the handful of screens (network view, relay detail/upgrade, region-unlock or monetization screen, settings) has a real empty state and a real error state, not just a success state screenshot. If a later phase can't produce evidence against these, it isn't done, it's demoed.
