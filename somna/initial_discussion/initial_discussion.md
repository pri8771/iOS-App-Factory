**Claude (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

Coming into this fresh with an IA and systems lens, here's where my head goes on Somna.

The one-sentence promise I'd lock: "Somna turns two ten-second daily logs into a plain-English answer for why you feel rested or wrecked." Not "sleep tracker" — the deliverable is the sentence of insight, the logging is just the fuel. If the final phase output frames this as a tracking app with insights bolted on, that's already a subtle mis-scope; it should be framed as an insight app with lightweight logging as the mechanism.

Primary user and scenario I'd anchor on: someone half-conscious at 11pm and half-conscious at 7am, opening the app twice a day for a few seconds each time, who checks in maybe once a week to actually read something. That means the app has two very different modes that need distinct IA: a "fast capture" mode (bedtime tap, wake tap, morning check-in) that should basically never require navigation — ideally reachable from a single tap on launch, not buried behind a tab — and a "reflection" mode (recap, insights, history) that's a slower, more spacious, read-only experience. I'd structure this as effectively two homes: whatever screen the app opens to should look at the current time of day and system state and decide "this person probably wants to log something right now" vs "this person is checking in on their trends," and lead with the right one rather than making both compete for space on one dashboard. A tab bar with "Today / Insights / History / Settings" is the safe IA choice, but the real design risk is making "Today" do too much — it needs to visually collapse to almost nothing once nothing is actionable (you already logged bedtime, wake, and check-in — show a quiet "done for today" state, not a live dashboard begging for attention).

The core loop as I'd state it: log bedtime → (sleep happens) → log wake + morning check-in → weekly recap surfaces → correlation insights update → that insight subtly changes tonight's behavior → log bedtime again. The thing I want preserved explicitly: the loop only has value if step 5 (insight changes behavior) actually closes back into step 1, and nothing in the UI should make that link invisible — e.g., if an insight says "you feel better with 7+ hours," the bedtime logging screen is a very natural place to quietly echo "target bedtime: 10:45pm to hit 7.5h" so the loop is visually closed, not two disconnected features.

Now the systems-thinker part, because this is where I think this app can quietly break in ways nobody's named yet. A "night" is not a clean unit — it's two independent events (bedtime tap, wake tap) that get stitched together after the fact, and the stitching logic is genuinely the riskiest piece of the whole data model. Concretely: what happens if someone logs bedtime at 11pm Monday, forgets Tuesday entirely, then logs a wake time Wednesday morning — does that wake event attach to Monday's dangling bedtime (wrong, that's a 30-hour "night"), attach to nothing (correct, but then Wednesday's morning check-in has no sleep data to pair with), or prompt the user to reconcile? I think the only sane rule is a session has a plausibility window — a wake log only auto-pairs with an open bedtime log if the gap is under some ceiling like 16 hours, otherwise the bedtime log is silently closed out as "incomplete/no wake logged" and the new wake log starts a fresh orphan session waiting for its own bedtime data. That needs to be a real rule in the model, not implicit behavior that happens to work in the demo because nobody tested logging out of order.

Second systemic risk: naps. Nothing in the spec mentions them, but the moment someone logs a bedtime at 2pm because they're a shift worker or logs a second "bedtime" the same calendar day, the "one night per day" assumption breaks. I don't think this MVP needs to solve naps, but the data model and correlation engine need to not silently corrupt when it happens — I'd rather have sessions keyed by their own start/end timestamps rather than by calendar date, so an odd-hours session is just an odd-hours session, not a crash or a duplicate-key collision.

Third: the correlation engine's gating threshold has direct IA consequences, not just a computation detail. The Insights screen needs at least three distinct visual states, not two — "still learning" (below threshold, show encouraging progress toward the number), "here's what we found" (threshold met, showing real insights), and a third state people will hit constantly that I don't think has been named yet: "logged enough nights, but this specific tag has no signal" (e.g., always drinks coffee, so there's no contrast group to compare against). That's not an error and it's not "still learning" — it's a legitimate null result, and if the UI conflates it with "still learning" the user will feel like the app is stuck at 0% forever even after logging for a month. I want that third state explicitly named as required, because it's the most likely one to get skipped since it's not in the spec's own language.

The scope boundary I'd add on top of what's already locked: no manual free-text notes field in v1. It's tempting to add "add a note" to the morning check-in for richness, but that directly fights the tap-only, sub-3-tap fast path already agreed on, and free text can't be correlated by a rule-based engine anyway — it would just be a graveyard of unused data. Tags only, chosen from a small fixed set.

For measurable success criteria, I'd want something sharper than "it works": (1) a cold-start user can complete a full bedtime-log-to-morning-check-in cycle in under 15 seconds combined without hitting a required text field; (2) with zero data, every screen (Today, Insights, History, Recap) renders a real designed empty state, not a blank view or a crash; (3) the correlation engine produces zero insights below its sample-size threshold and at least one correctly-worded insight once synthetic data crosses it, verifiable by seeding test data rather than waiting three real weeks; (4) an out-of-order or skipped log (wake with no bedtime, bedtime with no wake for 3+ nights) never crashes the app or corrupts the next night's pairing.
