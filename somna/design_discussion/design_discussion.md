SwiftData for nightly logs and morning check-ins; correlation engine computed on-device; optional read-only HealthKit import of sleep duration, never a requirement.

Repository boundary allows swapping the local store for CloudKit sync later; correlation engine stays fully on-device regardless of sync status for privacy.

None required; the correlation engine is simple on-device statistics, the honest and battery-safe choice over sensor-heavy sleep-staging.

Let me lay out where I land on this, coming at it purely as the person who has to imagine someone half-asleep at 11pm and slightly groggy at 7am actually using this thing.

I'd anchor the whole IA around three tabs, not more: Today, Insights, History — with a gear icon in the Today nav bar for Settings rather than a fourth tab, since settings gets touched once and shouldn't compete for thumb real estate with the screens people actually live in. Today is the launch screen, always, no exceptions — this app should never open to a dashboard or a tab picker when the whole pitch is "two ten-second taps." Today itself needs to be a single view that mutates through a small number of states rather than four different screens: a true first-launch empty state (moon glyph, one sentence of what this app does, one button — "Log Tonight's Bedtime"), an evening/pre-bedtime state once at least one cycle exists, an "asleep" state after bedtime is logged (a dimmed, slow-breathing card showing elapsed time since bedtime, with the bedtime button now reading "Update Bedtime" and a second, equally prominent "I'm Awake" button always available — because someone might wake at 3am, 6am, or after a 20-minute nap, and the screen shouldn't assume it knows which), and a "done for today" state after both are logged, which should feel like the screen exhaling — collapsed, quiet, showing just last night's duration and energy as a small glanceable summary plus the streak, with no further call to action nagging the user. This is the single most important product decision in this whole phase: the done state has to visibly and specifically reflect the morning check-in, not just the bedtime tap, because that's the one thing standing between this app and the exact churn pattern already flagged upstream.

Bedtime logging is its own lightweight sheet, not a full screen push — time defaults to now, one adjustable control, one confirm button, and if a session's already open, that same sheet just edits it in place rather than pretending to create a new one. Wake + check-in is also a sheet, and I want to be strict about the tap count because "sub-3-taps" is easy to violate by accident: tap 1 opens the sheet (wake time already defaulted to now), tap 2 is a single tap on a 5-point energy scale rendered as large horizontal segments (not a slider, not a numeric stepper — something you can hit accurately half-asleep), tap 3 is "Done." Tags live behind a single "Add detail" disclosure that's visually present but recessed — expanding it doesn't count against the fast path, but it also can't be hidden so deep nobody ever finds it, since tags are the entire fuel for the per-tag insight engine that's supposed to justify the subscription. If there's no open bedtime session when this sheet is triggered, it still submits fine as a standalone check-in — I would not add any messaging apologizing for that or explaining it'll "count later," because that adds a reading tax to a screen that's supposed to be operable half-conscious; it should just work silently and correctly.

Insights is where I want to spend the most design care, because it's the one screen carrying the entire differentiation claim. Three states, and I'd render them as genuinely different visual compositions, not the same layout with different text swapped in: still-learning shows progress toward the threshold using the moon-phase motif itself — a crescent that visibly fills as nights accumulate, with copy like "4 more nights until your first insight," which reuses the icon system as a functional progress indicator instead of a generic percentage bar, and ties the brand mark to the thing the user is actually waiting on. Real-insight shows one or more cards, each with the associational sentence plus a small two-bar comparison (e.g., "7+ hours" vs "under 7 hours," average energy) rendered in the same soft-glow card language — free tier sees exactly one of these (duration vs. energy), and any locked per-tag insight appears as a real card with the actual comparison bars blurred and a small "Unlock with Somna+" affordance overlaid, not a generic marketing panel — showing the shape of the value you're not getting yet is a lot more honest and a lot more persuasive than hiding it entirely. No-signal-for-tag gets its own visually distinct card type — I'd use a flat/muted treatment versus the glowing insight cards, with copy like "You almost always log caffeine — we can't compare yet," so it reads as a specific observation about the user's own data, not a system error state.

The weekly recap needs to live in two places, not one: a compact card threaded into Today once there's at least one logged night (this is what gets glanced at organically), and its own full-bleed screen reachable by tapping that card, which is what ShareLink actually exports. I would not make recap a fourth tab — it's a weekly-cadence artifact, not a daily-use screen, and giving it permanent tab real estate undersells how often people would actually want it versus Today/Insights.

Visually: the gradient should be driven by real time-of-day, not just a static "sleep app is dark" theme — deep indigo-to-violet through the evening and overnight hours, shifting to soft dawn peach/rose tones roughly from early morning through midday, independent of system light/dark mode (a night-shift worker opening this at 2pm should still get a calm daytime gradient, not a jarring dark screen because their iPhone happens to be in dark mode). Cards sit on a near-opaque frosted surface over the gradient, generous corner radius, soft outer glow rather than hard shadow, minimal rounded typography, and motion that's slow cross-fades and gentle scale — no bouncy springs anywhere, because "restful" is a real constraint on the animation curve library, not just the color palette. Haptics should be a single soft impact on the energy-scale tap and nowhere else; this app should feel like the one thing on your phone that doesn't buzz at you.

Accessibility specifics I want preserved verbatim into build: the energy-scale control must expose itself as an adjustable VoiceOver element with a spoken value name ("Energy: Rested," not "4 of 5"), not just a number: cards must sit on a guaranteed-contrast fill so gradient shifts never threaten text legibility (this was already flagged upstream and the visual system needs to bake it in structurally, not rely on someone remembering it per-screen); Dynamic Type up to accessibility sizes must cause cards to reflow rather than truncate, which means no fixed-height insight cards; tag chips and the bedtime/wake time controls need real 44pt targets even though they're visually compact; and reduced-motion should collapse the moon-phase fill animation and gradient time-of-day transitions to instant cross-fades rather than skip them outright, since the state information they carry (progress, time-of-day) still needs to reach a reduced-motion user some way.

The single biggest risk to my own proposal is the time-of-day-driven gradient: it's a nice idea but it's an extra dimension of visual state that has to be explicitly mapped (which hours map to which gradient stops, what happens at the boundary) or it'll either flicker inconsistently or just get skipped under time pressure and quietly become "always dark," which would be a real loss against the spec's explicit "lightening toward dawn tones in daytime views" requirement. I'd rather see that mapping nailed down now — three or four fixed time bands, each with a named gradient — than left as a vague aspiration that the build phase interprets as "make it dark mode." The other thing I'd fight hard to keep in the final output: the "done for today" state on Today explicitly and visibly reacting to the morning check-in specifically, and the no-signal-for-tag card being visually distinct from still-learning. Both are easy to quietly merge away under build pressure, and both are the exact details this whole five-phase discussion history has been protecting.
