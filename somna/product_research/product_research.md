Freemium, $4.99/mo or $29.99/yr for Somna+.

SwiftData for nightly logs and morning check-ins; correlation engine computed on-device; optional read-only HealthKit import of sleep duration, never a requirement.

Repository boundary allows swapping the local store for CloudKit sync later; correlation engine stays fully on-device regardless of sync status for privacy.

None required; the correlation engine is simple on-device statistics, the honest and battery-safe choice over sensor-heavy sleep-staging.

Nocturnal calm gradient system — deep indigo-to-violet dusk gradients lightening toward soft dawn tones in daytime views, soft-glow rounded components, a moon-phase mark, restful rather than clinical.

The realistic audience is people who already tried Sleep Cycle, Apple's own Health app sleep tracking, or a Whoop/Oura and bounced off either the cost, the hardware, or the fact that the output was a hypnogram nobody could act on. Comparable apps worth learning from, with the caveat that this is my own general knowledge of the category rather than fresh research: Sleep Cycle (mic/motion sensing, graph-heavy, subscription for trends), AutoSleep and Pillow (HealthKit-dependent, assume everyone's wearing an Apple Watch, which breaks for a chunk of the target audience described in this spec), and simpler streak-style habit trackers like Streaks or Way of Life for the check-in/streak mechanic. The gap Somna is explicitly aiming at — plain-English causal-flavored insight instead of a graph — is closer to what Bearable or Exist.io try to do for mood/symptom correlation, just narrower and sleep-specific. The risk with that comparison: those apps are widely known for taking a long time to feel useful and for people abandoning the daily log before the insight engine has enough data to say anything, which is the exact invalidation criteria already flagged upstream. That's not a new risk I'm surfacing, it's the central one, and it should shape the build, not just be acknowledged.

Platform-specific opportunity: this is a genuinely good Live Activity / Dynamic Island use case if it's scoped honestly — a quiet bedtime countdown that turns into "log your wake-up" as a Live Activity tap target the moment a wake time is plausible, is a much lower-friction path into the morning check-in than expecting someone to remember to open the app. That said, I'd treat that as a nice-to-have layered on top of a working in-app flow, not a load-bearing part of the core loop, because ActivityKit has real footguns — activities can silently fail to start if budget/permissions are off, background updates aren't guaranteed to fire on time, and testing it thoroughly in a simulator is limited (you basically need a device to trust it). WidgetKit's timeline refresh has a similar honesty problem: "tonight's target bedtime" on a widget is easy, "last night's score" updates reliably only if the timeline provider is reloaded at the right moments, which won't happen automatically just because the user logged something in the app unless that reload is explicitly triggered (WidgetCenter.shared.reloadTimelines) at every write path.

The biggest risk I'd flag that isn't just a restatement of the spec's own risk section: correlation-of-tiny-N statistics are dangerous even with an honest "still learning" gate, because the gate itself needs a real threshold decision (how many nights, how many nights with each tag present vs absent) and if that threshold is set carelessly you get a different failure mode — the app confidently declares something is "still learning" forever because the bar was set too high, and users who did log consistently for three weeks never see a single insight, which kills trust just as fast as false confidence would. This needs a concrete, testable number decided at build time (something like: minimum 5 nights with a tag present and 5 without, over at least 10 total logged nights, before that specific tag correlation is shown), not a vague "enough data" check.

Second risk: the data model has to gracefully handle the messy real-world logging pattern of bedtime logged one night and wake time logged the next morning as two separate taps, possibly on two different calendar dates, possibly with the user skipping one side entirely for a night. If bedtime/wake are modeled as a single record created at bedtime-log time, the morning check-in flow needs to reliably find "last night's still-open session" across a midnight boundary and DST transition, or you get orphaned records and the correlation engine silently drops nights instead of surfacing an error the user can act on.

Third risk, more of a build-priority one: HealthKit and Live Activity and the widget are three separate pieces of native-platform surface area that can each eat a day of debugging for marginal user-facing benefit compared to just making the core two-tap logging flow and the insight screen excellent. Given "smaller finished app over a larger half-built app" is a stated global rule, my instinct is the first build should nail logging, check-in, recap, and a correctly-gated insight engine with the paywall wired in, and treat HealthKit/Live Activity/widget as the first things cut in scope if time runs short — which the prompt contract already anticipated by calling those "degrade gracefully," and I'd keep that ordering.

For the first build specifically, the implication I want preserved: seed a way to test the correlation engine against more than zero real nights of data without literally waiting three weeks — either a debug-only seed data path or unit tests against synthetic logs — because otherwise nobody building this can verify the "still learning" gate or the insight text actually flips into showing real numbers, and that logic is the entire product differentiator per the spec's own positioning.
