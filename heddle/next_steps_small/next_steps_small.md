What actually proves Heddle works isn't "does the algorithm generate valid loom puzzles" — that's a solved technical problem, we already know how. What proves it is whether a stranger who's never seen a heddle before can pick up the phone, trace their first thread without reading a word, feel that small satisfying pull-taut-and-settle moment, and want to do it again tomorrow. That's the entire bet. Everything in the slice should serve proving or disproving that one thing, and almost nothing else.

So here's the boldest-but-still-smallest version I'd build first: one generator, one puzzle screen, one hub, one settings screen — and I mean truly one, not "MVP versions of four screens." Cold launch straight into a small, generated, guaranteed-solvable loom with no splash screen, no permission prompts up front, no "welcome to Heddle" anything. The player's thumb is on a thread within one second of first launch. If they can't figure out what to do without text, the whole premise is dead and no amount of downstream polish saves it — so this is the thing I'd stake the entire first slice on getting right, before I'd spend a single hour on the shop, the archive, or the paywall.

Must-have interactions, and I'd hold this list to a hard minimum: drag-to-trace a thread from a colored end to its match; the discrete tap-chain equivalent for VoiceOver (not an afterthought bolted on later — it has to work in the very first build, because retrofitting it is exactly the trap we already flagged); a visible, obviously-reachable undo, because "no fail state" is a hollow promise if the player has no way to unwind a tangle they regret; and the solve moment itself — haptic pulse plus a visual settle/bloom — which is doing more brand work than every other screen combined and deserves disproportionate craft time relative to its apparent simplicity. That's four interactions. I would not add a fifth for this slice, even something as tempting as a hint button — a hint button is a good idea but it's a second-order feature solving a problem (players getting stuck) we don't yet know how often actually happens, and it can wait one slice.

Now the cut list, and I want to be aggressive here because the instinct on a "puzzle game" brief is always to over-scope: no shop screen with real entitlement gating in this slice — a visibly-present but inert "Loom Pass" button that goes nowhere yet is enough to prove the paywall point exists architecturally, per the earlier phases' decision that monetization needs to be present, not live. No difficulty tiers beyond one or two hand-picked settings — the "custom generator panel" that's the whole subscription value proposition later is explicitly out of this slice; it's a second-slice feature once the core loop is validated, not a first-slice one. No archive/history browsing beyond "yesterday's puzzle exists somewhere" — a full 7-day rolling list is real work for a feature nobody will touch until they've solved puzzle number one. No share-image generation — genuinely great growth idea, explicitly deferred, not because it's unimportant but because growth mechanics are worthless if the core loop underneath them isn't validated first. No streak counter yet, even the gentle count-up kind — it's a retention feature for a product that doesn't have proven day-one retention yet. And no cosmetic skins at all — zero, not even one alternate palette — because that's pure production polish spent on a hypothesis (that people will pay for skins) we haven't earned the right to test yet.

The one place I will not compromise, even to keep the slice small: this still has to be a real generator producing genuinely solvable puzzles algorithmically, not three or four hand-authored puzzles hardcoded to fake it. That's tempting as a shortcut — "just ship 5 fixed loom layouts for the demo" — but it would quietly gut the one hard technical capability this whole app is supposed to prove out, and if we defer it we'll never come back and build the real thing under less time pressure than we have right now. Small scope, not fake scope.

Validation criteria, stated so someone can actually check them rather than eyeball a screenshot and shrug: a person handed the phone cold, with zero explanation, solves the first puzzle within 60 seconds using only touch, no reading required. Backgrounding or force-quitting mid-puzzle and reopening the app restores the exact in-progress thread positions, not a fresh board. The haptic settle on solve is felt and judged "satisfying, not gimmicky" by at least one person testing on a real device — Simulator doesn't count, full stop. And the generator, run against its one or two locked difficulty settings a few hundred times in a row, never produces an unsolvable puzzle. If all five of those hold, we've proven the concept and earned the right to build the shop, the archive, the streak, and the skins in the next slice.
