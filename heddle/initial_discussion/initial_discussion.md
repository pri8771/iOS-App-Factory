**Claude (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

Here's my read on this, coming at it from the screens-and-flows side rather than the business side, since that's where I think the real risk still sits even after three phases of groundwork.

The product promise I'd lock in: Heddle is a one-touch weaving puzzle where you trace threads across a small loom to connect matching ends, and the whole point is that pulling a thread taut feels like a small physical act, not a cognitive test. If I had to defend that in one sentence to a skeptic: it's Flow Free's path-tracing logic wearing a loom's clothing instead of a circuit board's, played for two to eight minutes at a time by someone who wants their hands occupied and their brain unbothered.

On the core loop, I want to be the one who commits to something concrete here rather than leaving "core loop" as a placeholder for a later phase to fill in, since this phase's required output explicitly asks for it and the phase rule says resolve ambiguity decisively. My proposal: each puzzle is a small grid of loom pegs with colored thread ends seeded around the edges and interior; the player drags (or, for VoiceOver, taps origin then taps successive cells) a continuous thread from one end to its matching color, and the puzzle resolves when every cell on the loom is covered by exactly one thread with no crossings. That's learnable in one wordless attempt because drag-a-line-to-its-match is about as close to a universal gesture as exists in mobile puzzle design, it's proven procedurally generatable with guaranteed solvability (generate the solution paths first, then strip them down to just the endpoints — this is well-trodden ground, not a research problem), and it naturally has both a continuous-gesture path and a discrete tap-path, which solves the accessibility constraint the last phase locked in rather than fighting it. I'd leave grid size, color count, and difficulty pacing to the dedicated mechanic phase — but the shape of the loop itself I think should be decided now, because everything else in this phase (screens, states, success criteria) depends on knowing whether we're building a tracing game, a routing game, or something else.

Primary user and scenario, concretely: someone lying in bed or on a couch, phone in one hand, thumb doing all the work, for a single puzzle or two before they put the phone down — not a session-length app, a between-things app. That has a real UX consequence I want flagged now: the app needs to be fully resumable to the exact in-progress thread state after a hard backgrounding or force-quit, not just resumable between completed puzzles. If someone gets pulled away mid-thread and comes back to a reset grid, that's a worse experience than a loading spinner, because it silently destroys the small thing they were doing with their hands. That's a persistence requirement beyond "save completed puzzles" — it's "save the live board state continuously," and I don't think it's been said explicitly anywhere yet.

The screen inventory I'd want preserved: a first-launch puzzle that IS the tutorial (no onboarding carousel, no "welcome to Heddle" screen — the first thing on screen is a small, obviously-solvable loom); a home/hub screen that's really just "today's puzzle" plus access to past puzzles and the (stubbed) shop; the puzzle screen itself with its four states — generating (loading, should be near-instant but must be handled as real async, not assumed synchronous), ready-to-play, solved (the celebration moment, which is doing a lot of the "warm and tactile" brand work), and a generation-failure error state that falls back to a cached or simpler puzzle rather than showing a dead end; and a settings screen with the accessibility and data controls. I'd resist adding more screens than that for MVP — a streak/stats screen or a cosmetics gallery can wait, they're not load-bearing for the core promise.

The biggest risk to my own direction: grid size is going to be in direct tension with the 44pt minimum hit target that's already a hard requirement. A visually rich, "many interconnected threads" puzzle wants a denser grid; a dense grid on an iPhone SE-class screen either shrinks pegs below 44pt or forces the puzzle to stay small and visually modest for the entire MVP. I'd rather name that trade now and cap puzzle complexity to whatever fits comfortably at 44pt on the smallest supported device, than let the mechanic phase discover it late and either violate accessibility or redesign under pressure.

Hard scope boundaries I want on the record: no multiplayer, no leaderboards, no social sharing, no streak mechanic that can break or reset (an optional streak that only ever counts up, never guilts), no more than a small curated set of hand-tunable difficulty tiers for MVP even though puzzles themselves are generated infinitely, and no real payment processing — entitlement flags and paywall UI only, per the earlier phase's decision.

Measurable success criteria, stated so they're checkable and not vibes: a first-time user reaches a solved first puzzle within 60 seconds without reading any text; force-quitting mid-puzzle and relaunching restores the exact thread state, not just the puzzle list; the entire core loop — select puzzle, trace thread, solve, advance — is completable using only VoiceOver and discrete taps, no drag required; every one of the four screens above shows correct empty/loading/success/error content, not a placeholder; and haptic feedback is verified on a physical device, not just assumed from Simulator behavior, before the build is called done. If any of those fail, I don't think we get to call this finished regardless of how the art looks.
