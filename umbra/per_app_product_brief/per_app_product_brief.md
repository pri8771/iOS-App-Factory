**Codex (Design Lead · Systems Thinker) — Round 1**

Umbra is for premium-style mobile puzzle players who want high-signal, deterministic spatial thinking in short sessions, and a small creator cohort that enjoys making one good challenge and sending it to a friend as a personal puzzle. The use case is a five-minute commute or break where someone wants a clear, tactile challenge, then optionally turns around and exports a level they made or edited.

The product exists to make shadow geometry feel like a tool, not a gimmick: players drag/rotate a light source and occluders to make the shadow cast match a target silhouette, with the same canvas and controls used in both play and design so “solving” and “making” feel like one system. The app keeps users coming back because each solved level can immediately become a remix candidate, giving the user a reason to replay and iterate instead of just complete-and-exit.

Paid value is unlocked by utility: a free starter vault gives 5+ high-quality levels plus core editor/play parity, while paid challenge packs add new mechanics, themes, and difficulty tiers that materially expand time-to-mastery. Optional subscription unlocks a true creator premium layer: constrained-problem templates, symmetry/precision constraints, solver analytics (attempt quality, solve angle patterns), batch-export tools, and prioritized new content each month. This is not skins or cosmetic convenience; it buys better content and better making power.

The subscription value is that it converts the app from “nice puzzle toy” into a reusable design-and-practice workflow for people who care about challenge density and puzzle quality. Subscribers can build and tune challenge sets they trust, then run repeatable sessions with friends using shareable codes that import exactly as authored.

The competitive wedge is not “shadow puzzle” alone; it’s deterministic Play/Edit parity plus a production-grade share contract that other geometric puzzle apps often skip. Most competitors in this space either have strong art or strong puzzle content, but not a validated round-trip artifact system with explicit recovery behavior. Winning here means if you share a code, it loads predictably and says exactly why it fails when it can’t.

Viral growth is niche-first and high trust: users grow the game by sharing compact level codes directly via standard iOS Share Sheet, especially after a solve or clever remix. Growth is strongest when the sender can confidently say, “open and play this exact puzzle in minutes,” and the recipient gets either instant success or a clear, specific import error instead of a silent failure.

Local-first behavior is core: all state is local-first and deterministic by design, with automatic saves, forced-quit recovery, offline mode by default, and robust handling for empty/corrupt local saves. No accounts and no backend for v1, so privacy and responsiveness are guaranteed from day one.

The cloud-ready path is a thin persistence seam, not an architecture rewrite: keep one canonical versioned level schema and a separate serialization adapter layer for local JSON/store. If cloud sync is added later, it syncs the same immutable level records and progress snapshots, not gameplay logic.

Design and systems-wise, the app must preserve three non-negotiables to stay believable: same geometry primitives in play and edit, versioned serialization with exact import/export identity, and non-color success feedback (outline, overlap score, haptic cue, and clear VoiceOver states). Biggest risk is that these guarantees get treated as hard engineering details and get deferred behind UX, which would erode trust; the brief must explicitly preserve the solve-check parity and round-trip contract as first-class product requirements.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Closer) — Round 1**

Umbra is a local-first SwiftUI puzzle app where a player positions a light source and occluders on a canvas until the cast shadow matches a target silhouette, and where the exact same primitives double as a level editor that exports to a compact shareable code. The person paying for this is the existing premium-puzzle buyer — someone who's already bought Monument Valley or The Witness, plays in five-to-ten-minute deterministic sessions, and expects the free portion of the app to feel complete rather than gated-to-frustration. The free tier is a starter vault of five-plus authored levels plus full editor and share functionality, because a designer/share mode that's locked behind a paywall isn't a real designer/share mode, it's a demo — and a demo doesn't generate the friend-to-friend share codes that are this app's only growth mechanism. What people pay for is more content and more capability: themed/difficulty challenge-pack bundles as one-time or subscription unlocks, and a pro editor tier with symmetry/constraint tools and solve-history analytics for the smaller creator segment who wants to build harder puzzles than the base toolset supports. That's a defensible subscription because the thing being sold is more puzzles and more power, not a skin or an ad-removal toggle.

The core loop is vault, play, solve, optional remix into edit on that exact level, then share or save — and I want to be blunt about where this loop actually breaks in the field, because that's my job this phase. It breaks at three points, and if any one of them isn't solid the whole "real designer/share mode" promise collapses into a marketing claim nobody can verify. First, the solve-detection itself: if the match feedback is even slightly wrong at the edges — a shadow that's touching-only, a near-zero-area sliver, a rotation that's one degree off from what the UI implies — the player stops trusting the app's judgment of their own puzzle, and that kind of trust doesn't come back after the first bad experience. Second, editor-to-solver parity: if someone can build and save a level in the editor that the solve-checker can't correctly grade later (light source parked inside an occluder, a target silhouette with no achievable full-overlap state), that's not a rare bug, it's a certainty unless save-time validation runs through the identical solve-checker used in play — I'm not going to accept "we'll catch that in QA," it needs to be structurally impossible to save an ungraded level. Third, and this is the one I'd stake the phase on: the share artifact. A friend receiving a corrupted or truncated code from a copy-paste or a messaging app mangling special characters isn't a rare edge case, it's an expected, routine failure mode for every single share attempt, and it needs three distinct honest error states — truncated, version-mismatched, geometrically-invalid — not one generic "couldn't import this." If import fails silently or vaguely, the app's entire viral mechanism dies at the one moment it was supposed to fire, and there's no other growth lever to fall back on since remote publishing is explicitly off the table.

The competitive wedge against Shadowmatic-style silhouette games and generic puzzle-of-the-day apps is that the editor isn't a toy bolted onto a game — it's the same primitive set as play, so anything you can solve you can also remix, and anything you remix is provably identical when it reopens on a friend's phone, because that's not a marketing claim but a testable round-trip contract (encode, decode, diff, zero drift). Competitors either don't let you build levels at all, or their "editor" is a stripped-down toy that can't express what the game engine actually supports — Umbra's bet is that closing that gap is worth more to a premium-puzzle buyer than five extra built-in levels would be. The growth mechanism is narrow and deliberately non-social: a level code sent directly to one person as a personal challenge, distributed through iMessage or whatever the recipient already uses, with zero backend and zero discovery feed. That's a niche viral loop, not a broad one, and I'd rather ship that honestly than pretend a two-person share pattern is going to produce app-store-chart virality.

Local-first is non-negotiable for v1: SwiftUI, on-device persistence with auto-save, forced-quit recovery that resumes exact in-progress geometry, and every reachable flow — play, edit, save, load, export, import — carrying real empty/loading/success/error states rather than happy-path-only screens. The cloud-ready seam I'd actually build now, even though nothing needs it yet: the level schema gets a version field from day one, and the persistence layer is written behind a small protocol boundary (something like a LevelStore interface) so that a future CloudKit-backed sync implementation is a new conformance, not a rewrite of the geometry engine or the UI layer. I want that seam named explicitly now because retrofitting versioning after real share codes exist in the wild is a much worse problem than a one-line field today.

What actually keeps someone using this past the first session isn't more levels, it's whether the first three solves and the first edit-and-share attempt all worked exactly as promised with zero confusion about why a near-miss shadow didn't count — accessibility cues (outline, tick, haptic, numeric overlap) aren't a nice-to-have layered on top of that, they're the mechanism that makes "why didn't that work" legible to every player, not just the ones who can distinguish color. I'd hold the line that this app isn't done until the acceptance pack — five-plus solvable fixture levels, the round-trip diff test, and fuzzed geometry permutations biased toward the degenerate cases, not uniform random placement — actually passes, because a puzzle game that's wrong 2% of the time at the edges isn't a smaller version of this app, it's a different, untrustworthy app wearing the same UI.
