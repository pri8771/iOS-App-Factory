**Codex (Design Lead · Skeptic) — Round 1**

Chapterly should be defined, very plainly, as a private reading journal for people who finish books and want to save quotes, reactions, and a few thoughtful exchanges with a tiny trusted circle, without ever entering a public feed. That one sentence needs to stay intact because it keeps the app from drifting into either “notes app with a share button” or “mini Goodreads.”

The primary user is not a casual reader and not a book-club organizer. It is an adult who reads consistently, finishes books with strong opinions, underlines lines they want to keep, and occasionally has one or two specific people they want to send that reaction to. The key scenario is: they finish a chapter or a book, capture a quote or thought immediately, add a short reflection and rating, and then either keep it private or package it into a circle-ready artifact to share over Messages. If that scenario is not fast and satisfying, the social layer will never matter.

The core loop should be narrow and disciplined: log a book, capture a quote manually or with OCR, attach page context plus a brief reflection, see that entry land in a personal timeline, then optionally share that finished entry into one private circle. The important UX decision is that “share” happens after a complete personal artifact exists. This should feel like sharing marginalia, not posting into a chat stream. The home screen should therefore center the reader’s own books, quotes, and progress first, with circle activity clearly secondary.

Hard scope boundaries need to be enforced aggressively. No public profile, no follower graph, no discovery, no algorithmic recommendations, no live threaded chat, no broad group-club management, no backend sync, no general-purpose note-taking sprawl, and no attempt to support every reading use case. One circle is enough for v1. One strong quote-capture flow is enough. Stats should stay personal and lightweight. Widgets and App Intents should support the journaling habit, not invent new product surface area. If OCR is unreliable, the app must degrade cleanly to manual entry instead of pretending camera capture is the hero feature.

The measurable success criteria for this phase should be practical. A developer should be able to describe the app in one sentence without mentioning a feed or community. A first-time user should be able to log a book, save a quote, add a reflection, and find that entry again without instruction. A user should be able to share one finished entry to one circle through Messages without creating an account or needing a server. The timeline and stats views should feel meaningful with a small amount of data, not only after months of use. Permission denials for camera or contacts must still leave the product usable. If those conditions are met, the MVP is scoped correctly.

The best direction is to treat the journal entry as the atomic unit of the product, not the message or the book. That gives the app emotional weight and keeps the interface editorial instead of chatty. The biggest risk in that direction is that the “social networking” value proposition becomes too weak if sharing is too tucked away or too static. So the final output must preserve one crucial detail: Chapterly is solo-first, but it is not solo-only. The app must make it natural to send a finished thought to a very small circle, while refusing every temptation to become a feed.

**Claude (QA & Risk · Pragmatist) — Round 1**

Let me give my independent read on this before any group debate.

The product promise: Chapterly is a private reading journal for solo capture and reflection, with an optional lightweight share-out to 2-6 trusted people via Messages — not a social feed, not a chat app.

Primary user and scenario: an adult who reads regularly and just finished a book (or a chapter) with a reaction they want to record — a quote, a rating, a note — and occasionally wants to forward that reaction to a couple of specific people who'd care, without starting a group chat thread that gets buried.

Core loop: log a book → capture a quote (typed or OCR'd from the page) → rate/reflect → optionally share that entry via Messages to your circle → see it land in your personal timeline/stats.

Now the QA/pragmatist part, which is where I actually want to spend my words, because this spec has several places where "sounds simple" and "is simple to build correctly" diverge.

First, the single riskiest technical claim in this whole spec is OCR quote capture, and it's being scoped as if it's a checkbox feature. Vision's text recognition is genuinely good on a flat, well-lit printed page, but real usage is: slightly curved paperback pages, glare from overhead lighting, italics, footnotes, dropped caps, two-column layouts in some books, and someone's thumb in frame. If the MVP doesn't explicitly define a "confirm and edit" step as a first-class, always-present UI state — never a silent auto-accept — this feature will produce wrong quotes and it will look like a bug rather than a known model limitation. The crop-confirm screen from the design handoff prompt is not a nice-to-have, it's the actual error-handling mechanism for this feature. I'd want the success criteria to explicitly say "OCR result is always user-editable before saving" rather than "OCR works."

Second, "one circle via Messages" is doing a lot of hand-waving about what state lives where. Since there is no server, once you share an entry via the share sheet, that data leaves your local store as a snapshot — a message, an image, a link — and there is no real "circle" data structure that syncs back. If we build an actual Circle model with member lists persisted locally, we need to be honest that this models "who I'd share with," not "who has seen what." A failure mode I'd flag: if the product spec implies replies flow back into the app (page-synced discussion), that requires either a share extension that can receive input or is simply not true in a local-only build. This phase should nail down, explicitly, that circle "membership" is a local contact list for repeat-sharing convenience, and the actual discussion happens in Messages itself, outside the app, at least for v1. If that's not spelled out now, someone in the design or build phase will quietly try to build a fake inbox for replies, which is scope creep into "feed/chat" territory — exactly the tripwire from the last phase.

Third, permissions. Camera (for OCR), Contacts (for inviting), and Photos (if importing a cover or picture) are three separate permission prompts, each with a deny path. A pragmatic MVP should treat "no camera access" as a normal, expected state — user can still add quotes manually — and "no contacts access" should degrade to manual phone number/email entry into the share sheet rather than blocking the invite workflow entirely. If either of these hard-blocks the workflow, that's a one-star review waiting to happen, not a hypothetical.

Fourth, on measurable success criteria — the last phase already gave good directional metrics (solo retention at 4 weeks, invite rate), but those are business metrics for after ship, not something this build can measure in one pass. For this phase, the developer-facing success criteria need to be testable right now: app launches to a non-empty-feeling home screen using seeded/sample data or an honest empty state; a book can be added, a quote captured both manually and via OCR, a rating/reflection saved, all surviving a force-quit and relaunch; a share sheet invocation actually opens with real content (not a placeholder string) for at least one contact; widget shows real currently-reading data, not a static mock; and an App Intent can log a quote without opening the app fully. Those are the checks I'd hold the build to — behavioral, not aspirational.

Fifth, scope boundary I want explicit: no attempt at multi-device sync, no CloudKit, no real inbox for incoming replies, no discussion-prompt LLM feature unless it's trivially low-risk (a static or templated prompt generator counts as satisfying that clause; an on-device model that has to be downloaded/managed does not, and I'd push back hard on that in build phase given time constraints).

The single detail I most want the final phase output to preserve: OCR and sharing are both leave-and-return boundaries, not seamless pipes — OCR requires user confirmation before anything is saved, and sharing requires the user to actually invoke Messages and there's no return path for replies into the app. If either of those two facts gets fuzzy in translation to design or architecture, the app will overpromise "circles" as a live discussion feature it structurally cannot deliver without a server, which was explicitly ruled out for v1.

**Product promise:** Chapterly is a private reading journal — log a book, capture a quote (typed or OCR'd), rate and reflect — where a finished journal entry can optionally be shared, as a complete artifact, to one small trusted circle (2-6 people) via Messages. - No CloudKit/multi-device sync, no on-device LLM unless it's a trivial templated/static prompt generator (no model download/management).

**Measurable success criteria for this phase:**
- One sentence describes the app with no mention of "feed" or "community."
- A first-time user can log a book, capture a quote, add a reflection, and find it again with no instructions.
- A finished entry can be shared to one circle via Messages with real content (not placeholder text), no account or server needed.
- Data (books, quotes, reflections, ratings) survives force-quit and relaunch.
- Denied camera or contacts permission still leaves the app fully usable via manual entry.
- Timeline/stats feel meaningful even with just a few entries, not only after months.
